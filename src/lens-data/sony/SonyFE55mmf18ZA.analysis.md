# Sony Sonnar T* FE 55mm F1.8 ZA — Optical Design Analysis

## Patent Reference and Design Identification

**Patent:** US 2015/0092100 A1, "Imaging Lens and Imaging Apparatus"
**Applicant:** Sony Corporation, Tokyo, Japan
**Inventors:** Yonghua Chen (Tokyo), Naoki Miyagawa (Kanagawa)
**Filed:** August 21, 2014 (US); **Priority:** September 27, 2013 (JP 2013-201345)
**Published:** April 2, 2015
**Embodiment used:** Example 1 (Numerical Example 1, Tables 1–3; Figs. 1, 12, 13)

The identification of Example 1 as the production Sony Sonnar T* FE 55mm F1.8 ZA (SEL55F18Z) rests on the following convergent evidence:

1. **Element and group count.** The patent prescription contains 7 optical elements in 5 air-separated groups, matching Sony's published specification exactly.
2. **Aspherical element count and topology.** The prescription places aspherical surfaces on 3 elements — L14 (single-sided, rear), L21 (double-sided), and L31 (double-sided) — totaling 5 aspherical surfaces on 3 elements, two of which are double-sided. Sony's marketing language describes the design as having "three aspherical elements, two double-sided."
3. **Focal length.** The computed EFL of the prescription is 53.61 mm. Production lenses marketed as "55 mm" commonly have design focal lengths in the 52–55 mm range; the deviation is within standard industry rounding.
4. **Maximum aperture.** The patent states F/1.86 for Example 1. The production lens is marketed as f/1.8; the ~3% difference reflects the standard practice of rounding to the nearest standard f-number.
5. **Image circle.** The aberration diagrams show Y = 21.63 mm, yielding an image circle diameter of 43.26 mm — full-frame 35 mm format (43.27 mm diagonal).
6. **Half angle of view.** The patent states ω = 22.15°, giving 2ω = 44.3°. Sony publishes 43°; the slight difference reflects vignetting-limited vs. paraxial-traced field.
7. **Inner focus mechanism.** The second lens group (G2) travels along the axis during focusing, consistent with Sony's specification of a linear-motor inner-focus system.
8. **Filing chronology.** The Japanese priority date of September 27, 2013 precedes the lens's announcement by fewer than three weeks (the α7 system and SEL55F18Z were announced on October 16, 2013), consistent with a design patent filed at or near the end of the product development cycle.
9. **Mount compatibility.** The patent describes the design as suitable for mirrorless cameras with short flange distances; the E-mount flange distance of 18 mm is compatible with the back focal distance of approximately 14.8 mm (from the last lens surface to the image plane, including the cover glass thickness).

## Optical Architecture

The Sonnar T* FE 55mm F1.8 ZA is a three-group inner-focus design with a positive–negative–positive power distribution. The overall architecture departs from the classical symmetric Gauss type; the patent text (¶0060) explicitly notes that the symmetrical positive–negative–negative–positive arrangement of a Gauss-type lens produces a large air lens in the middle of the first group, preventing reduction in on-axial thickness. Instead, the first group adopts a compact arrangement of two cemented doublets that slightly diverge then converge the incoming beam, enabling both spherical aberration correction and a short G1 track length.

The three groups and their roles are:

**G1 (positive, f = +42.8 mm).** Four elements in two cemented doublets. Collects the incoming beam, provides the bulk of the system's converging power, and corrects on-axis spherical aberration and axial chromatic aberration through its internal negative–positive pairings.

**G2 (negative, f = −49.3 mm).** A single negative meniscus lens (L21) that serves as the focus group. Its low weight (enabled by low-index glass and thin center thickness) and modest power produce small focus travel (1.26 mm for the infinity-to-β = −0.033 range) and low eccentric sensitivity, enabling high-speed linear-motor autofocus with minimal aberration variation during focusing.

**G3 (positive, f = +73.3 mm).** Two elements — a positive biconvex front sub-group (GF, f = +38.9 mm) and a negative meniscus rear sub-group (GR, f = −49.3 mm). The positive front sub-group converges the beam toward the image, while the negative rear sub-group provides a cancelling function for on-axis aberration generated by GF and, critically, increases the incidence height of off-axis rays to enable effective correction of distortion and field curvature (¶0065).

The aperture stop (STO) is located between G1 and G2, in the air gap immediately following the second cemented doublet. This placement is significant: the stop is forward of the focus group, so the chief ray geometry at G3 is relatively stable during focus travel, aiding off-axis aberration control.

The overall total track length is 79.35 mm (from front surface to image), yielding a telephoto ratio of TL/f = 79.35/53.61 = 1.48 — slightly longer than unity, reflecting the non-telephoto, non-retrofocus character of the design.

## Element-by-Element Analysis

### L11 — Biconcave Negative (cemented with L12)

nd = 1.58144, νd = 40.9. Glass: S-TIM2 (OHARA) — light flint. f = −36.6 mm.

L11 is the first element of the front cemented doublet. Its biconcave form generates strong negative power, causing the incoming collimated beam to diverge slightly. This divergence serves two purposes: it reduces the ray height on the subsequent positive elements (moderating their contribution to spherical aberration) and establishes a chromatic under-correction that L12 partially compensates. S-TIM2 is a conventional light flint with moderate dispersion (νd = 40.9), making it an effective chromatic counterpart to the higher-Abbe lanthanum crown of L12. The element's center thickness of 3.78 mm and its relatively modest index (nd = 1.58) keep its weight low.

### L12 — Biconvex Positive (cemented with L11)

nd = 1.72916, νd = 54.7. Glass: S-LAL18 (OHARA) — lanthanum crown. f = +33.8 mm.

L12 is cemented to L11, forming the first doublet of G1. Its biconvex shape and lanthanum-crown glass (high index, moderate-high Abbe number) provide strong positive power with controlled spherical aberration contribution. The cement interface at R = +40.397 mm bends the chromatic under-correction introduced by L11 into a near-achromatic state for the doublet as a whole. S-LAL18 is a workhorse lanthanum crown widely used in high-performance imaging systems; its nd = 1.729 and νd = 54.7 place it in the LaK class, offering high refractive power per unit curvature. The 7.54 mm center thickness — the largest in G1 — reflects the biconvex shape's large sag differential at the 49 mm filter-thread–constrained aperture.

### L13 — Negative Meniscus, Convex to Object (cemented with L14)

nd = 1.846663, νd = 23.8. Glass: S-TIH53 (OHARA) — dense flint. f = −119.9 mm.

L13 is the first element of the second cemented doublet. Its negative meniscus form (R1 = +32.361, R2 = +23.982 — both radii positive, forming a crescent convex toward the object) generates moderate negative power. The extremely high index (nd = 1.847) and very low Abbe number (νd = 23.8) identify S-TIH53 as a dense flint in the SF/TiH class. Its role in the design is threefold: it provides chromatic correction (its high dispersion counters the low-dispersion positive elements), it contributes negative Petzval curvature to flatten the field, and its high index keeps the surface curvatures gentle relative to the power delivered, which moderates higher-order aberration contributions. The thin center thickness (1.60 mm) reflects the meniscus form's inherently small center-to-edge sag differential.

### L14 — Positive Meniscus, Convex to Object (cemented with L13; 1× aspherical)

nd = 1.768015, νd = 49.2. Glass: M-TAF101 (HOYA) — moldable lanthanum flint. f = +37.6 mm.

L14 completes the second doublet and is the last element in G1 before the aperture stop. Its positive meniscus form (R1 = +23.982, R2 = +127.461) delivers the dominant converging power of the rear doublet. The rear surface (surface 6) is aspherical — the only aspherical surface in G1 — where its departure from spherical form (approximately +44 µm at h = 10 mm, with positive A4) flattens the peripheral zone of the wavefront, correcting residual spherical aberration and coma from the first doublet. The glass (nd = 1.768, νd = 49.2, code 768/492) matches HOYA M-TAF101 (nd = 1.76802, νd = 49.24) to within Δnd < 0.0001, placing it in the lanthanum flint (La-F / TAF) family. The "M-" prefix in HOYA's nomenclature designates a glass grade optimized for precision glass molding — consistent with the aspherical rear surface. L14 pairs with the S-TIH53 flint of L13 to form a chromatic doublet. The cement interface at R = +23.982 mm is the most strongly curved junction in the system.

### L21 — Negative Meniscus, Convex to Object (2× aspherical) — FOCUS GROUP

nd = 1.487489, νd = 70.4. Glass: S-FSL5 (OHARA) / N-FK5 (Schott) / FC5 (HOYA) — fluorocrown. f = −49.3 mm.

L21 constitutes the entirety of G2, the inner-focus group. Its negative meniscus form (R1 = +229.883, R2 = +21.728 — strongly concave toward the image) generates the group's negative power. Both surfaces are aspherical; the front surface (surface 8) has A4 = −1.53 × 10⁻⁵ (negative, steepening the periphery), while the rear surface (surface 9) has A4 = −1.67 × 10⁻⁵ (also negative). Together these aspherical departures control the variation in spherical aberration and coma during focus travel, which is the central challenge of any inner-focus design.

The glass choice — S-FSL5 / N-FK5, a fluorocrown with nd = 1.487 and νd = 70.4 — is significant for three reasons. First, its low index (the lowest in the system) means modest surface curvatures relative to the element's power, reducing higher-order aberration contributions. Second, its high Abbe number (low dispersion) suppresses the chromatic variation that would otherwise accompany the group's axial motion during focusing. The patent text (¶0062) explicitly recommends "a single lens made of low-dispersion glass" for the focus group when the goal is to minimize aberration variation. Third, fluorocrown glasses have relatively low density compared to the lanthanum and dense flint glasses used elsewhere in the system, directly benefiting the linear-motor drive's speed and power consumption.

During focusing from infinity to the close-focus state at β = −0.033, L21 travels 1.26 mm toward the image. The air gap in front of it (D7, the stop-to-L21 spacing) increases from 2.81 mm to 4.07 mm, and the air gap behind it (D9, the L21-to-L31 spacing) decreases from 13.67 mm to 12.41 mm. The sum D7 + D9 = 16.48 mm is conserved exactly, confirming that only G2 moves during focus — the classic inner-focus constraint where the front and rear groups remain fixed.

### L31 — Biconvex Positive (2× aspherical)

nd = 1.592014, νd = 67.0. Glass: Fluorocrown / phosphate crown, likely PGM-class moldable glass (no exact catalog match; nearest: HOYA FCD10, nd = 1.59349, νd = 67.0, Δnd = 0.0015). f = +38.9 mm.

L31 is the front sub-group (GF) of G3 and the most strongly powered positive element in the rear half of the system. Its biconvex form (R1 = +43.205, R2 = −44.134 — nearly symmetric) produces strong convergence, and its 12.00 mm center thickness — the thickest element in the prescription — reflects the deep biconvex sag at the element's clear aperture. Both surfaces are aspherical: the front surface (surface 10) has positive A4 = +4.59 × 10⁻⁶ (flattening the periphery to reduce spherical aberration), while the rear surface (surface 11) has negative A4 = −3.28 × 10⁻⁶ (steepening the periphery to control coma and astigmatism in the off-axis beam).

The glass identification is uncertain. The patent's nd = 1.592014 does not match any major catalog entry exactly; the nearest candidates are HOYA FCD10 and CDGM H-FK95N (both nd = 1.59349, νd = 67.0), with a residual Δnd of 0.0015. The Abbe number νd = 67.0 matches these glasses precisely, placing L31 firmly in the fluorocrown / phosphate crown family (FK/FCD class). The discrepancy in nd may indicate a proprietary melt optimized for precision glass molding (PGM) — an inference supported by the fact that both surfaces are aspherical, which in production-scale manufacturing strongly suggests PGM fabrication. Alternatively, the patent may intentionally offset the index from the production glass to protect the exact formulation while preserving the design's paraxial behavior.

Whatever the exact glass, L31's role is clear: it is the primary positive relay element in G3, converging the diverging beam from G2 toward the image while its aspherical surfaces handle the bulk of the system's higher-order aberration correction in the rear group.

### L32 — Negative Meniscus, Concave to Object

nd = 1.69895, νd = 30.1. Glass: S-TIM35 (OHARA) — medium flint. f = −49.3 mm.

L32 is the rear sub-group (GR) of G3 and the final optical element before the cover glass. Its negative meniscus form (R1 = −18.057, R2 = −39.016 — concave toward the object, with the front surface more strongly curved) generates negative power that partially cancels G3's overall positive power, yielding the group's net focal length of +73.3 mm. The patent (¶0065) explains that this rear negative sub-group serves two functions: it retains "a function of canceling the on-axial aberration in the front sub-lens group having positive refractive power," and it increases "an incidence height of off-axial light rays," making the system "advantageous in securing a function of cancelling off-axial aberration (in particular, distortion)."

S-TIM35 is a medium flint with νd = 30.1 — the second-highest dispersion in the system after S-TIH53 (νd = 23.8). Its high dispersion relative to the fluorocrown L31 (νd = 67.0) provides chromatic correction across the G3 sub-system, analogous to the flint–crown pairing in G1. The element's position — well behind the stop, at the periphery of the system — means it intercepts off-axis beams at high incidence heights, amplifying its influence on distortion, lateral chromatic aberration, and field curvature. Despite being only 1.20 mm thick at center, L32 carries significant corrective weight in the system's aberration budget.

The glass palette reveals a consistent chromatic strategy. Each doublet or pseudo-doublet pair uses a high-dispersion flint opposite a low-dispersion positive element: L11 (νd = 40.9) vs. L12 (νd = 54.7) in the first doublet; L13 (νd = 23.8) vs. L14 (νd = 49.2) in the second doublet; L31 (νd = 67.0) vs. L32 (νd = 30.1) in the G3 sub-system. The focus element L21 uses a fluorocrown (νd = 70.4) — the lowest-dispersion glass in the system — to minimize chromatic focus shift during inner-focus travel.

The presence of both OHARA and HOYA glasses in the same design is not unusual for Sony/Zeiss designs, which commonly source from multiple vendors. The five OHARA-matched glasses (L11, L12, L13, L21, L32) and one HOYA match (L14, M-TAF101 — a moldable grade) represent standard catalog entries; the remaining glass (L31) is likely a proprietary or specialty moldable glass optimized for precision glass molding of double-sided aspherics.

## Focus Mechanism

The lens employs an inner-focus system in which only G2 (the single element L21) moves axially during focusing. G1, the aperture stop, and G3 remain fixed.

| Parameter | Infinity focus | β = −0.033 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| D7 (STO → L21 front) | 2.81 mm | 4.07 mm | +1.26 mm |
| D9 (L21 rear → L31 front) | 13.67 mm | 12.41 mm | −1.26 mm |
| **Sum (D7 + D9)** | **16.48 mm** | **16.48 mm** | **0.00 mm** |

The gap sum is exactly conserved, confirming that L21 translates rigidly without any accompanying compensator motion. This single-element focus group is the lightest possible configuration for an inner-focus system: L21's fluorocrown glass (S-FSL5, density ≈ 2.46 g/cm³ — considerably lighter than the lanthanum and dense flint glasses used in G1 and G3) and thin center thickness (1.00 mm) minimize the mass that the linear autofocus motor must accelerate, directly enabling the fast, silent AF performance that Sony specifies for video-capable E-mount bodies.

The short-distance focus state at β = −0.033 corresponds to an object distance of approximately 1.7 m from the sensor plane — well beyond the marketed MFD of 0.5 m. The patent tabulates only this representative near-focus state; the full close-focus position (MFD = 0.5 m, yielding approximately 0.14× magnification per Sony's specification) would require further travel of L21 beyond the 1.26 mm tabulated for β = −0.033.

The close-focus gaps at MFD = 0.5 m (object distance ≈ 421 mm from the front surface) have been extrapolated via paraxial inner-focus solve, enforcing the 16.48 mm gap sum conservation constraint. The computed values are D7 ≈ 8.08 mm, D9 ≈ 8.40 mm, corresponding to a total focus stroke of approximately 5.27 mm. These extrapolated values are used in the companion data file's variable-gap array.

The patent (¶0061) notes that a smaller focus travel is desirable to reduce total length, and that the refractive power of G2 should be appropriately set to suppress aberration variation. Conditional expression (3), which constrains |f2/f| to the range 0.60–5.00, governs this trade-off: a shorter focal length for G2 (stronger power) reduces the required travel but increases eccentric sensitivity and aberration variation, while a longer focal length requires more travel but produces gentler aberration changes. Example 1's value of |f2/f| = 0.92 sits near the lower end of this range, indicating a relatively strong focus group — consistent with the design goal of a compact total track (TL/f = 1.48).

## Aspherical Surfaces

Five aspherical surfaces appear in the design, distributed across three elements. All use the standard even-polynomial sag equation:

$$Z(h) = \frac{h^2/R}{1 + \sqrt{1 - (1+K)(h/R)^2}} + A_4 h^4 + A_6 h^6 + A_8 h^8 + A_{10} h^{10}$$

All five surfaces have conic constant K = 0 (spherical base curve), meaning the aspherical departure is carried entirely by the polynomial terms.

### Surface 6 (rear of L14, G1 exit surface)

| Coefficient | Value |
|---|---|
| K | 0 |
| A4 | +4.72026 × 10⁻⁶ |
| A6 | −6.13511 × 10⁻⁹ |
| A8 | +3.19538 × 10⁻¹¹ |
| A10 | −5.08717 × 10⁻¹⁴ |

The positive A4 on a weakly curved converging surface (R = +127.5 mm) flattens the peripheral zone, reducing the marginal ray's angle of convergence relative to a spherical surface. At h = 10 mm, the aspherical departure is approximately +44 µm. This surface's primary role is fine correction of residual spherical aberration from G1 before the beam reaches the aperture stop.

### Surfaces 8 and 9 (both surfaces of L21, the focus group)

| Coefficient | Surface 8 (front) | Surface 9 (rear) |
|---|---|---|
| K | 0 | 0 |
| A4 | −1.53071 × 10⁻⁵ | −1.66843 × 10⁻⁵ |
| A6 | +5.95902 × 10⁻⁸ | +5.20407 × 10⁻⁸ |
| A8 | −1.03151 × 10⁻¹⁰ | +3.77076 × 10⁻¹² |
| A10 | −1.18635 × 10⁻¹³ | −6.66338 × 10⁻¹³ |

Both surfaces have strongly negative A4 coefficients, steepening the peripheral zone. The front surface's departure is approximately −105 µm at h = 10 mm; the rear surface's is approximately −121 µm. These departures are the largest in the system, reflecting the focus group's critical role: as L21 translates, the aspherical profiles must maintain stable correction of spherical aberration and coma across the full focus range. The nearly matched magnitudes of the two A4 coefficients (−1.53 × 10⁻⁵ vs. −1.67 × 10⁻⁵) suggest a balanced aspherical strategy where both surfaces share the correction load, reducing the sensitivity to manufacturing tilt or decenter of the element.

The use of double-sided aspherics on the focus element suggests glass molding fabrication. S-FSL5 / N-FK5 belongs to the fluorocrown family, which generally has a lower transformation temperature than dense flint or lanthanum glasses, making it compatible with precision glass molding processes — though OHARA's "S-" prefix denotes an eco-safe (lead/arsenic-free) standard grade rather than the "L-" prefix grades explicitly designated for low-temperature molding.

### Surfaces 10 and 11 (both surfaces of L31, G3 front sub-group)

| Coefficient | Surface 10 (front) | Surface 11 (rear) |
|---|---|---|
| K | 0 | 0 |
| A4 | +4.58950 × 10⁻⁶ | −3.28037 × 10⁻⁶ |
| A6 | −1.29121 × 10⁻⁸ | −2.72677 × 10⁻⁸ |

The front surface has positive A4 (flattening the periphery, +34 µm at h = 10 mm), while the rear surface has negative A4 (steepening the periphery, −57 µm at h = 10 mm). This sign reversal across the element is characteristic of aspherical correction for both on-axis (spherical aberration) and off-axis (coma, astigmatism) simultaneously: the front surface handles the spherical aberration of the converging on-axis beam, while the rear surface adjusts the off-axis beam geometry to control field curvature and astigmatism. The relatively large A6 contributions (both negative) indicate significant higher-order correction, which is expected given L31's large clear aperture and thick center section (12.00 mm).

As with L21, the double-sided aspherical surfaces on L31 imply PGM fabrication. The glass (nd = 1.592, νd = 67.0) is consistent with a moldable fluorocrown or phosphate crown grade, though no exact catalog match was found (the nearest being HOYA FCD10 with Δnd = 0.0015).

## Aberration Correction Strategy

The Petzval sum of the system is +0.00156 mm⁻¹, yielding a Petzval radius of 643 mm — approximately 12.0× the focal length. This is a well-flattened field for a large-aperture normal prime; by comparison, a simple positive singlet of the same focal length in typical glass (nd ≈ 1.5) would have a Petzval sum roughly 8× larger. The field flattening is achieved through the distribution of negative power across L11, L13, L21, and L32, whose negative Petzval contributions partially cancel the positive contributions from L12, L14, and L31.

The design's chromatic correction relies on the three flint–crown pairings described in the Glass Selection section. No glass in the design reaches the ED (extra-low dispersion) class as typically marketed by lens manufacturers (νd > 80, e.g., S-FPL51 or FCD1); the highest Abbe number in the system is νd = 70.4 (L21, S-FSL5), placing it in the low-dispersion category but short of the ED designation. The chromatic strategy is therefore classical achromatic rather than apochromatic, consistent with the aberration plots (Figs. 12–13) which show modest residual longitudinal chromatic aberration at full aperture.

The five aspherical surfaces carry the bulk of the monochromatic higher-order correction. Their distribution — one surface on the G1 exit, two surfaces on the focus group, and two surfaces on the G3 relay — places corrective authority at three distinct positions along the optical axis, enabling independent control of spherical aberration (primarily surfaces 6 and 10), coma (surfaces 8, 9, 11), and astigmatism/field curvature (surfaces 10, 11). The Sonnar heritage of the design is evident in the low element count and the resulting reduction in air–glass interfaces (only 10 air–glass transitions vs. 14–20 in more complex double-Gauss or retrofocus designs at comparable aperture), which suppresses ghosting and flare — a property that Sony and Zeiss emphasize in the product's marketing alongside the T* anti-reflective coating.
