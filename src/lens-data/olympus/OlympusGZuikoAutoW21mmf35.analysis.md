# Olympus G.Zuiko Auto-W 21mm f/3.5 — Optical Analysis

## Patent Reference and Design Identification

**Patent:** US 3,884,556  
**Filed:** May 4, 1973  
**Priority:** May 10, 1972 (JP 47-46084) and September 6, 1972 (JP 47-89313)  
**Granted:** May 20, 1975  
**Inventor:** Jihei Nakagawa  
**Assignee:** Olympus Optical Co., Ltd., Tokyo, Japan  
**Title:** Retrofocus Wide-Angle Lens System  
**Embodiment analyzed:** Embodiment 3 / Claim 4

US 3,884,556 discloses a compact retrofocus wide-angle family for single-lens-reflex cameras. The patent states the design goal as an f/3.5 to f/2.8 lens with an 84° to 92° field angle, fewer lenses than then-conventional retrofocus designs, reduced overall length, and smaller front-lens diameter. Embodiment 3 is the relevant seven-element f/3.5 prescription.

The identification with the Olympus G.Zuiko Auto-W 21mm f/3.5 rests on convergent prescription and production evidence:

1. Embodiment 3 is a seven-element, seven-group, all-air-spaced spherical design. The Olympus `G.Zuiko` designation denotes seven glass elements in Olympus's period naming convention, and published OM-System specifications list the 21mm f/3.5 as 7 elements in 7 groups.
2. The patent gives F/3.5 for Embodiment 3, matching the production maximum aperture.
3. The patent prescription is normalized to $f = 1.0$. Scaling by 21 gives a computed production focal length of 21.0005 mm.
4. The patent aberration plots for Embodiment 3 are labeled at a 46° half field. A 21 mm rectilinear lens covering the 36 × 24 mm format gives a 45.85° diagonal half field, consistent with the published 92° angle of view.
5. The computed infinity back focal distance is 1.7333 in normalized units, or 36.40 mm at 21 mm scale. This is the expected long back-focus behavior for an OM-mount SLR retrofocus wide-angle lens.
6. The patent gives only one fixed prescription for Embodiment 3 and no close-focus floating spacings, consistent with a unit-focus f/3.5 wide-angle rather than a floating close-range design.

Embodiments 1 and 2 are six-element f/3.5 examples. Embodiments 4 through 8 are faster f/2.8 examples with cemented components or additional elements. Embodiment 3 is therefore the only example in this patent that simultaneously matches the seven-element f/3.5 production lens.

## Optical Architecture

The lens is a seven-element, seven-group retrofocus wide-angle with the power distribution positive-negative-negative-positive-negative-positive-positive. It divides naturally into a diverging front group, L1-L3, and a converging rear group, L4-L7, with the stop between L4 and L5.

The front group is the retrofocus engine. L1 is a positive meniscus, followed by two strong negative menisci, L2 and L3. The combined L1-L3 focal length is $f_{123} = -0.6483f$, or -13.61 mm at 21 mm scale. This negative front subsystem shifts the rear principal plane forward and produces the long 36.40 mm back focal distance.

The rear group supplies most of the positive imaging power and correction. L4 is a thick biconvex element and is the strongest standalone positive element in the lens. L5 is a dense-flint biconcave negative element behind the stop. L6 and L7 are positive rear menisci with their concave surfaces facing the object side. The patent explains this general architecture in functional terms: the positive first lens helps correct the negative distortion typical of ultra-wide retrofocus designs; the two negative menisci reduce the principal-ray angle and front diameter; the thick fourth component corrects spherical aberration and astigmatism generated by the negative front lenses; and the low-Abbe fifth component assists longitudinal and lateral chromatic correction.

The data file inserts `STO` halfway through the L4-L5 air space because the patent drawing shows the stop in that gap but does not tabulate an exact stop coordinate. This stop placement does not affect the Gaussian focal length or back focal distance. The stop semi-diameter is set to 4.582 mm so that the paraxial entrance pupil radius is 3.000 mm, corresponding to f/3.5 at 21 mm.

## Element-by-Element Analysis

### L1 — Positive Meniscus, convex to object

nd = 1.72000, νd = 42.1. Glass: unmatched vintage lanthanum flint, code 720/421; LaF2-class. f = +90.8 mm.

L1 is a weak positive meniscus with both radii positive. The front surface is more strongly curved than the rear surface, so the element contributes positive power while preserving a compact convex-front profile. Its standalone focal length is long compared with the system focal length, but its location at the front makes its ray-angle effect important.

The patent assigns the first positive meniscus a distortion-correction role: it increases the principal-ray angle after the first component so that positive distortion from the front element offsets the large negative distortion otherwise produced by a compact retrofocus wide-angle. The 720/421 glass is a high-index, moderate-dispersion lanthanum-flint type. No current public OHARA, HOYA, HIKARI, or SCHOTT catalog entry was found that matches both nd = 1.7200 and νd = 42.1 closely enough for a confident modern catalog name, so the data file labels this element as unmatched rather than forcing a circular glass identification.

### L2 — Negative Meniscus, convex to object

nd = 1.61800, νd = 63.4. Glass: S-PHM52 (OHARA, current equivalent) / PCD4-class phosphate crown. f = -26.2 mm.

L2 is the first strong negative meniscus in the front group. Its rear surface has a much shorter radius than its front surface, so the element has strong negative power even though both surfaces are convex toward the object side.

The high Abbe number is significant. L2 contributes necessary diverging power without adding excessive axial color. Its glass is best treated as a phosphate-crown class equivalent: modern OHARA S-PHM52 is nd = 1.61800 with νd ≈ 63.3, matching the patent closely within normal catalog rounding.

### L3 — Negative Meniscus, convex to object

nd = 1.71300, νd = 54.0. Glass: S-LAL8 (OHARA) / N-LAK8 class lanthanum crown. f = -21.3 mm.

L3 is the stronger of the two negative front menisci. Together with L2 it completes the compact diverging front subsystem. The patent states that the second and third negative menisci reduce the principal-ray angle and the front lens diameter while supporting off-axis correction across the wide field.

The nd/νd pair matches the LAK8/S-LAL8 class. OHARA S-LAL8 is a close modern catalog match at nd = 1.71300 and νd ≈ 53.9; SCHOTT N-LAK8 is similar but not identical. Because this is a 1970s Japanese Olympus design, the OHARA equivalent is the least speculative catalog label.

### L4 — Biconvex Positive, thick

nd = 1.59550, νd = 39.2. Glass: S-TIM8 (OHARA) / F8-class light flint. f = +17.5 mm.

L4 is the thick central positive element. Its center thickness is 0.6153f, or 12.92 mm after scaling, making it by far the thickest element in the prescription. Both surfaces contribute positive power.

The patent describes the fourth component as the corrective positive lens that balances spherical aberration and astigmatism caused by the two strong front negative menisci. Its moderately low Abbe number is deliberate: it participates in the lens's chromatic balance rather than serving merely as a high-Abbe converging crown. Modern OHARA S-TIM8, nd = 1.59551 and νd ≈ 39.24, is an excellent match to the patent's rounded nd/νd values.

### L5 — Biconcave Negative

nd = 1.84670, νd = 23.9. Glass: SF57 (SCHOTT, legacy dense-flint class). f = -21.1 mm.

L5 is a high-index, very low-Abbe biconcave negative element immediately behind the stop. It is the principal chromatic counterweight in the design. The patent specifically notes that selecting a material with a small Abbe number for the fifth component is effective for correcting both longitudinal and lateral chromatic aberration in cooperation with the first and fourth components.

The nd/νd pair corresponds closely to legacy SCHOTT SF57, nd = 1.84666 and νd ≈ 23.83. Modern N-SF57 is close in index but has slightly different Abbe value; the historical SF57 class is the more direct match to the patent's rounded 1.8467 / 23.9 data.

### L6 — Positive Meniscus, concave to object

nd = 1.61800, νd = 63.4. Glass: S-PHM52 (OHARA, current equivalent) / PCD4-class phosphate crown. f = +32.9 mm.

L6 is a rear positive meniscus whose concave side faces the object. The patent identifies this rear positive meniscus form as effective for correcting spherical aberration and coma. Its low-dispersion crown glass complements the dense-flint L5 directly ahead of it.

In Embodiment 3 the rear positive component is split into L6 and L7 rather than being a single rear lens. This adds correction freedom while retaining seven separate air-spaced singlets.

### L7 — Positive Meniscus, concave to object

nd = 1.61800, νd = 63.4. Glass: S-PHM52 (OHARA, current equivalent) / PCD4-class phosphate crown. f = +36.2 mm.

L7 is the final positive meniscus. Its very weak front surface and stronger concave rear surface give it positive power while helping shape the exit pupil and field behavior of the rear group.

The patent notes that the sixth positive component can be divided into two positive lenses, and that both are preferably positive menisci with their concave surfaces toward the object when the aperture ratio remains moderate. Embodiment 3 follows that form exactly.

| L1 | 1.72000 | 42.1 | Unmatched vintage lanthanum flint, code 720/421 | High-index front meniscus for ray-angle and distortion control |
| L2 | 1.61800 | 63.4 | S-PHM52 (OHARA) current equivalent / PCD4-class phosphate crown | Low-dispersion negative front-group power |
| L3 | 1.71300 | 54.0 | S-LAL8 (OHARA) / N-LAK8 class lanthanum crown | High-index negative front-group power |
| L4 | 1.59550 | 39.2 | S-TIM8 (OHARA) / F8-class light flint | Thick positive power and aberration balancing |
| L5 | 1.84670 | 23.9 | SF57 (SCHOTT legacy dense flint class) | Primary chromatic correction behind the stop |
| L6 | 1.61800 | 63.4 | S-PHM52 (OHARA) current equivalent / PCD4-class phosphate crown | Rear positive meniscus correction |

The glass selection is conventional but efficient. Three elements use the same high-Abbe 1.618 / 63.4 phosphate-crown class, while the strongest chromatic lever is the dense-flint L5. L4's νd = 39.2 is also flint territory, not crown; the appropriate catalog-class description is light flint or titanium flint. This L4-L5 Abbe separation across the stop is the main chromatic correction mechanism in an otherwise all-spherical, all-singlet design.

No ED, fluorite, anomalous partial-dispersion, or aspherical elements are present. The prescription supports an achromatized wide-angle design, not an apochromatic one.

## Focus Mechanism

The patent provides only an infinity prescription and no variable internal spacings. The production lens is therefore modeled as unit focus: the entire optical assembly moves while all internal element spacings remain fixed.

The data file models close focus by changing only the final back-focus gap:

| Focus state | BFD | Notes |
|---|---:|---|
| Infinity | 36.399 mm | Computed paraxial BFD from the rounded prescription; patent lists 36.404 mm after scaling |
| 0.20 m | 39.835 mm | Finite-conjugate paraxial solution for a 0.20 m object distance measured from the image plane |

The close-focus solution gives a paraxial lateral magnification of about 0.164×, or approximately 1:6.1. Published OM-System close-focus field data of roughly 21 × 14 cm implies about 0.171× on the 36 × 24 mm frame, so the first-order unit-focus model is consistent with the production specification to the expected accuracy for a patent-normalized paraxial reconstruction.

## Conditional Expressions

The patent states two general design conditions:

$$0.4f < |f_{123}| < 0.8f, \quad f_{123} < 0$$

$$0.5f < D_4 + D_5 < f$$

Here $f_{123}$ is the combined focal length of L1-L3, $D_4$ is the axial thickness of the fourth component, and $D_5$ is the axial thickness of the fifth component.

For Embodiment 3, the independent paraxial trace gives:

| Condition | Computed value | Result |
|---|---:|---|
| $|f_{123}|$ | 0.6483f | Satisfies 0.4f to 0.8f |
| $D_4 + D_5$ | 0.8317f | Satisfies 0.5f to 1.0f |

Claim 10 gives tighter seven-element ranges:

$$0.64f < |f_{123}| < 0.72f, \quad f_{123} < 0$$

$$0.65f < D_4 + D_5 < 0.84f$$

Embodiment 3 also satisfies the tighter claim limits: $|f_{123}| = 0.6483f$ and $D_4 + D_5 = 0.8317f$.

## Design Heritage and Context

The G.Zuiko Auto-W 21mm f/3.5 belongs to the first generation of Olympus OM compact wide-angle lenses. Its patent is characteristic of the early OM design program: all-spherical, mechanically compact, and optically economical. The design uses seven singlets rather than cemented groups, relying on glass selection and air-space curvature rather than special materials or aspheres.

The result is not a symmetric wide-angle lens adapted to an SLR mount, but a true retrofocus design. The long BFD/EFL ratio is approximately 1.73, well above unity, and the front negative subsystem is the defining optical mechanism. The appropriate classification is therefore retrofocus, not merely a short-focal-length wide-angle.
