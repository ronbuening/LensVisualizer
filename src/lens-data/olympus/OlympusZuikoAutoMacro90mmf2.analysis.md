# Olympus Zuiko Auto-Macro 90mm f/2

## US 4,792,219 — Embodiment 3 (Shin-ichi Mihara / Olympus Optical Co., Ltd.)

### Patent overview

US Patent 4,792,219 was granted on December 20, 1988, from a Japanese priority filing dated July 18, 1985 (JP 60-157052). The inventor, Shin-ichi Mihara, was one of Olympus's most prolific optical designers during the 1980s and early 1990s, responsible for a broad range of designs spanning catadioptric telephotos, compact camera lenses, and video zoom systems. This particular patent covers the optical design of the Zuiko Auto-Macro 90mm f/2, a lens that held the distinction of being the first macro lens in the 90–105 mm focal length class to offer an f/2 maximum aperture. It was introduced in 1986 as part of the Olympus OM system and remained in production until 2003.

The patent presents three numerical embodiments, all sharing the same general optical layout and all specified at a normalized focal length of f = 100 mm with a design f-number of F/2.06 (the production lens is marketed as f/2, the standard nominal rounding). Embodiment 3 is identified as the production design based on two converging lines of evidence: the computed focusing helicoid travel, when scaled to the 90 mm production focal length, matches the Olympus-specified 47 mm helicoid range to within a fraction of a millimeter, and the Embodiment 3 glass selection shows the greatest diversity and refinement among the three examples, consistent with a final optimized design rather than a conceptual variant.

### Production specifications (from Olympus documentation)

The manufacturer's published specifications for the Zuiko Auto-Macro 90mm 1:2 are as follows. The focal length is 90 mm with a maximum aperture of f/2 and minimum aperture of f/22. The angle of view is 27°. The optical construction consists of 9 elements in 9 groups, all multicoated, with no cemented pairs. The diaphragm is automatic with 9 blades. Minimum focus distance is 0.40 m, yielding a maximum reproduction ratio of 1:2 (0.5×) with a working distance of 240 mm at closest focus. The focusing helicoid has 47 mm of travel. The lens uses 55 mm screw-in filters, weighs 550 g, and measures 71 mm in length by 72 mm in maximum diameter. It was designed for the Olympus OM bayonet mount (46 mm flange distance).

### Scaling relationship

All numerical data in the patent is specified at a normalized focal length of f = 100 mm. The production lens has a marketed focal length of 90 mm, giving a uniform scale factor of ×0.9 applied to all radii, thicknesses, spacings, and semi-diameters. The paraxial ray trace of the unscaled Embodiment 3 prescription yields an effective focal length of 99.98 mm, which is in excellent agreement with the patent's stated f = 100. (A note on the patent source: the OCR rendering of Embodiment 3 contains several garbled values, including the φA and φB power parameters which appear as "0.0145" and "−0.0102" — these are the power values themselves, not focal lengths as shown for Embodiments 1 and 2. Independent ABCD computation confirms the focal lengths as fl_A = 69.0 mm and fl_B = −98.2 mm, consistent with the patterns across all three embodiments. Similarly, the d₆ value appears as "1.8882" in the OCR, which is corrected to 11.8882 based on cross-embodiment comparison with Embodiments 1 and 2, where d₆ = 11.1110 and 8.8889 respectively. The corrected value is confirmed by the resulting barrel length match: 71.7 mm computed vs 71 mm Olympus spec.)

At production scale, the computed EFL is 89.98 mm and the back focal length is 39.86 mm. The rear element of the lens therefore protrudes approximately 6 mm behind the OM mount flange (46 mm flange distance), which is standard practice for telephoto and medium-telephoto lenses in the OM system — the constraint is mirror clearance, not flange distance. The total optical track from front vertex to image plane is approximately 111.5 mm, giving a telephoto ratio (total track ÷ EFL) of 1.24. This satisfies the patent's stated bound of "1.25 or less," and is compact for an f/2 macro lens of this era; competing designs from the mid-1980s frequently exceeded 1.4. The mechanical length from front vertex to rear vertex is 71.7 mm at production scale, which matches the Olympus-published barrel length of 71 mm almost exactly.

### Aspherical surfaces

There are no aspherical surfaces in this design. The patent makes no mention of aspherical coefficients, no sag equation is presented, and the aberration correction figures label their axes with "F/2.06" or "NA 0.0220" rather than aspherical-related notation. All 19 optical surfaces are purely spherical. This is consistent with the mid-1980s era of the design: aspherical surface manufacture for interchangeable SLR lenses was still uncommon, and the modified Gauss architecture with a floating rear group provided sufficient degrees of freedom to control aberrations across the full focus range without aspherical assistance.

### Optical layout and group structure

The lens follows a three-group architecture described in the patent as comprising a first lens group (Group I) with positive refractive power, a stop, a second lens group (Group II) with positive refractive power, and a third lens group (Group III). Groups I and II together form what the patent calls a "modified Gauss type subsystem" — a classic double-Gauss derivative with the stop located in the central air space. Group III is a weakly-powered rear group that acts as an aberration-correcting element during focusing.

**Group I** (front positive group, elements L1–L3) has a combined focal length of 171.0 mm at the f = 100 scale. It comprises two positive meniscus lenses followed by a negative meniscus, arranged to gradually converge the incoming beam while introducing controlled amounts of spherical aberration and coma for balancing against the rear groups. This group contains the thickest element in the entire system — L2, a dense lanthanum crown meniscus with a center thickness of 14.0 mm (12.6 mm at production scale), which the patent specifically addresses via Condition (8): 0.1f < d₃ < 0.17f. The thick meniscus serves to control the g-line (short wavelength) spherical aberration that would otherwise become excessively positive.

**Group II** (rear positive group, elements L4–L6) has a combined focal length of 109.6 mm. It sits behind the aperture stop and comprises a negative meniscus, a positive meniscus, and a biconvex lens. This group completes the double-Gauss symmetry and provides the primary positive power of the system. The biconvex element L6 uses the highest-index glass in the entire lens (nd = 1.77250, HOYA TAFL3 / Schott LaK10), which the patent requires via Condition (10): n₆ > 1.7. This high-index crown is essential for simultaneously controlling Petzval sum and spherical aberration.

The subsystem formed by Groups I and II (including the stop) has a combined focal length of 100.5 mm — nearly equal to the system focal length, confirming that the subsystem alone is already a nearly complete imaging system. Its aberrations are corrected "to some extent" in isolation, which provides manufacturing tolerance for the mechanically imprecise air gap between the subsystem and Group III.

**Group III** (floating rear group, elements L7–L9) has a combined focal length of 391.1 mm — an extremely weak positive group whose power is only about 2.6% of the system power. Patent Claim 1 describes this group as "consisting of a negative lens unit and one biconvex lens," meaning that L7 and L8 together form the negative subunit while L9 is the single biconvex element. Despite its weakness at the group level, it contains individually strong elements: L9 is a biconvex lens with fl = 69.0 mm (the strongest positive element in the group), and L7 is a biconcave lens with fl = −98.2 mm (the strongest negative element). The patent's Conditions (3) and (4) require these individual elements to have substantial power for correcting residual fifth-order aberrations and Petzval sum, even though their contributions largely cancel at the group level. Condition (5) requires that the positive element have a higher refractive index than the negative element (n₉ = 1.65160 > n₇ = 1.51742), which helps to flatten the Petzval field.

The patent states ΣDIII (the distance from the first to last surface within Group III) as 21.153 mm, which the design conditions require to lie between 0.14f and 0.23f (i.e., 14.0–23.0 mm). Summing the patent's individual thickness values d₁₄ through d₁₈ yields 20.954 mm — a small 0.2 mm discrepancy attributable to a probable minor OCR transcription error in one of the spacing values. Both the stated and computed values satisfy Condition (9).

The computed Petzval sum for the complete system is 0.00125 mm⁻¹, corresponding to a Petzval radius of −801 mm at the f = 100 scale (−721 mm at production). This is a moderately well-corrected Petzval sum for an f/2 lens of this focal length — flat enough for good macro performance but not so aggressively flattened as to sacrifice other aberrations.

### Element-by-element description

What follows is a detailed account of each of the nine elements, proceeding from the front of the lens (object side) to the rear (image side). All dimensions are given at the patent's f = 100 scale unless otherwise noted. Focal lengths and shape classifications are derived from thick-lens ABCD matrix computation.

**Element L1** — Positive meniscus, convex toward the object. R₁ = +58.78, R₂ = +1042.0, center thickness 7.80 mm. Glass: nd = 1.69680, νd = 55.52, identified as HOYA TAC4 (exact match) or equivalently OHARA S-LAL14 / Schott LaK14. This is a lanthanum crown with moderate index and high Abbe number. Focal length: +89.1 mm. As the first element, L1 provides the initial convergence of the marginal ray. Its nearly plano-convex shape (the rear surface has over 1000 mm radius) minimizes spherical aberration contribution from the front surface, distributing the bending smoothly. It is the primary contributor to the entrance pupil geometry.

**Element L2** — Positive meniscus, convex toward the object. R₃ = +42.73, R₄ = +76.13, center thickness 14.01 mm. Glass: nd = 1.71300, νd = 53.84, identified as HOYA TACL1 / OHARA S-LAL18 / Schott LaK8. Another lanthanum crown, slightly denser than L1. Focal length: +116.3 mm. This is the thick meniscus that the patent discusses at length. Its exceptional center thickness creates a significant difference between the marginal ray heights at the front and rear surfaces, which produces a net negative contribution to spherical aberration at short wavelengths (g-line) that partially cancels the positive g-line spherical introduced by the system's overall convergence. The deep meniscus shape also contributes to coma correction. At 14.0 mm (12.6 mm production), this element alone accounts for roughly 18% of the total optical track.

**Element L3** — Negative meniscus, convex toward the object. R₅ = +156.59, R₆ = +27.14, center thickness 3.82 mm. Glass: nd = 1.74000, νd = 28.29, identified as HOYA FD4 / Schott SF4 / OHARA S-TIH6. This is a dense flint glass with high dispersion (low Abbe number). Focal length: −44.9 mm. L3 is the strongest negative element in the front group and plays multiple critical roles. Its high-dispersion glass provides the primary chromatic aberration correction for Group I, acting as the "flint" in what is effectively an achromatic front group. Its strongly curved rear surface (R₆ = 27.14 mm) introduces substantial negative spherical aberration and positive coma, both of which are needed to balance the contributions from L1 and L2. The air gap between L3's rear surface and the aperture stop (d₆ = 11.89 mm) is one of the largest in the system.

**Aperture stop** — Located between Groups I and II, the stop is placed 11.89 mm behind L3 and 4.78 mm ahead of L4. In the patent's numbering, it is r₇ = ∞ (flat), at surface index 7. The stop position splits the system into its double-Gauss halves, enabling the approximate symmetry that is the foundation of the design's wide-angle aberration correction.

**Element L4** — Negative meniscus, concave toward the object. R₈ = −42.44, R₉ = −532.61, center thickness 2.12 mm. Glass: nd = 1.56732, νd = 42.83, identified as HOYA BACL1 / Schott BaK1 / OHARA S-BAM4. A light barium crown. Focal length: −81.4 mm. L4 is the first element behind the stop and begins the rear half of the double-Gauss subsystem. The patent describes it as "a negative lens having a concave surface with strong curvature on the object side" — the strongly concave R₈ = −42.44 mm surface carries a heavy burden of aberration correction, particularly for spherical aberration and coma. Condition (7) constrains the curvature of L4's rear surface (|1/r₉|), balancing spherical and chromatic aberration against off-axis performance.

**Element L5** — Positive meniscus, concave toward the object (with both radii negative, the element is concave toward the object and convex toward the image). R₁₀ = −112.39, R₁₁ = −63.36, center thickness 5.00 mm. Glass: nd = 1.74400, νd = 44.73, identified as HOYA LAF7 / Schott LaF21 / OHARA S-LAM7. A lanthanum flint — a high-index glass with intermediate dispersion. Focal length: +187.1 mm. L5 provides moderate positive power behind the stop while using a lanthanum flint glass that contributes favorably to the Petzval sum correction. Its meniscus shape is characteristic of positive elements positioned just behind the stop in double-Gauss designs, where the near-symmetry of the front and rear groups demands matched curvature orientations. The high refractive index allows positive power with relatively weak curvatures, which suppresses higher-order spherical aberration.

**Element L6** — Biconvex. R₁₂ = +85.56, R₁₃ = −153.00, center thickness 4.83 mm. Glass: nd = 1.77250, νd = 49.66, identified as HOYA TAFL3 (exact match to patent values including νd = 49.66). Schott LaK10 and OHARA S-LAH55 have νd = 49.60, a small but real difference that distinguishes the HOYA formulation. Focal length: +71.7 mm. L6 is the most powerful positive element in the entire subsystem and carries the principal imaging power of Group II. It is the only true biconvex element in the front half of the lens. The patent's Condition (10) explicitly requires nd > 1.7 for this element to simultaneously correct Petzval sum and spherical aberration. The glass is a lanthanum crown — not a flint — despite its intermediate Abbe number, and it provides a favorable balance between high index (good for Petzval) and moderate dispersion (acceptable chromatic contribution when paired with the flint elements).

**Element L7** — Biconcave. R₁₄ = −1087.1, R₁₅ = +53.33, center thickness 2.11 mm. Glass: nd = 1.51742, νd = 52.41, identified as HOYA CFKL2 / Schott KF9 / OHARA S-NSL5. This is a light crown/flint glass with low refractive index. Focal length: −98.2 mm. L7 is the first element of Group III and the "negative lens having the strongest refractive power" referenced in the patent's conditions. Its low refractive index (n₇ = 1.51742) is paired against L9's higher index (n₉ = 1.65160) to satisfy Condition (5): nA > nB. This index differential is the mechanism by which Group III contributes a negative Petzval sum, flattening the overall field curvature. The nearly-flat front surface (R₁₄ = −1087.1 mm) means that almost all of L7's optical power comes from the strongly curved rear surface R₁₅ = +53.33 mm.

**Element L8** — Negative meniscus, convex toward the object (with both radii positive, the element is concave toward the image side). R₁₆ = +105.40, R₁₇ = +70.64, center thickness 3.33 mm. Glass: nd = 1.69895, νd = 30.12, identified as HOYA FD5 / Schott SF5 / OHARA S-TIM25. A dense flint glass. Focal length: −319.0 mm. L8 is a weak negative element that acts primarily as a chromatic corrector within Group III. Its dense flint glass provides the dispersion needed to achromatize the group's net (weak positive) power. Despite being classified as a negative meniscus, its power is modest, and its primary contribution is to the spectral balance of Group III rather than to its focal length.

**Element L9** — Biconvex. R₁₈ = +70.53, R₁₉ = −118.01, center thickness 8.50 mm. Glass: nd = 1.65160, νd = 58.52, identified as HOYA BSC7 (exact match) / OHARA S-BSL7 / Schott SK2. A dense barium crown with high Abbe number — a classic crown glass for positive power applications. Focal length: +69.0 mm. L9 is the strongest positive element in Group III and the element referenced as "a positive lens having the strongest refractive power" in the patent's Condition (3). It is also the rearmost element in the system, and its rear surface R₁₉ defines the back focal distance. At 8.50 mm center thickness (7.65 mm production), it is the second-thickest element in the lens after L2. Its position at the rear of the system, far from the stop, gives it strong leverage over off-axis aberrations, particularly astigmatism and field curvature. The patent's ideal composition of Group III — negative element on the object side, positive element on the image side, with substantial separation — is realized here with L7 and L9 separated by L8 and two air gaps spanning a total of ΣDIII = 21.15 mm.

The glass selection reveals a deliberate strategy. The front group uses two lanthanum crowns (high index, low dispersion) paired with one dense flint for chromatic balance. The rear of the subsystem deploys a barium crown, a lanthanum flint, and the highest-index lanthanum crown in the system. Group III uses a notably low-index crown for the negative element (n = 1.517) against a dense barium crown for the positive element (n = 1.652), creating the index differential that drives Petzval correction via Condition (5).

### Focusing mechanism

The Zuiko 90mm f/2 Macro uses what the patent describes as a differential-speed whole-lens-advance focusing system with aberration correction (commonly known as "floating elements"). The key parameter is α = (Δ₁₂ − Δ₃)/Δ₁₂ = 0.370, where Δ₁₂ is the total advance of the subsystem (Groups I + II) and Δ₃ is the advance of Group III.

The focusing mechanism works as follows. As the focus ring is turned from infinity toward close focus, the entire lens advances forward. However, the subsystem (Groups I + II plus the stop) advances faster than Group III. Group III advances at 63.0% of the subsystem's speed (1 − α = 0.630). This differential motion causes the air gap d₁₃ (between L6's rear surface and L7's front surface) to increase progressively, from 0.89 mm at infinity to 20.28 mm at 1/2× magnification — a change of 19.39 mm at the f = 100 scale.

At the production scale (×0.9), the subsystem advances approximately 47.2 mm from infinity to half life size. This matches the Olympus specification of a 47 mm focusing helicoid to within 0.2 mm, providing strong confirmation that Embodiment 3 is the production design. Group III advances approximately 29.7 mm over the same range.

The variable gap d₁₃ is the sole variable air spacing in this design, which means the focusing mechanism requires only a simple cam or differential helicoidal coupling rather than the more complex multi-cam arrangements needed for designs with multiple variable gaps. The patent explicitly notes this as an advantage: "for the airspace between the second lens group II and the third lens group III, it is not easy to obtain high accuracy in the dimension from the view point of the structure of the lens mount." By concentrating the aberration correction into a single variable gap and ensuring that the subsystem is well-corrected in isolation, the design is tolerant of mechanical imprecision in the Group III coupling.

The patent provides d₁₃ values at three focus positions (at f = 100 scale):

| Object distance | Magnification | d₁₃ (mm) | Change   |
|-----------------|---------------|-----------|----------|
| ∞               | 0×            | 0.889     | —        |
| ~1000 mm        | 1/10×         | 5.283     | +4.394   |
| ~400 mm         | 1/2×          | 20.277    | +19.388  |

The design is optimized for performance at 1/10× magnification, as confirmed by the Olympus documentation. At this magnification the gap has expanded to 5.28 mm — large enough to provide meaningful aberration correction but not so large as to push the group separation into a regime where higher-order effects dominate. The aberration plots (Figs. 9–11 in the patent) confirm this optimization: Fig. 10 (1/10×) shows the best-corrected spherical aberration and the tightest astigmatism curves, while Figs. 9 (infinity) and 11 (1/2×) show slightly less optimal but still well-controlled performance.

### Aberration performance

The patent provides aberration plots for Embodiment 3 at three focus distances. At infinity (Fig. 9, F/2.06), spherical aberration shows a modest negative residual of approximately 0.2 mm for the d-line, with the g-line showing stronger undercorrection typical of a high-speed lens. Astigmatism is controlled within ±0.2 mm across the 13.5° half-field, with the sagittal and meridional field curves showing moderate separation. Distortion is essentially negligible — well under 1% at full field. Coma at 9.45° shows controlled d-line contribution with some g-line flare.

At 1/10× magnification (Fig. 10, NA 0.0220), the aberrations reach their best-corrected state. Spherical aberration is nearly zero for the d-line, astigmatism is tight, and coma is well-balanced. This confirms the Olympus documentation's statement that the lens was designed for "optimum performance at a magnification of ×0.1."

At 1/2× magnification (Fig. 11, NA 0.0763), the aberrations increase but remain remarkably well-controlled for a lens operating at half life size. Spherical aberration shows a more pronounced negative residual, and the astigmatic field curves separate further, but neither degradation is severe. The floating element mechanism is clearly earning its keep at this magnification — without it, the aberration variation would be far larger.

### Design heritage and context

The Zuiko 90mm f/2 Macro belongs to the "second series" of Olympus OM lenses, which were designed with an emphasis on large aperture and high performance rather than the extreme compactness that characterized the first series. The first series included the Zuiko Auto-Macro 50mm f/3.5, a much smaller and slower lens. The 90mm f/2 Macro was a statement lens — it demonstrated that Olympus could deliver f/2 speed in a macro lens, something no other manufacturer had achieved in this focal length range at the time.

The modified Gauss architecture is a natural choice for an f/2 lens, and versions of this basic layout appear in many fast normal and short telephoto lenses from the 1960s onward. What distinguishes the Mihara design is the addition of the three-element floating rear group (Group III), which allows the lens to maintain high performance across a focus range spanning infinity to half life size without requiring multiple variable air gaps or a complex cam mechanism. The patent's analysis explicitly compares this approach to earlier floating-element designs and demonstrates its superiority for the specific combination of large aperture, long focus, and wide focus range.

The lens was produced in at least two marking variants ("ZUIKO AUTO-MACRO 90mm 1:2" and "ZUIKO AUTO-MACRO f=90mm 1:2.0") and was offered with either metric or Imperial focusing scales. Serial numbers span several blocks from 101xxx through 113xxx, indicating substantial production volume over the lens's 17-year production life. The lens accepted a clamp-on 57 mm rubber hood (shared with the 135mm f/4.5 Macro) and was compatible with Olympus's macro flash accessories through its 55 mm filter thread.
