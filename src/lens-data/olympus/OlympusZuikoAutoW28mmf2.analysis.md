# Olympus Zuiko Auto-W 28mm f/2

## Patent Reference and Design Identification

**Patent:** US 3,862,794A\
**Application Number:** US 324,567\
**Filed:** January 18, 1973\
**Granted:** January 28, 1975\
**Priority:** January 26, 1972 (Japan 47-9015); August 17, 1972 (Japan 47-81802)\
**Inventor:** Toru Fujii\
**Assignee:** Olympus Optical Co., Ltd.\
**Title:** Large-Aperture Ratio Retrofocus Lens System\
**Embodiment analyzed:** Embodiment 2, Table 2, FIG. 2; repeated in Claim 4

The patent describes an approximately 76°-field, f/2 retrofocus family for single-lens reflex cameras. Embodiment 2 is the strongest match to the production Olympus Zuiko Auto-W 28mm f/2, although the patent does not name a commercial lens. The identification rests on convergent evidence:

1. **Element and group count.** Embodiment 2 has nine elements in eight air-separated groups. Olympus specifies the production lens as nine elements in eight groups.
2. **Aperture and field.** The patent gives F:2 and an approximately 76° family field; the Embodiment 2 aberration chart is plotted to 37° semi-field. Olympus specifies 28mm f/2 and a 75° angle of view.
3. **Rear-component arrangement.** Embodiment 1 is 9/7 because L5 is cemented. Embodiment 2 separates the two L5 elements and is uniquely 9/8. Embodiment 3 is 10/9, while Embodiments 4 and 5 are 10/8.
4. **All-spherical prescription.** Table 2 contains only spherical radii, consistent with the production-era construction.
5. **Focus specification.** Olympus identifies the production lens as a floating-element design with a 0.3 m minimum focus distance. The patent gives only a single prescription and does not disclose the floating trajectories.

Direct inspection of Table 2 on patent page 5 and its repetition in Claim 4 on patent page 9 resolves two OCR-prone entries. The rear surface of L5b is $r_{12}=+223.486$, and the long air space behind L4 is $d_9=13.76$, not 3.76. Both values are printed consistently in the two independent occurrences. With the correct $d_9$, a reduced-angle paraxial matrix trace gives

$$
f_{\mathrm{EFL}}=99.993492
$$

in patent units, agreeing with the stated normalization $f=100$ to 0.0065%. The production-scale data use

$$
S=\frac{28}{99.9934920817}=0.2800182234,
$$

which gives an exact paraxial EFL of 28.000000 mm without changing any radius sign or relative spacing.

## Optical Architecture

The design is a two-group retrofocus, or inverted-telephoto, system. A negative front group creates the long back focus required by an SLR mirror box; a positive rear group restores convergence and performs most of the high-aperture aberration correction.

The front group contains a positive meniscus L1 followed by two negative menisci L2 and L3, all convex toward the object. The patent identifies division of the conventional front concave component into two negative menisci as a principal means of reducing the positive spherical aberration and off-axis coma generated by a strongly diverging front group (columns 1–2).

The rear group contains the cemented L4 doublet, the air-spaced two-element L5 component, positive meniscus L6, and biconvex L7. The cemented interface $r_8$ carries strong positive surface power because light crosses from $n_d=1.50378$ to $n_d=1.72000$ at a short positive radius. L5 has net negative power and supplies coma and sagittal-flare correction within the otherwise positive rear group. L6 and L7 complete the spherical-aberration, field-curvature, and coma balance.

At patent scale, the front group focal length is $-126.8078$ and the rear group focal length is $+94.2597$. At production scale these become $-35.5085$ mm and $+26.3944$ mm. The back focal distance is 134.5733 patent units, giving BFD/EFL = 1.345821. Because the back focal distance exceeds the effective focal length, the layout is unambiguously retrofocus.

## Element-by-Element Analysis

### L1 — Positive Meniscus, convex to object

$n_d=1.64000$, $\nu_d=60.3$. Glass: LACL60-class lanthanum crown, closest catalog code 640/602 to the patent pair 640/603. Standalone $f=+86.269$ mm.

L1 is a thick, weak positive collector. It limits how abruptly the wide-angle bundle enters the negative front section and distributes the front-group power over more surfaces. Its high Abbe number limits the chromatic burden of a large front element.

### L2 — Negative Meniscus, convex to object

$n_d=1.50378$, $\nu_d=66.8$. Glass: unmatched crown, code 504/668. Standalone $f=-31.661$ mm.

L2 supplies most of the negative power in the front group. Its high Abbe number is deliberate: a strongly powered negative element placed far forward would otherwise add substantial lateral chromatic aberration.

### L3 — Negative Meniscus, convex to object

$n_d=1.50378$, $\nu_d=66.8$. Glass: unmatched crown, code 504/668. Standalone $f=-110.123$ mm.

L3 is the weaker negative meniscus. Splitting the front negative function between L2 and L3 avoids concentrating the required divergence at one highly curved rear surface. Together, L1–L3 form a front group of $f=-35.508$ mm at production scale.

### L4a — Negative Meniscus, front member of cemented L4

$n_d=1.50378$, $\nu_d=66.8$. Glass: unmatched crown, code 504/668. Standalone in-air $f=-47.531$ mm.

The quoted focal length is the element’s standalone in-air value. L4a does not behave as an isolated negative singlet in the cemented assembly. Its rear surface is the $r_8$ junction into L4b, and the positive index step across that interface is central to the patent’s correction strategy.

### L4b — Plano-Convex Positive, rear member of cemented L4

$n_d=1.72000$, $\nu_d=46.0$. Glass: K-LaFn11-class lanthanum flint. Standalone in-air $f=+26.303$ mm.

L4b is thick and strongly positive. Its plane rear surface leaves most of the singlet power at the curved front interface. The complete cemented L4 component has $f=+59.395$ mm at production scale, but its aberration-control role depends specifically on the in-situ $n_5-n_4$ step at $r_8$ and cannot be reduced to that net focal length alone.

### L5a — Biconvex Positive

$n_d=1.78800$, $\nu_d=47.5$. Glass: TAF4-class dense lanthanum flint. Standalone $f=+28.504$ mm.

L5a is the positive front member of the divided fifth component. Its first surface is nearly plane, while its strongly negative rear radius supplies most of the singlet’s positive power. The air separation from L5b distinguishes Embodiment 2 from the cemented Embodiment 1.

### L5b — Biconcave Negative

$n_d=1.76182$, $\nu_d=26.6$. Glass: FD140-class dense flint. Standalone $f=-20.732$ mm.

L5b is a high-dispersion negative element. Its two radii are $r_{11'}=-75.997$ and $r_{12}=+223.486$ in patent units, making it biconcave under the patent sign convention. L5a and L5b together have net $f=-94.521$ mm at production scale. The patent links this negative component, particularly its negatively curved surface, to coma asymmetry and sagittal-flare control (columns 3–4).

### L6 — Positive Meniscus, convex to image

$n_d=1.69680$, $\nu_d=55.6$. Glass: K-LaK14-class lanthanum crown. Standalone $f=+42.571$ mm.

Both radii are negative, so the element is concave toward the object and convex toward the image. Its high index supplies positive power with moderate thickness, while $\nu_d>50$ limits chromatic burden. The patent assigns L6 and L7 an important role in producing negative spherical aberration that offsets the front group’s positive contribution.

### L7 — Biconvex Positive

$n_d=1.69680$, $\nu_d=55.6$. Glass: K-LaK14-class lanthanum crown. Standalone $f=+53.523$ mm.

L7 is the final positive element. Its weak object-side curvature satisfies the patent’s coma-control condition on $r_{15}$. Used with L6, it completes the rear positive relay and contributes to the field-curvature and coma balance.

## Glass Identification and Selection

The patent gives only $n_d$ and $\nu_d$; it does not identify glass manufacturers. Catalog names are therefore class matches, not proof of the original Olympus melts. Six distinct optical glasses are present.

| Patent pair | Authoritative catalog-class match | Residual / confidence | Elements | Optical role |
|---|---|---:|---|---|
| 1.64000 / 60.3 | LACL60 class, HOYA reference 640/602; N-LAK21 and S-BSM81 equivalents | $\Delta n_d\approx0$, $\Delta\nu_d=-0.1$; probable class | L1 | Low-dispersion front collector |
| 1.50378 / 66.8 | Unmatched crown, 504/668 | No sufficiently close catalog entry; retain as unmatched | L2, L3, L4a | Front negative group and L4 front member |
| 1.72000 / 46.0 | K-LaFn11, SUMITA 1.72013 / 46.0 | $\Delta n_d=+0.00013$; high-confidence class | L4b | Strong positive cemented-interface partner |
| 1.78800 / 47.5 | TAF4, HOYA reference 788/475 | Six-digit pair match; high-confidence class | L5a | High-index positive member of L5 |
| 1.76182 / 26.6 | FD140, HOYA 762/266 | $\Delta n_d\approx-0.00001$; high-confidence class | L5b | Dense-flint negative and chromatic partner |
| 1.69680 / 55.6 | K-LaK14, SUMITA 697/556 | Exact $n_d/\nu_d$ match | L6, L7 | Rear positive correctors |

The 504/668 material should not be labeled N-BK10: the index mismatch is too large for a confident identification. The patent’s $n_d/\nu_d$ values also do not establish anomalous partial dispersion, so no APD or apochromatic claim is made.

## Focus Mechanism

Olympus’s lens catalog identifies the production 28mm f/2 as a floating-element design and gives a minimum focus distance of 0.3 m. US 3,862,794 does not identify which element or group floats and does not publish alternate finite-distance spacing tables. The divided L5 construction alone is insufficient evidence for assigning a specific floating trajectory.

The data file therefore preserves the patent infinity geometry and uses a clearly labeled BFD-only conjugate surrogate for the viewer. Solving the reduced-angle system matrix for an object plane 0.3 m from the image plane gives:

| State | Final BFD |
|---|---:|
| Infinity | 37.682976 mm |
| 0.3 m surrogate | 41.308916 mm |
| Extension | 3.625940 mm |

The surrogate produces paraxial magnification $|m|\approx0.1295\times$. It is not a reconstruction of the production floating-element mechanism or its close-range aberration correction.

## Data-File Implementation

The patent does not dimension the aperture-stop coordinate. FIG. 2 draws the stop near L5 within the long $d_9$ air space; the data file places it approximately 85% through that gap. At production scale the split is 3.275093 mm before the stop and 0.577958 mm after it. A paraxial pupil trace gives a stop semi-diameter of 9.076170 mm for a 7.000000 mm entrance-pupil radius, reproducing f/2 at 28 mm.

Numerical semi-diameters are absent from the patent. The file estimates them from combined marginal and chief rays, the FIG. 2 section, and the production lens’s 49 mm filter envelope. The refined values preserve the large front collector, taper the middle and rear elements toward the proportions drawn in FIG. 2, and pass the renderer's rim-slope, positive-edge-thickness, cross-gap, hidden-trim, and default 30%-field ray-envelope checks.

These semi-diameters are implementation estimates, not patent dimensions. The wide-open edge field is mechanically vignetted; the diagram therefore displays a 30%-field off-axis fan rather than implying unobstructed ray transmission to the full 37.5° semi-field.

## Design Context

The patent states that conventional approximately 76° retrofocus systems of the period generally used a positive and a negative front component and were difficult to extend beyond about f/3.5. Fujii’s design distributes the front negative power over two menisci and adds a strongly positive cemented interface in the rear group. The numerical examples explore several forms of the negative L5 component; the separated two-element form of Embodiment 2 is the only one matching the production lens’s 9/8 construction.
