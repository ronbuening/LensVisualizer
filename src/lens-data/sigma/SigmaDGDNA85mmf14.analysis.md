## 1. Production-lens identification

The patent contains eight numerical embodiments. Example 1 corresponds to the production 85 mm F/1.4 DG DN | Art. The identification rests on convergent evidence rather than on any single distinguishing fact:

The patent prescription gives 15 elements arranged in 11 groups, exactly matching the production lens's published construction. The patent design F-number is 1.46; the marketed aperture of F/1.4 is the nearest standard half-stop label and is the value Sigma publishes. The single most distinctive identifier is the close-focus geometry — among the patent's eight numerical embodiments, Example 1 is the only one whose minimum-object-distance state places the subject at 850 mm from the image plane (`d0 + total lens length = 740.6522 + 109.35 = 850.00 mm`); Examples 2–8 all use 800 mm. The Sigma 85 mm F/1.4 DG DN | Art's published minimum focusing distance is 0.85 m, fitting only Example 1.

Several other markers converge. Example 1 has exactly one aspherical element — the rear-most L15 with both surfaces aspherical — matching Sigma's optical diagram, which identifies one aspherical element at the rear. Example 1 also implements the simple inner-focus mechanism in which a single negative meniscus L7 between the stop and the rear group translates rigidly along the axis (claim 8); Examples 2 and 3 alternatively implement claims 10 and 11 (different focus-group definitions involving the stop). Sigma's product description specifies a stepping-motor inner-focus design with a small focus element, consistent with the single-element L7 mechanism of Example 1.

The full field angle reported in the patent (2ω = 28.12° at infinity) and the angle of view specified by Sigma (28.6°) are both consistent with an 85 mm-class lens projecting onto the 43.27 mm full-frame diagonal but represent different conventions. Sigma's value is the paraxial *f*-tan calculation at the marketed focal length: 2 · atan(21.63/85) = 28.55°, which rounds to 28.6°. The patent's value is the actual chief-ray angle in object space at the design focal length 82.96 mm; the difference between this and the undistorted paraxial prediction 2 · atan(21.63/82.96) = 29.24° corresponds to approximately +4 % pincushion distortion at the corner — a residual visible directly in the patent's figure 2 (Example 1, infinity, distortion plot), and one that the production lens corrects in software via the camera-side distortion profile.

## 2. Optical architecture

Example 1 is described in the patent as a three-group construction: a positive front group G1, a negative inner-focus group G2, and a positive rear group G3. The patent overlays a second functional decomposition onto this: G1 corresponds to the front group GA (前側レンズ群); G2 is the focus component LF (合焦レンズ成分); and the union of G2 and G3 corresponds to the rear group GB (後続レンズ群). The aperture stop S sits between G1 and G2, immediately preceding the focusing element. This is an asymmetric fast-portrait architecture — not a classical symmetric Gauss layout — in which a strong, element-rich positive front group converges the beam onto a small aperture stop, after which a heavily-corrected rear tail (with the focus element interposed) cleans up off-axis residuals and supplies the final optical power required to land the image on the sensor.

The fundamental design problem this layout addresses is that an 85 mm F/1.4 lens places extreme demands on chromatic correction. The marginal-ray height in the front group is large (the entrance-pupil diameter is 82.96 / 1.46 ≈ 56.8 mm at the patent design F-number), so any colour error introduced there is amplified by a long lever arm before reaching the image plane. Sigma's published technical description for this lens places "particular emphasis given to the correction of axial chromatic aberration," which is the dominant residual that compromises out-of-focus rendering at this aperture.

## 3. Element-by-element analysis

Element focal lengths are computed as standalone thick-lens-in-air values rather than in-context contributions; the latter would require accounting for the convergence state of each ray bundle and would not isolate each element's intrinsic refractive role. The patent prescription assigns surfaces 1–11 to the front group G1, the stop at surface 12, the focus element L7 at surfaces 13–14, and the rear group G3 at surfaces 15–27.

### 3.1 Front group G1 (= GA): the converger

L1 is a positive meniscus convex to the object (`R₁ = 76.05 mm`, `R₂ = 107.53 mm`, `t = 3.63 mm`) made of a very dense flint with `n_d = 1.94595`, `ν_d = 17.98`. Its standalone focal length is +260 mm — only mildly positive. The element's role is not to do the bulk of the converging work: with a beam diameter approaching 60 mm at the front, the dominant requirement is to compress that beam diameter quickly so that downstream elements can be made smaller and lighter. The patent identifies this element explicitly as the LPH — the high-refractive-index, high-dispersion positive lens that is the patent's claimed novelty. Paragraph ¶0028 of the patent states the thesis directly: applying a glass material that is simultaneously high-index, high-dispersion, *and* anomalously dispersive in the short-wavelength region near the g-line to the LPH within GA allows colour, spherical aberration, and astigmatism all to be suppressed while permitting the system to be made smaller and lighter. By giving L1 a very high refractive index, the element bends rays steeply at modest curvature, reducing both spherical-aberration generation per surface (because the radius can be longer for the same power) and the beam diameter of the elements behind it. The trade-off — heavy g–F dispersion (`ν_d` below 18 is exceptional even among dense flints) — is converted from a liability into an asset by the LPH-class glass's anomalous partial-dispersion behaviour, which gives the downstream high-Abbe positives and the LN flint a third lever for secondary-spectrum cancellation. Paragraph ¶0079 adds a secondary motivation for siting this glass at the most-object-side surface: high-`n_d` glasses in this regime exhibit low surface abrasion, so placing the LPH at the front-most position contributes to the lens's durability and weather resistance as well.

L2 (`R₁ = 71.22`, `R₂ = 136.93`, `t = 5.43`) and L3 (`R₁ = 42.01`, `R₂ = 74.25`, `t = 6.45`) are positive menisci sharing the glass `1.55032 / 75.50` — a high-Abbe, low-index optical glass with anomalous partial dispersion typical of the SLD (special low dispersion) class. Their standalone focal lengths are +262 mm and +164 mm respectively. Together with the positive elements that follow, they perform classical chromatic correction: the LPH front element contributes large negative C–F and g–F dispersion which L2 and L3 partially cancel because their high `ν_d` means they bend rays nearly equally across wavelengths. This is exactly the situation contemplated by the patent's condition (4): `ν_d > 60` for the *non*-LPH positive elements in GA.

L4 + L5 forms the only cemented doublet in G1 (denoted D1 in the data file), with the cemented junction convex to the object (`R = 75.06`). L4 is positive (`R₁ = 34.38`, `R₂ = 75.06`, `t = 7.05`, `n_d = 1.59282`, `ν_d = 68.62` — another SLD-class glass, `f₄ = +101 mm`), while L5 is a thin negative meniscus (`R₁ = 75.06`, `R₂ = 25.76`, `t = 1.20`, `n_d = 1.85451`, `ν_d = 25.15`, `θ_gF = 0.6103`, `f₅ = −46 mm`). The L5 glass is precisely the LN element invoked by the patent's claim 4: an anomalous-partial-dispersion flint with `ν_d < 30` and `θ_gF − (0.648285 − 0.00180123 × ν_d) = +0.0073 < 0.010`. The cemented doublet has a net focal length of −103 mm — strongly negative. This is the patent's claim 5 in action: among the negative elements in GA (only L5 is negative), the LN has the strongest negative power, which lets it contribute most aggressively to g-line secondary-spectrum correction. The doublet's strong negative power also corrects spherical aberration generated by the steeply curved positive predecessors.

L6 (`R₁ = 42.10`, `R₂ = 118.46`, `t = 4.47`) is the final element of G1 — a positive meniscus in the same `1.59282 / 68.62` SLD glass as L4. Its focal length is +108 mm. It re-converges the beam after the negative L4–L5 doublet has dispersed it, completing the front group's net positive power and bringing the beam to a controlled diameter at the aperture stop. The 3.35 mm air gap that follows L6 (before the stop) plus the variable gap d12 sets the working distance from the front group to the focusing element.

### 3.2 The aperture stop and focus element

The aperture stop S is at surface 12, immediately following L6 with a 2.7882 mm air gap at infinity focus.

L7 (`R₁ = 240.45`, `R₂ = 31.73`, `t = 0.90`, `n_d = 1.59349`, `ν_d = 67.00`) is a thin negative meniscus with both surfaces convex to the object — a very long, nearly flat front surface and a strongly curved rear. Its focal length is −61.7 mm. It is the entire G2 group, and it is the entire focus mechanism. The patent identifies this single element as the focus component LF (合焦レンズ成分), and section 6 of this analysis treats it in detail. The choice of glass — a moderate-index glass with high Abbe number — is significant: the focus element must not introduce focus-state-dependent chromatic aberration as it moves, so its dispersion is kept low. The element is also lightweight (the high Abbe number generally correlates with lower density in this index range), which reduces the inertial load on the focus actuator.

### 3.3 Rear group G3: the corrector

L8 + L9 forms a cemented doublet (denoted D2) whose junction (surface 16, `R = +22.80`) is convex to the object — both bonded faces share this curvature. L8 is a thin negative meniscus convex to the object (`R₁ = 36.89`, `R₂ = 22.80`, `t = 0.90`, `n_d = 1.85451`, `ν_d = 25.15` — the same anomalous flint as L5) and L9 is a positive meniscus also convex to the object (`R₁ = 22.80`, `R₂ = 58.05`, `t = 4.36`, `n_d = 1.59282`, `ν_d = 68.62`); these shape descriptions match the patent's own prose at ¶0096. The doublet's net focal length is +489 mm — only weakly positive. Its function is not converging power but rather residual chromatic correction: the high-anomalous-dispersion negative L8 paired with the high-Abbe positive L9 cleans up the secondary spectrum that survives the front group, particularly for rays traversing the doublet at non-zero field height. Note that this doublet inverts the front-group D1's L4/L5 ordering — here the negative element is on the object side rather than the image side — which alters the surface-by-surface aberration generation pattern in a way useful for correcting field-dependent residuals. (For the same reason this junction does not satisfy the patent's claim 6 in the strict sense, since the object-side index `n_d = 1.85451` exceeds the image-side `1.59282`; the design relies on the L10–L11 and L13–L14 cement junctions further back, both of which place lower index on the object side as claim 6 requires, to provide the coma-suppression role described in ¶0067.)

L10 + L11 (D3) is a biconcave + biconvex cemented doublet (junction at `R = 55.76`, convex to the object). L10 (`R₁ = −46.55`, `R₂ = 55.76`, `t = 0.95`, `n_d = 1.85451`, `ν_d = 25.15`) is a thin biconcave element of the same anomalous flint glass as L5 and L8; L11 (`R₁ = 55.76`, `R₂ = −136.97`, `t = 3.96`) uses a very dense glass `n_d = 2.00100`, `ν_d = 29.13` — likely from the lanthanum-niobate family typically marketed as TAFD-class glass. The doublet's net focal length is −123 mm. Together with the biconvex L12 that follows, this section forms a strong correcting Gaussian module that handles the beam at large field heights with substantial off-axis aberration burden.

L12 (`R₁ = 100.85`, `R₂ = −58.99`, `t = 4.95`, `n_d = 2.05090`, `ν_d = 26.94`) is a biconvex element using the highest-index glass in the design — an exceptionally dense flint, likely from the niobate or titanate family. Its focal length is +36.0 mm, making it the strongest single positive element in the rear group. It does the bulk of the rear group's converging work and would be unsuitable for the front group because its very low Abbe number would generate unacceptable axial colour at the large beam diameters there. Placed deep in the rear group where the chief ray is high but the marginal ray is moderate, its aberration generation is dominated by oblique rays — exactly where its high index is most useful.

L13 + L14 (D4) is another cemented doublet (junction at `R = 42.30`, convex to the object). L13 (`R₁ = −93.44`, `R₂ = 42.30`, `t = 1.05`, `n_d = 1.69895`, `ν_d = 30.05`) is a thin biconcave negative paired with the biconvex L14 (`R₁ = 42.30`, `R₂ = −124.29`, `t = 6.93`, `n_d = 2.00100`, `ν_d = 29.13`, the same dense glass as L11). The doublet's net focal length is +128 mm. This is the rear group's secondary chromatic correction stage, working on the residuals left after L10–L12.

L15 (`R₁ = −916.59`, `R₂ = 54.24`, `t = 3.00`, `n_d = 1.73077`, `ν_d = 40.50`) is a biconcave element with both surfaces aspherical. Its base-sphere focal length is −70.0 mm, but the aspherical departures (analysed in detail in section 5) modify the local power away from the optical axis substantially. The patent identifies this element as the aberration-correcting asphere required by claim 7. Its glass — a moderate-high-index moderate-Abbe material around 1.73/40.5 — is consistent with a moldable lanthanum glass suitable for precision aspherical fabrication; this index/Abbe combination is offered by the major catalogue makers (for example as a moldable lanthanum dense crown, with the OHARA L-LAM69 catalogue entry being a close exemplar of the family) but identification by manufacturer cannot be made with certainty without independent confirmation.

## 4. Glass selection strategy

The Example 1 prescription uses nine distinct optical glasses across fifteen elements. Glass selection is the patent's central claim, so this section examines the strategy in detail.

The glass map shows three distinct populations. The high-Abbe cluster (`ν_d > 65`) contains L2/L3 (the SLD glass `1.55032 / 75.50`), L4/L6/L9 (`1.59282 / 68.62`), and the focus element L7 (`1.59349 / 67.00`). These are all SLD-class crown glasses with anomalous partial dispersion (low residual relative to the Abbe-Buchdahl line on the g–F partial-dispersion plot), used to correct primary chromatic aberration without introducing secondary spectrum. Of the five positive elements in front group GA (L1, L2, L3, L4, L6), four — every positive except the LPH itself — fall into this category, and the average `ν_d` of those four is 72.06. This satisfies the patent's condition (4) — `60.0 < V_d_LPL` — comfortably above its tighter recommended bounds at 65 (preferred) and 70 (most preferred).

The high-index cluster on the right (`n_d > 1.85`, `ν_d < 30`) contains the LPH front element L1 (`1.94595 / 17.98`) — among the densest, highest-dispersion conventional glasses in production — and the anomalous-flint cluster around `1.85451 / 25.15` used for L5 (the LN element), L8, and L10. L11 and L14 share `2.00100 / 29.13` (a niobium-rich high-index flint), and L12 sits at the extreme corner with `2.05090 / 26.94`. This cluster realises the patent's condition (1) (`n_d > 1.85`) for the LPH and provides the high-density correcting glasses for the rear group. The `1.85451 / 25.15` glass is reused three times (L5, L8, L10) — a deliberate consolidation that simplifies sourcing and exploits one anomalous-flint melt's `+0.0073` g-line departure across multiple cemented junctions.

The intermediate cluster (L13 at `1.69895 / 30.05` and L15 at `1.73077 / 40.50`) fills specific aberration-correction roles. L13's relatively low Abbe number positions it for fine secondary-colour balancing in its cemented pair, while L15's moderate index and Abbe number provide the substrate for the design's only aspherical surfaces.

The patent's background-art section (¶0006–¶0008) explicitly criticises three precedents: a design using HOYA BSC7 (criticised for inadequate refractive power and weak g–F correction), one using HOYA FCD100 (criticised for inadequate refractive power), and one using HOYA TAFD55 / TAFD45 (criticised for high specific gravity and weak g–F correction). The Example 1 LPH glass at `1.94595 / 17.98` evades all three criticisms simultaneously: high index for converging power, low specific gravity for the index class (typical of flint families that achieve high index through phosphate/niobate compositions rather than dense lanthanum), and high anomalous dispersion that contributes to short-wavelength colour correction rather than fighting it.

Glass identification by manufacturer catalogue is intrinsically uncertain because multiple vendors offer materials in the same `n_d / ν_d` neighbourhood, and the patent itself omits glass names. Identifications by family rather than vendor can be made with confidence. The `1.55032 / 75.50` glass falls in the fluorophosphate-crown SLD family (close in `n_d / ν_d` to glasses such as HOYA FCD705 / OHARA S-FPL55-class materials). The `1.59282 / 68.62` glass is in the dense fluorophosphate-crown / phosphate-crown SLD family. The `1.85451 / 25.15` glass is in the anomalous-flint family characterised by a small positive deviation from the Abbe-Buchdahl normal partial-dispersion line — for L5 specifically, the patent reports `θ_gF = 0.6103`, which lies `+0.0073` above the normal value `0.648285 − 0.00180123 × 25.15 = 0.6030`. That positive deviation is what makes the glass useful as a secondary-spectrum corrector when paired with a high-Abbe positive. The `2.00100 / 29.13` and `2.05090 / 26.94` glasses are in the dense lanthanum-niobate / titanate-flint family typically marketed under TAFD-class designations. Catalogue-vendor identification with greater specificity requires comparing each glass's full partial-dispersion data against vendor catalogues, and in this prescription only L5 carries an explicit `θ_gF` value, so vendor-level identification of the other glasses on the basis of this prescription alone is not warranted. (Sigma's own product description classifies the design's element complement as "five SLD elements and one aspherical lens" with the remainder employing high-refractive-index glass; the five SLD-class elements correspond to the high-Abbe positives L2, L3, L4, L6, and L9, with L7 — also `ν_d > 65` — performing the focusing role and apparently not counted by Sigma in the SLD tally.)

## 5. Aspherical surfaces

Example 1 has exactly two aspherical surfaces — both belong to the rear element L15. The aspherical sag is described by the standard even-asphere equation given in the patent (¶0084):

`z(y) = (1/r) y² / (1 + √(1 − (1+K)(y/r)²)) + A₄y⁴ + A₆y⁶ + A₈y⁸ + A₁₀y¹⁰ + A₁₂y¹² + A₁₄y¹⁴`

The patent specifies `K = 0.00000` for both surfaces, so the conic term reduces to a pure sphere; the entire aspherical departure is carried by the polynomial coefficients A₄ through A₁₄. (The patent uses the standard form in which `K = 0` corresponds to a sphere — directly readable from the equation above by setting `K = 0` and observing the radical collapses to `√(1 − (y/r)²)`, the elementary sphere sag.)

The polynomial coefficients are reproduced directly from the patent table, in scientific notation:

| Coefficient | Surface 26 (L15 front) | Surface 27 (L15 rear) |
|---|---|---|
| K | 0 | 0 |
| A₄ | +2.10225 × 10⁻⁷ | +6.83977 × 10⁻⁶ |
| A₆ | −2.78595 × 10⁻⁸ | −2.78757 × 10⁻⁸ |
| A₈ | +1.49681 × 10⁻¹⁰ | +1.40045 × 10⁻¹⁰ |
| A₁₀ | −4.65535 × 10⁻¹³ | −3.96220 × 10⁻¹³ |
| A₁₂ | +7.39256 × 10⁻¹⁶ | +5.96411 × 10⁻¹⁶ |
| A₁₄ | −4.45971 × 10⁻¹⁹ | −3.74448 × 10⁻¹⁹ |

The two surfaces share strikingly similar A₆ through A₁₄ progressions (sign-alternating, comparable magnitudes) but differ by a factor of ~33 in A₄. This pattern — common A₆+ tail with very different A₄ — is the asphericist's idiom for "match high-order behaviour in both surfaces, then use one surface's A₄ to set the field-dependent low-order departure." The result is a biaspheric pair whose aggregate effect is dominated by the difference in A₄ profile, not the high-order details.

Quantifying the departure from the base sphere requires picking a reference height. Two choices are useful: the **operative semi-diameter** at which the data file's per-surface clear aperture is set (this is what the optical system actually uses, and is bounded by both the on-axis marginal-ray height and the realistic — i.e., vignetted — chief-ray bundle at the corner), and the **unvignetted full-corner extent**, the geometric envelope of the corner light cone before vignetting is applied. The two differ for fast lenses because the corner of the image is heavily vignetted at F/1.4.

| Surface | Operative SD | Δsag at operative SD | Δsag at full-corner extent (≈ 23 mm) |
|---|---|---|---|
| L15 front (26) | 19.53 mm | −353 µm | −599 µm |
| L15 rear (27) | 18.74 mm | +522 µm | +1070 µm |

*Table 2 — L15 aspheric departures from the base sphere. The "operative SD" column corresponds to the clear-aperture values stored in the data file (combined marginal+chief envelope at offAxisFieldFrac = 0.60, +8 % mechanical clearance, then trimmed for edge thickness and gap-overlap rules). The right column is the geometric upper bound that the corner ray bundle would touch in the absence of vignetting.*

Whichever rim convention is used, the qualitative story is the same. Surface 27 (the rear of L15) does most of the asphericising; its leading coefficient `A₄ = +6.84 × 10⁻⁶` is roughly thirty times larger in magnitude than surface 26's (`A₄ = +2.10 × 10⁻⁷`). The surface-26 departure goes negative (the asphere lies inside the base sphere) while the surface-27 departure is positive (the asphere lies outside the base sphere). This pairing is intentional: it preserves the element's net axial power close to the −70 mm base-sphere value while reshaping the off-axis power distribution. The local rate of change of the departure at the operative rim is moderate — about −4.1° at the front surface's clear-aperture rim (`h = 19.53 mm`) and +6.0° at the rear surface's clear-aperture rim (`h = 18.74 mm`) — well within the manufacturable range for a glass-molded asphere.

The patent's claim 7 (with its supporting explanation in ¶0068–¶0069) states that when GB carries an aspherical surface, coma and astigmatism are suppressed. This is consistent with what is observed numerically: L15 sits at the back of the rear group where the chief-ray height is at its largest fraction of the marginal-ray height, so any non-axial aberration generated by the powerful rear-group elements (especially the dense-flint L12 and L14) sees its largest correcting lever arm on L15. The biconcave geometry combined with positive A₄ on the rear surface and small-magnitude positive A₄ on the front yields a position-dependent surface profile that allows the off-axis aberration generation of L15 to be tuned independently of its on-axis power. In effect, the asphericisation acts as a field-dependent corrector targeting the field-curvature and astigmatism residuals of the high-index rear group.

The element pulls double duty: the same biaspheric L15 also flattens the field. The whole-system Petzval sum (using the surface-by-surface formula `P = Σ φᵢ / (nᵢ · n′ᵢ)`) is `P = +0.001457 mm⁻¹`, equivalent to a Petzval radius of +686 mm. The product `P · EFL = +0.121` is moderate for an F/1.4 design and reflects active flat-field correction; the largest individual Petzval contributions come from steeply curved glass-to-air surfaces (L5's image-side surface at `R = 25.76 mm`, `Pᵢ = −0.0179`, and L7's rear at `R = 31.73 mm`, `Pᵢ = −0.0117`), which are the dedicated field-flattening surfaces of the design.

## 6. Focusing mechanism

Example 1 implements the patent's claim 8: the negative lens component LF, adjacent to the aperture stop, moves during focusing. Group G2 in this design consists of the single element L7 — there is no other element in G2 — and L7 alone is the focus group. ¶0070 of the patent identifies Numerical Examples 1 and 4–8 specifically as the embodiments implementing this claim, and ¶0071 makes the dual rationale explicit: a single negative lens component moving in this position simultaneously suppresses focus-state-dependent variation in astigmatism and other aberrations *and* keeps the moving mass low. Sigma's published technical specifications describe the lens as inner-focus with a stepping motor, both of which are consistent with this minimal single-element focus mechanism.

The patent's variable-interval data for Example 1 (infinity vs. 850 mm states) gives `d12 = 2.7882 → 12.3908` mm (the gap between the stop and L7's front) and `d14 = 13.3900 → 3.7874` mm (the gap between L7's rear and L8's front). The sums are identical to four decimal places (16.1782 mm in both states), and the difference `12.3908 − 2.7882 = 9.6026` matches `13.3900 − 3.7874 = 9.6026`, so L7 is rigidly translated rather than independently displaced relative to its neighbours. The back-focal distance `BF = 24.0375` mm is held constant across focus states, as expected for an inner-focus design where the image surface is fixed.

A subtle aspect of this focus design is that the focus element's negative power is close to the front group's positive power in magnitude (`f_GA = +73.30 mm` vs. `f_LF = −61.70 mm`). This near-balance means small displacements of L7 produce relatively large changes in the system focal length and conjugate distance — precisely what is wanted for fine focus control without large mechanical excursions. The patent's condition (5), `0.20 < f / f_A < 2.50` with verified value `f / f_A = 1.13`, places this design well inside the recommended bounds.

Choosing a single negative meniscus as the entire focus group has several practical advantages. The element is light (a 0.9 mm thick meniscus of `ν_d ≈ 67` glass), reducing actuator load for fast and quiet stepping-motor operation. Because L7 sits immediately behind the aperture stop, the marginal-ray height on it is small relative to the front group, so vignetting at close focus is intrinsically limited by the stop diameter rather than by L7's clear aperture. The patent text (¶0046) makes this point explicitly for the broader class of designs covered by the patent: keeping the front element fixed, with focusing performed by an interior element, allows the moving element to be one with smaller maximum ray height and therefore smaller mass.

The geometric verification of the focus throw is straightforward. At the close-focus state the patent specifies object distance `d0 = 740.6522` mm from the object plane to the first lens surface. Adding the total lens length (109.35 mm) gives a subject-to-image distance of `850.00` mm, matching both the patent's stated condition and Sigma's published 0.85 m minimum-focus specification. Sigma's global product page reports a maximum-magnification ratio of `1:8.4` (≈ 0.119×) for the production lens; the patent does not directly tabulate the magnification, but the close-focus focal-length contraction from 82.96 mm to 80.67 mm visible in the various-data table is consistent with a magnification in this range.

| (5) `0.20 < f / f_A < 2.50` | both | 1.13 | 1.132 |
| (6) `−1.00 < f / f_B < 2.00` | both | 0.52 | 0.525 |
| (7) `V_d_LN < 30.0` | upper | 25.2 | 25.15 (L5) |

*Table 3 — Patent conditional expressions evaluated for Example 1.*

Each expression has a clear physical meaning. Conditions (1) and (2) demand that the LPH glass be both high-index (for converging power without large surface curvatures) and high-dispersion (for subsequent anomalous-dispersion compensation to actually have something to balance). Condition (3) demands that the LPH be in the front portion of GA — placing it specifically where the beam diameter is large so its converging effect is most useful for downsizing the rest of the system. Condition (4) demands that the *non*-LPH positives in GA be high-Abbe so they can chromatically counter the LPH's strong dispersion. Condition (5) bounds the front-group power between two failure modes (GA too strong → in-group spherical aberration becomes uncorrectable; GA too weak → ray bundle at the stop swells, dragging the rear-group diameter up with it); condition (6) bounds the rear-group power between an analogous pair (GB too strongly positive → GA's positive contribution falls and the lens grows long; GB too strongly negative → adequate back focus cannot be maintained without enlarging the stop diameter). Conditions (7) and (8) define the LN glass's required anomalous partial-dispersion characteristic — specifically, that its `θ_gF` lies above the Abbe-Buchdahl normal line by no more than 0.010, with the small positive deviation providing the third lever needed for g-line secondary-spectrum cancellation in concert with the LPH and the high-Abbe positives.

The condition values match the patent table to the published precision, confirming that the prescription transcribed from the patent PDF is consistent and that the paraxial verification captures the same quantities the patent author computed.

Beyond the basic conditional bounds, the patent specifies tighter "preferred" and "more preferred" sub-ranges for each condition (¶0032, ¶0036, ¶0040, ¶0044, ¶0052, ¶0055, ¶0059, ¶0063). Example 1 satisfies even the most-preferred ranges for every condition: condition (1) most-preferred lower bound 1.91 (Example 1: 1.946); condition (2) most-preferred upper bound 26.0 (17.98); condition (3) most-preferred upper bound 0.25 (0.00); condition (4) most-preferred lower bound 70.0 (72.06); condition (5) tighter bounds 0.40–2.00 (1.13); condition (6) tighter bounds −0.50–1.50 (0.52); condition (7) most-preferred upper bound 26.0 (25.15); condition (8) most-preferred upper bound 0.009 (0.0073). Example 1 is therefore not just a generic embodiment of the patent's invention but one that lies comfortably in the inner core of the patent's preferred parameter space — consistent with this being the embodiment selected for production.

## 8. Aberration-correction strategy

The Sigma 85 mm F/1.4 DG DN | Art's optical design philosophy can be summarised as a deliberate concentration of correction effort: heavy chromatic correction in the front group, heavy off-axis correction in the rear group, and minimal compromise on either by avoiding any one element having to do too much.

Axial chromatic aberration is the dominant residual at F/1.4, and its correction is the patent's claimed novelty. The strategy uses the LPH front element to begin converging the beam aggressively at the cost of large g–F dispersion, then balances that dispersion in three stages: the high-Abbe positives L2/L3/L4/L6 cancel primary colour, the cemented L4+L5 doublet (with L5 being the anomalous-flint LN element) cancels secondary spectrum at the g-line specifically, and the rear-group cemented doublets L8+L9 and L13+L14 mop up residuals that survive the front group. The patent's condition (4) — that the average `ν_d` of the non-LPH positives in GA exceeds 60, with 72.1 actually achieved — is a quantitative measure of how aggressively the design commits to anomalous-dispersion correction.

Spherical aberration correction relies on the front group's mixed positive-negative-positive power distribution: L1 (positive) → L2 (positive) → L3 (positive) → L4 (positive) + L5 (negative) → L6 (positive) generates and cancels spherical aberration in alternation, with the cemented L4+L5 junction providing the strongest single negative contribution. The rear group continues this pattern with L8 (negative) + L9 (positive), L10 (negative) + L11 (positive), L12 (positive), L13 (negative) + L14 (positive), and finally L15 (negative). This kind of saw-tooth power distribution — alternating positive and negative powers along the optical axis — is essential for maintaining F/1.4 spherical correction in a fast portrait lens and is what allows each individual surface to operate within its own paraxial-only regime rather than accumulating large higher-order residuals.

Field curvature is moderately corrected: the Petzval sum of `+0.001457 mm⁻¹` corresponds to a field-curving residual that the rear-group aspherical L15 reshapes into a near-flat image surface at the relevant field heights. The largest individual Petzval contributors are steeply curved glass-to-air surfaces of the strong elements: L5's image-side rear surface (`R = 25.76` mm, `Pᵢ = −0.0179`), L8's front surface (`R = 36.89` mm, `Pᵢ = +0.0125`), L7's rear surface (`R = 31.73` mm, `Pᵢ = −0.0117`), and L4's front surface (`R = 34.38` mm, `Pᵢ = +0.0108`). The cancellation between adjacent positive and negative Petzval contributions is what keeps the net sum manageable.

Coma and astigmatism are addressed primarily by the rear group's L15 biaspheric element (per the patent's claim 7). The asphericisation of both L15 surfaces with opposite-sign net effect provides position-dependent power that compensates the field-dependent residuals generated by the high-index elements L11 and L14 sitting just before it.

Lateral colour is addressed primarily within the rear group's cemented L13+L14 doublet, where the high-index positive L14 (`2.00100 / 29.13`) paired with the moderate-Abbe negative L13 (`1.69895 / 30.05`) corrects chief-ray colour at intermediate field heights. Distortion in this kind of fast asymmetric portrait layout is the residual the design accepts in exchange for prioritising sharpness, chromatic correction, and compactness; the patent's distortion plot (figure 2, Example 1, infinity) shows approximately +4 % pincushion at the corner, consistent with the gap between the patent's actual chief-ray angle 2ω = 28.12° and the undistorted-paraxial prediction 29.24° at the design focal length. The production lens carries this residual into its software correction profile — Sigma's product description explicitly notes that the optical design is intended to work with the camera-side lens-aberration-correction function, allowing the optical system to concentrate on aberrations (axial chromatic in particular) that cannot be corrected after capture.

## 9. Verification summary

All numerical claims in this analysis were independently verified by paraxial ray trace from the patent prescription. The whole-system effective focal length was reproduced as 82.9607 mm (patent: 82.96), the back focal distance as 24.0386 mm (patent: 24.0375), and the three group focal lengths to within 0.005 mm of the patent-stated values. The total length sum of `d`-values (109.348 mm) reproduces the patent's stated total length (109.35 mm) to two decimal places. The eight conditional expressions were all reproduced to the published precision (Table 3). The variable-airspace conservation across focus states holds to four decimal places, confirming the rigid-body translation of L7 by +9.6026 mm toward the image plane. The Petzval sum was computed surface-by-surface using the standard formula `P = Σ φᵢ / (nᵢ · n′ᵢ)`, and aspherical departures were computed directly from the patent's `K, A₄, … A₁₄` coefficients at both the operative SDs used in the data file and at the geometric full-corner extent.

The standalone thick-lens-in-air focal lengths of every element and every cemented doublet were independently computed and match the values cited inline in section 3 to better than 0.5 % in every case.

Two notes on conventions: First, the conic-constant convention used in this patent is the standard form in which `K = 0` corresponds to a sphere; this can be read directly from the sag equation given in ¶0084 (with `K = 0`, the radical reduces to `√(1 − (y/r)²)` and the formula is the elementary sag of a sphere of radius `r`). Both aspherical surfaces of L15 carry `K = 0`, so the entire aspherical departure is supplied by the polynomial coefficients A₄ through A₁₄. Second, glass identifications by family are made conservatively: the SLD-class designations are warranted by the position of those glasses on the Abbe diagram, but specific catalogue-vendor identifications cannot be made without comparing partial-dispersion data that the patent only partially provides (the patent tabulates `θ_gF` only for L5 — and for the analogous front elements of Examples 4, 5, 6, and 8, never for any other element of any embodiment).

The analysis is consistent with Sigma's published specifications for the production 85 mm F/1.4 DG DN | Art (15 elements in 11 groups; one aspherical element; minimum focus 0.85 m; F/1.4 maximum aperture; stepping-motor inner-focus mechanism; 77 mm filter thread). Where the patent design value differs slightly from production specification — most notably the design F-number of 1.46 versus marketed F/1.4 — these are the expected differences between an optimised numerical embodiment and a marketing-rounded production specification. The data file (`SigmaDGDNA85mmf14.data.ts`) follows the project convention of using the manufacturer-marketed F-number (F/1.4) for `nominalFno` while preserving the patent design F-number (F/1.46) in `apertureDesign`.
