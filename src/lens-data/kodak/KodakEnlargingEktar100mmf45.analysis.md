## Patent Reference and Design Identification

**Patent:** US 2,279,384
**Application Number:** US 373,785
**Filed:** January 9, 1941
**Granted:** April 14, 1942
**Inventor:** Fred E. Altman
**Assignee:** Eastman Kodak Company, Rochester, N.Y.
**Title:** Photographic Objective
**Embodiment analyzed:** Example 6

Example 6 is a five-element, three-group modified triplet objective. The patent gives the example as a 100 mm f/4.5 design with 26° covering power, where the patent defines covering power as the half angle of the covered field. The worked example is not named as a production Kodak Enlarging Ektar in the patent itself. The identification as the Kodak Enlarging Ektar 100mm f/4.5 is therefore a production cross-identification, not a literal patent label.

The match rests on convergent evidence. The patent example is exactly f/4.5 at f = 100 mm, it uses the five-element 2-1-2 modified-triplet form associated with the Kodak f/4.5 enlarging Ektar family, and the patent text states that Example 6 is deliberately adjusted for finite-conjugate use in an enlarger. A Kodak enlarging-equipment brochure lists a Kodak Enlarging Ektar Lens, 4-inch f/4.5, for negatives up to approximately 2¼ × 3¼ inches, and describes the Enlarging Ektar lenses as specially color-corrected for close working distances in enlarging and copying. The optical match is strong enough to use Example 6 as the representative prescription for the 100 mm / 4-inch f/4.5 Enlarging Ektar, but it should not be read as proof that every production barrel carried this exact patent prescription.

The patent includes a certificate of correction dated June 30, 1942. The corrections affect several typographical errors in the printed specification but do not alter the numerical prescription of Example 6.

## Optical Architecture

The design is a Heliar-type modified triplet: a positive cemented front doublet, a single negative central element, and a positive cemented rear doublet. In power order the architecture is positive-negative-positive across three air-spaced groups.

The computed standalone group focal lengths are +48.03 mm for the front doublet, -32.59 mm for the central negative element, and +61.22 mm for the rear doublet. The front positive group is therefore stronger than the rear positive group, while the central negative element supplies the principal Petzval correction and chromatic counter-power.

At the patent scale, the computed paraxial effective focal length is 100.36 mm. The axial distance from the first to the last refracting surface is 28.08 mm, and the computed rear-vertex back focal distance is 82.13 mm. The front-vertex-to-image total track at infinity is therefore 110.21 mm. This is not a telephoto design: the total track is slightly longer than the effective focal length, and the back focal distance is shorter than the effective focal length.

All eight refracting surfaces are spherical. The patent does not tabulate a diaphragm position. The data file places the stop at the midpoint of the first internal air gap, between the front doublet and the central negative element. This is an inferred renderer/modeling choice: it yields f/4.5 with a 9.357 mm physical stop semi-diameter and allows the 26° half-field to pass with physically plausible clear apertures. A stop placed in the larger rear-side air gap would require a substantially larger front group for the same field and is not used in the delivered prescription.

## Element-by-Element Analysis

### L1 — Biconvex Positive

nd = 1.611, νd = 57.2. Glass: Unmatched vintage high-index crown class, 611/572. f = +26.68 mm.

L1 is the main positive front element. Its surfaces R1 = +28.3 mm and R2 = -34.3 mm make it a thick biconvex crown element, and at 8.06 mm center thickness it is the thickest element in the design. Surface R1 supplies the strongest positive first-surface refraction in the lens, while cemented surface R2 is part of the front doublet's inward-convex correction surface.

The patent's preferred embodiments require the front positive element to have an index at least 0.025 greater than its cemented negative partner. In Example 6 the index difference is 1.611 - 1.573 = 0.038, satisfying that condition.

### L2 — Biconcave Negative, weak rear surface

nd = 1.573, νd = 57.5. Glass: Unmatched vintage crown class, 573/575. f = -53.19 mm.

L2 is the negative element cemented behind L1. The rear surface R3 = +280.1 mm is optically weak, so the element is nearly plano-concave in function even though both radii are finite. The element's principal job is not classical crown/flint achromatization, since its Abbe number is almost identical to that of L1.

The front cemented surface R2 is concave to the front and convex toward the central negative component. Altman's patent uses this inward-convex cemented surface to correct lower rim-ray disagreement in oblique pencils. Because L1 and L2 have νd = 57.2 and 57.5, the front cemented surface has little chromatic leverage; it is primarily a monochromatic aberration-correction surface.

### L3 — Biconcave Negative

nd = 1.605, νd = 38.2. Glass: Unmatched vintage dense-flint class, 605/382. f = -32.59 mm.

L3 is the single central negative component of the modified triplet. It is the strongest individual negative element in the design. Its front surface R4 = -72.7 mm is weaker than its rear surface R5 = +27.4 mm, concentrating much of the negative power toward the rear side of the element.

The patent identifies the high-index single negative component as a preferred feature for zonal spherical-aberration control. Example 6 satisfies that condition with nd = 1.605, above the patent's nd > 1.58 threshold for the negative component.

### L4 — Negative Meniscus

nd = 1.575, νd = 41.4. Glass: Unmatched vintage light-flint class, 575/414. f = -78.53 mm.

L4 is the negative member of the rear cemented doublet. Its front surface R6 = +400.9 mm is very weak, so the element's optical action is concentrated mostly at the cemented junction R7 = +40.5 mm.

This element is the critical change that distinguishes Example 6 from the neighboring f/4.5 examples. Examples 4 and 5 use a stronger-dispersion 1.605 / 38.2 glass in the corresponding rear negative element. Example 6 instead uses 1.575 / 41.4, reducing the rear doublet's achromatizing contribution. The result is the patent's stated slight undercorrection of longitudinal color at infinity, chosen to improve color correction at finite enlarging conjugates.

### L5 — Biconvex Positive

nd = 1.744, νd = 45.8. Glass: Unmatched vintage high-index flint class, 744/458. f = +34.83 mm.

L5 is the positive rear element and the highest-index glass in the objective. Its high index is not incidental: the patent states that astigmatism can be greatly reduced by using a glass with nd greater than 1.7 in the rear positive element. Example 6 satisfies this with nd = 1.744.

The cemented surface R7 is convex to the front and convex inward toward the central negative element. It supplies the rear doublet's upper rim-ray correction. The index step at this cemented surface is 1.744 - 1.575 = 0.169, much larger than the patent's required minimum of 0.025.

## Glass Identification and Selection

The patent publishes only D-line refractive indices and Abbe numbers. It does not name Schott, Bausch & Lomb, Kodak, or other melt/catalog designations. Because the design dates from 1941 and likely used period American glass production, the delivered data file does not assign modern catalog names. Each element is therefore marked as an unmatched vintage glass class using its six-digit nd/νd code.

| Element | nd | νd | Code | Delivered glass label | Role |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|---|
| L1 | 1.611 | 57.2 | 611/572 | Unmatched vintage high-index crown class | Front positive crown |
| L2 | 1.573 | 57.5 | 573/575 | Unmatched vintage crown class | Front doublet negative partner |
| L3 | 1.605 | 38.2 | 605/382 | Unmatched vintage dense-flint class | Central negative component |
| L4 | 1.575 | 41.4 | 575/414 | Unmatched vintage light-flint class | Rear doublet negative partner |
| L5 | 1.744 | 45.8 | 744/458 | Unmatched vintage high-index flint class | Rear positive high-index element |

The chromatic strategy is asymmetric. The front doublet uses nearly matched Abbe numbers and therefore uses its cemented surface primarily for rim-ray correction rather than color correction. The central negative element provides the strongest flint dispersion in the system. The rear doublet then supplies the remaining chromatic balance, but in Example 6 L4 is deliberately less dispersive than the corresponding element in the sibling examples. That substitution leaves the slight infinity-conjugate longitudinal color undercorrection identified by the patent.

## Focus Mechanism

The optical prescription is a unit-focus design. There are no internal focus groups and no variable internal spacings. In enlarger use, focusing is achieved by translating the complete lens relative to the negative and paper plane through the enlarger's bellows or lens-stage mechanism.

The data file uses the infinity prescription as the base state and adds a representative finite-conjugate state for a 3× enlargement. This is not a manufacturer-published minimum focus distance; it is a practical visualization state consistent with the patent's finite-conjugate enlarger purpose.

## Chromatic Correction Strategy

Example 6 is explicitly a finite-conjugate variant. In a normal achromatized cemented doublet, the cemented surface often performs a large share of the color correction. Altman's front doublet does not work that way: L1 and L2 have nearly identical dispersive indices, so the front cemented surface is reserved for the patent's rim-ray correction mechanism.

The central negative flint and rear doublet carry most of the color balancing. In Example 6 the rear negative element is changed from the 1.605 / 38.2 glass used in the adjacent examples to 1.575 / 41.4. The lower dispersion reduces the rear cemented surface's color power. The design is therefore not simply an infinity-corrected photographic objective repurposed for printing; it is a member of the same modified-triplet family deliberately biased for enlarger conjugates.

## Conditional Expressions

The patent defines the front cemented surface by the Petzval power of R2 multiplied by the overall axial length Δ:

$$
P_2\Delta = \frac{(N_{II}-N_I)\Delta}{N_I N_{II} R_2}
$$

For Example 6, using N_I = 1.611, N_II = 1.573, R2 = -34.3 mm, and Δ = 28.08 mm gives P2Δ = 0.012276. The patent table gives 0.0124. The value lies within the patent's preferred 0.008-0.013 interval.

The rear cemented surface is governed by the corresponding expression at R7:

$$
P_7\Delta = \frac{(N_V-N_{IV})\Delta}{N_{IV} N_V R_7}
$$

Using N_IV = 1.575, N_V = 1.744, R7 = +40.5 mm, and Δ = 28.08 mm gives P7Δ = 0.042658. The patent table gives 0.0426. This is within the broad 0.025-0.075 claimed interval, though it is slightly above the narrower 0.027-0.040 preferred range stated for the Δ form.

The patent also gives an alternate rear expression using Δ - t5:

$$
P_7(\Delta-t_5) = \frac{(N_V-N_{IV})(\Delta-t_5)}{N_{IV} N_V R_7}
$$

With t5 = 5.19 mm, this gives 0.034774. The patent table gives 0.0347, and the value lies inside the 0.020-0.035 alternate interval.

The surface-by-surface Petzval sum is 0.002300, compared with the patent's tabulated 0.0022. The corresponding Petzval radius is about 434.9 mm. This value was computed from the surface formula Σφ/(n·n′), not from a thin-element approximation.

The prescription was independently re-entered from the Example 6 table and verified by paraxial y-nu ray trace and ABCD matrix calculation. Both methods agree.

Standalone in-air component focal lengths were also recomputed. L1 = +26.68 mm, L2 = -53.19 mm, L3 = -32.59 mm, L4 = -78.53 mm, L5 = +34.83 mm. The cemented front doublet is +48.03 mm and the cemented rear doublet is +61.22 mm. These are standalone component focal lengths, not in-situ contributions inside the complete lens.

The patent omits clear apertures. The data file's semi-diameters are inferred values. They pass the 26° half-field at f/4.5, maintain sd/|R| below the renderer limit, preserve front/rear element semi-diameter ratios below 1.25 for the cemented groups, and keep cross-gap sag intrusion below 90% of each relevant air gap.
