## Patent Reference and Design Identification

**Patent:** US 4,170,403
**Application Number:** US 848,016
**Filed:** November 2, 1977
**Granted:** October 9, 1979
**Priority:** JP 51-134561, November 9, 1976
**Inventor:** Toshiko Shimokura
**Assignee:** Konishiroku Photo Industry Co., Ltd., Tokyo, Japan
**Title:** Retro Focus Type Wide Angle Lens
**Embodiment analyzed:** Example 1

US 4,170,403 describes a compact five-group retrofocus wide-angle lens. Example 1 is the embodiment used here. The patent normalizes the prescription to $f = 1$ and lists $F/2.8$, $2\omega = 64°$, and $f_B = 1.116$. The associated aberration diagram for Example 1 labels the maximum image height as $Y = 0.6094$; at the 35 mm scale used in the data file, this is 21.329 mm, consistent with the 135-format diagonal field after normal patent rounding.

The identification with the late Konica Hexanon AR 35mm f/2.8 rests on convergent evidence rather than on an explicit model name in the patent:

1. **Optical layout.** Example 1 is a 5-element, 5-group all-spherical retrofocus design. Secondary Konica lens references document the late f/22 Hexanon AR 35mm f/2.8 as 5 elements in 5 groups, while earlier versions used 6 elements in 5 groups.
2. **Focal length class.** Scaling the normalized patent example by 35 gives an independently computed effective focal length of 35.0008 mm. The patent field of $2\omega = 64°$ agrees with a 35 mm lens on the 135 full-frame diagonal within normal rounding.
3. **Aperture.** Example 1 is $F/2.8$, matching the production maximum aperture.
4. **Back focus.** The computed back focal distance is $1.11606f$, which is consistent with an SLR retrofocus wide-angle design. It exceeds the focal length, so the design is retrofocus in the strict optical sense.
5. **Timing.** The patent was filed in 1977 and granted in 1979. The compact 5/5 late production version is documented as the later f/22 variant.

Examples 2 and 3 have $2\omega = 75°$ and therefore belong to the wider 28 mm class. Example 1 is the only worked example in the patent matching a 35 mm f/2.8 design.

## Optical Architecture

The design is a five-element, five-group retrofocus wide-angle lens for 135-format SLR use. The group power sequence is negative-positive-negative-positive-positive.

L1 is a negative meniscus with its convex surface facing the object. It supplies the front negative power that increases back focus. L2 is a thick biconvex positive lens; the patent treats this axial thickness as part of the aberration-correction mechanism rather than merely as a mechanical consequence. L3 is a biconcave negative lens directly behind the diaphragm region. L4 and L5 are both positive menisci with their concave surfaces toward the object. The patent identifies these two rear concave surfaces as the main correction mechanism for the negative distortion generated by the front negative lens.

The diaphragm is not a separate refracting element. The patent states that the diaphragm is positioned 0.07 focal-length units behind the fourth lens surface. In the data file, the L2-L3 air space is therefore split into the surface 4 to stop distance and the stop to surface 5 distance.

The design has no cemented components and no aspherical surfaces.

## Element-by-Element Analysis

All focal lengths below are standalone in-air element focal lengths at the 35 mm production scale. They are not in-situ group powers.

### L1 — Negative Meniscus, Convex to Object

$n_d = 1.5163$, $\nu_d = 64.1$. Glass: S-BSL7 (OHARA). $f = -40.3$ mm.

L1 has two positive radii and is a negative meniscus because the rear surface is much more strongly curved than the front. Its role is to give the system the front negative power needed for a back focal distance longer than the effective focal length. That front negative power also produces the coma and distortion terms that the rear meniscus pair is designed to counterbalance.

The glass is a low-index borosilicate crown. Its high Abbe number keeps the chromatic contribution of this strong negative front lens moderate.

### L2 — Biconvex Positive

$n_d = 1.7570$, $\nu_d = 47.9$. Glass: J-LAF04 (HIKARI), high-index lanthanum flint class. $f = +24.1$ mm.

L2 is the strongest positive component in the lens. It has a long center thickness of $0.3386f$ in the patent prescription, or 11.85 mm when scaled to a 35 mm focal length. This is not incidental: the patent explains that, instead of enlarging the L2-L3 air gap excessively, the design relies on the converging effect of L2's thickness to help correct the negative coma and distortion produced by L1.

The prior draft treated this glass as unmatched. Rechecking against manufacturer catalog data shows an exact six-digit-code match to HIKARI J-LAF04: code 757479, $n_d = 1.757000$, $\nu_d = 47.86$. Because $\nu_d$ is below 50, the glass is better described as a lanthanum flint or LAF-class glass, not as a crown.

### L3 — Biconcave Negative

$n_d = 1.7407$, $\nu_d = 27.8$. Glass: S-TIH13 (OHARA). $f = -19.4$ mm.

L3 is a dense high-dispersion biconcave element behind the diaphragm. It is the principal negative chromatic partner to L2. The patent's condition (2) constrains L3 thickness, not only for mechanical strength but also because a relatively thick L3 pushes the beam toward the periphery before it reaches the two rear positive menisci.

Its glass matches OHARA S-TIH13 very closely. This is a titanium dense flint glass with high dispersion, appropriate for the negative element in the separated L2/L3 achromatizing pair.

### L4 — Positive Meniscus, Concave to Object

$n_d = 1.6968$, $\nu_d = 55.5$. Glass: S-LAL14 (OHARA). $f = +64.3$ mm.

L4 is the first rear positive meniscus. Both surfaces have negative radii, making the object-side surface concave. The patent emphasizes this geometry because the concave front surfaces of L4 and L5 apply a diverging action to off-axis bundles before the rear surfaces reconverge them. This is the mechanism used to reduce the negative distortion introduced by L1.

S-LAL14 supplies high refractive index with moderate dispersion. Together with L5, it also satisfies the rear-index condition in the claim.

### L5 — Positive Meniscus, Concave to Object

$n_d = 1.7130$, $\nu_d = 53.9$. Glass: S-LAL8 (OHARA). $f = +32.4$ mm.

L5 is the stronger of the two rear positive menisci. Its object-side radius is very weak compared with the image-side radius, so most of its standalone power comes from the rear surface. It completes the rear positive power needed to form the image while maintaining the concave-to-object meniscus form required by the patent's distortion-correction strategy.

S-LAL8 is another high-index lanthanum crown. The average rear-pair refractive index is $(1.6968 + 1.7130)/2 = 1.7049$, which falls inside the patent's claimed range.

The design uses one borosilicate crown, one high-index lanthanum flint, one dense flint, and two lanthanum crowns.

L1, L3, L4, and L5 are close OHARA matches. L2 is not unmatched: it is a direct match to HIKARI J-LAF04, with only patent rounding separating $\nu_d = 47.9$ from the catalog value of 47.86. The glass-selection correction changes the interpretation of the design. L2 should be treated as a high-index LAF-class flint, paired with the much higher-dispersion L3 across the stop.

## Focus Mechanism

The patent publishes only the infinity-focus prescription. It gives no close-focus spacing table, no floating group, and no magnification data. The production lens is a manual-focus unit-focus lens by external behavior, so the data file models close focus by translating the complete optical unit relative to the image plane. Internally, all lens-element separations remain fixed; only the final back-focus gap varies.

The production minimum focus distance is documented as 0.30 m. Solving the finite-conjugate paraxial focus condition for a 300 mm object distance measured from the image plane gives the following model values:

| Focus state | Back focal distance | Back focal distance / EFL | Paraxial magnification |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Infinity | 39.062 mm | 1.1161 | 0 |
| 0.30 m | 44.886 mm | 1.2824 | -0.166 |

The close-focus magnification is therefore about 1:6.0 in this paraxial unit-focus model. This replaces the prior thin-lens estimate of approximately 1:7.6, which did not account for the principal-plane placement of the retrofocus system.

## Conditional Expressions

The patent's description gives broad design ranges, and the claim gives narrower ranges for the same basic quantities. All values below use the patent's normalized $f = 1$ units.

| Condition | Expression | Description range | Claim range | Example 1 value | Status |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| (1) | $(n_4 + n_5)/2$ | $1.60 < \cdot < 1.85$ | $1.69 < \cdot < 1.72$ | 1.7049 | Pass |
| (2) | $d_5/f$ | $0.05 < \cdot < 0.09$ | $0.06 < \cdot < 0.08$ | 0.0705 | Pass |
| (3) | $d_4/f$ | $0.13 < \cdot < 0.19$ | $0.14 < \cdot < 0.18$ | 0.1467 | Pass |
| (4) | $d_3/f$ | $0.15 < \cdot < 0.40$ | $0.20 < \cdot < 0.34$ | 0.3386 | Pass, close to upper claim limit |

Condition (1) controls the refractive index of the rear meniscus pair. Condition (2) controls L3 thickness. Condition (3) limits the air space between L2 and L3 so that the stop can be inserted without relying on an excessive air gap. Condition (4) controls L2 thickness, which the patent uses as a correction mechanism.

## Verification Summary

Independent paraxial verification was run with a $y,n u$ ray trace and cross-checked by ABCD matrix multiplication. The surface-by-surface Petzval calculation used $\phi/(n n')$ for each refracting surface.

|---|---:|---:|
| Effective focal length, normalized | 1.0000 | 1.000023 |
| Effective focal length, scaled | 35 mm nominal | 35.0008 mm |
| Back focal distance, normalized | 1.116 | 1.116056 |
| Back focal distance, scaled | 39.06 mm | 39.062 mm |
| Petzval sum | 0.2097 | 0.20985 |
| First-to-last-surface track, scaled | — | 36.3545 mm |
| Front vertex to image plane, scaled | — | 75.4165 mm |
| Fig. 2 maximum image height, scaled | $Y = 0.6094f$ | 21.329 mm |

The patent's Seidel table lists Petzval sum $P = 0.2097$. The surface-by-surface value of 0.20985 differs by only rounding of the patent prescription.

Semi-diameters in the data file are inferred because the patent does not publish clear apertures. The stop semi-diameter is computed as 6.734 mm from the entrance-pupil requirement for $F/2.8$. Other semi-diameters are conservative rendering apertures constrained by the rim-slope and cross-gap sag limits; they should not be read as measured mechanical diameters of the production lens.

## Design Heritage and Context

The 35mm f/2.8 Hexanon existed in earlier six-element versions before the compact late f/22 variant. The patent example aligns with the late five-element design because of the 5/5 construction, aperture, focal-length class, field angle, and timing. Secondary Konica references document the last f/22 version as smaller and lighter than the earlier six-element version, with a 38 mm length, 200 g weight, 55 mm filter thread, and 0.30 m minimum focus.

The patent's technical contribution is not merely reducing the element count. It relocates much of the coma and distortion correction burden to the two rear object-concave positive menisci, while keeping the L2-L3 air gap small enough to avoid the usual Petzval and front-diameter penalties of a wide separation.
