# Nikon Reflex-Nikkor 500mm f/8 (New)

## Patent Reference and Design Identification

**Patent:** US 4,666,259 A — "Catadioptric Telephoto Lens"  
**Application Number:** US 06/705,721  
**Priority:** 1 March 1984 (Japanese application 59-39268)  
**Filed:** 26 February 1985  
**Granted:** 19 May 1987  
**Inventor:** Yutaka Iizuka  
**Assignee:** Nippon Kogaku K.K.  
**Embodiment analyzed:** First embodiment, Figure 1 / Table 1 / claim 3; f = 499.482 mm, geometric F-number = 6.7, image angle 2ω = 5°

The prescription transcribed here is the first of the patent's two numerical embodiments. It is the better match to the production Reflex-Nikkor 500mm f/8 (New) for four convergent reasons.

1. The first embodiment is a six-element, six-group formula: a biconvex front lens, two air-separated Mangin mirror elements, and three single rear corrector elements. The second embodiment places a cemented correcting assembly on the primary mirror and no longer matches the published 6/6 construction.

2. The first embodiment has a central aperture in the primary mirror, with the rear corrector group located inside that aperture. Nikon's own design history distinguishes the unperforated-mirror solution from the bored-primary solution and states that the bored-primary arrangement was selected for the production lens.

3. The patent gives a close-focus object distance of 1500 mm, approximately three times the focal length, with 8.05 mm of integral front-unit travel. The production lens specification is 1.5 m close focus and approximately 1:2.5 maximum reproduction.

4. The focal length and field match the production lens directly: f = 499.482 mm and 2ω = 5° for the full 135-format diagonal.

A note on attribution is necessary. The patent names Yutaka Iizuka as inventor, while Nikon's historical essay credits the production optical design to Teruyoshi Tsunashima. Those are different categories: patent inventorship of record and later designer attribution need not be identical. The optical prescription nevertheless matches the first patent embodiment and Nikon's described selected architecture.

The numerical table was re-read from the patent page image rather than from OCR alone. The surface 8-to-9 spacing in Table 1 is 41.50 mm; OCR commonly drops the leading digit and reads it as 4.50 mm. The 41.50 mm value is required to reproduce both the patent focal length and the stated 142.716 mm total length.

## Optical Architecture

The lens is a two-mirror Mangin catadioptric telephoto of the apertured-primary type. In optical processing order it consists of four components: a weak positive refractive front collector, a strong positive catadioptric primary mirror, a negative catadioptric secondary mirror, and a net negative refractive rear corrector group.

L1 is a weak biconvex positive collector. M1 is a meniscus Mangin primary mirror, concave to the object side, with a silvered or aluminum-coated rear surface and an annular reflective zone around a central aperture. M2 is a separate small meniscus Mangin secondary mirror close to the rear of L1. The rear corrector L2 contains three air-spaced elements: L21, a near-plano-concave negative; L22, a negative meniscus concave to the object side; and L23, a positive high-index meniscus convex to the object side. The rear corrector group is placed inside the central aperture of the primary mirror.

The main design decision is the separation of L1 and M2. Earlier compact mirror-lens layouts sometimes bonded the secondary mirror to the front lens. This patent instead places the secondary behind L1 as an independent element, giving separate control over the biconvex collector and the secondary mirror surfaces. The patent ties that freedom to better correction at both infinity and close focus, and also notes the practical advantages of placing the secondary inside the barrel where shielding and mechanical protection are easier.

The design is all spherical. No aspherical surfaces are used. The aberration correction is instead achieved through the shape factors and power distribution of the three powered components plus the three-element rear field corrector.

## Element-by-Element Analysis

### L1 — Biconvex Positive Collector

nd = 1.51680, νd = 64.12. Glass: 517/641 borosilicate crown, BSC7 / N-BK7 class. f = +569.7 mm.

L1 is almost symmetrical: R1 = +587.000 mm and R2 = −587.300 mm. Its computed shape factor Q1 is +0.0003, which the patent rounds to 0.0. The biconvex form is not incidental; the patent contrasts it with the meniscus front lenses of earlier catadioptric telephotos and states that the biconvex form helps suppress spherical-aberration variation when the focus unit moves toward the object.

Its power is deliberately modest. The element begins convergence but leaves the dominant system power to the primary mirror. Its focal-length ratio f1/f = 1.141 lies inside the patent's condition (4), which balances compactness against close-focus aberration stability.

### M1 — Positive Mangin Primary Mirror

nd = 1.51680, νd = 64.12. Glass: 517/641 borosilicate crown, BSC7 / N-BK7 class. Catadioptric component f = +128.0 mm.

M1 is the dominant converging component. Light enters the front refractive surface R3 = −116.666 mm, traverses the glass, reflects at the silvered or aluminum-coated rear surface R4 = −192.743 mm, and exits again through the same refractive surface, encoded in the patent as R5. The refractive entry and exit surfaces partially cancel the spherical aberration that a bare mirror would introduce, while the rear reflection supplies the strong positive power.

The shape factor Q2 = 4.067 sits within the patent's required range of 3.8 < Q2 < 4.6. This component is highly sensitive: small changes in its meniscus shape alter both spherical aberration and field curvature, so the patent treats Q2 together with the secondary mirror's Q3.

The mirror is annular. Its central aperture passes the final image-forming beam into the rear corrector group, while the reflective coating occupies the outer zone.

### M2 — Negative Mangin Secondary Mirror

nd = 1.51680, νd = 64.12. Glass: 517/641 borosilicate crown, BSC7 / N-BK7 class. Catadioptric component f = −86.9 mm.

M2 is a separate meniscus Mangin secondary mirror positioned near the rear side of L1. In optical passage order, light from M1 enters the surface R6 = −58.093 mm, reflects at R7 = −99.328 mm, and exits again through R8, the same physical surface as R6.

Its diverging power is strong: f3/f = −0.174. This is the principal telephoto-compression component after the primary mirror has folded the light back toward the front of the lens. The shape factor Q3 = −3.818 lies within the patent's condition (3). Its positive residual spherical contribution compensates the negative residual from M1, which is the cancellation mechanism described in the patent's discussion of the third-order coefficients.

### L21 — Near-Plano-Concave Negative Corrector

nd = 1.51680, νd = 64.12. Glass: 517/641 borosilicate crown, BSC7 / N-BK7 class. f = −49.2 mm.

L21 is the first rear-corrector element. Its object-side surface R9 = +10206.648 mm is effectively flat, while its image-side surface R10 = +25.338 mm is strongly concave toward the image side. The patent specifically describes this first rear element as a negative lens with its stronger curvature on the image side.

This element begins the net negative power of the rear corrector and strongly participates in field correction. Its surface 10 also carries one of the largest distortion coefficients in the patent's Table 3.

### L22 — Negative Meniscus Corrector

nd = 1.51823, νd = 58.96. Glass: Hoya E-C3 exact by nd/νd; Ohara S-NSL3 is a near-equivalent 518/590 crown. f = −222.7 mm.

L22 is a weak negative meniscus, concave to the object side, with R11 = −29.450 mm and R12 = −41.602 mm. It is less powerful than L21 but provides a controlled curvature transition between the strong negative first corrector and the positive rear meniscus.

Its role is mainly off-axis balancing. The patent identifies the negative lens group as the component that principally corrects image-angle-dependent aberrations, especially distortion and field behavior.

### L23 — Positive High-Index Meniscus Corrector

nd = 1.79631, νd = 40.96. Glass: unmatched 796/410 lanthanum dense flint; nearest current public catalog class is around S-LAH52 / K-LaSFn3 / H-LaF54, but those are not exact matches. f = +125.6 mm.

L23 is a positive meniscus convex to the object side, with R13 = +41.434 mm and R14 = +66.948 mm. It is the only high-index element in the lens and the only positive element in the rear corrector group.

The high index allows a positive field-correcting contribution with moderate curvatures. The element itself is positive, but the rear group as a whole remains net negative because L21 is strongly negative and L22 is also negative. The computed rear-group focal length is −65.1 mm, giving f4/f = −0.130 as in the patent.

## Glass Identification and Selection

The glass palette is conservative. Four of the six elements use the same 517/641 crown, while the rear corrector adds one ordinary light crown and one high-index dense flint.

| Element(s) | Patent nd | Patent νd | Identification | Role |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| L1, M1, M2, L21 | 1.51680 | 64.12 | 517/641 borosilicate crown; BSC7 / N-BK7 class | Front collector, both Mangin mirror bodies, first rear corrector |
| L22 | 1.51823 | 58.96 | Hoya E-C3 exact; Ohara S-NSL3 near-equivalent | Weak negative meniscus corrector |
| L23 | 1.79631 | 40.96 | Unmatched 796/410 lanthanum dense flint; nearest current public catalog family is roughly S-LAH52 / K-LaSFn3 / H-LaF54, not exact | Positive high-index field-corrector meniscus |

The mirror-powered architecture is the main chromatic-correction mechanism. Mirrors do not disperse light, so the dominant positive and negative powers do not introduce ordinary longitudinal color in the way a purely refractive 500 mm telephoto would. The glass choices therefore keep residual refractive color under control rather than attempting an apochromatic refractive correction.

The actual count of BK7-class crown elements is four: L1, M1, M2, and L21. L22 is a separate 518/590 crown, and L23 is a separate dense flint.

## Focus Mechanism

The focus mechanism is front-unit focus. L1 and M2 move together as a rigid unit toward the object, while the primary mirror, rear corrector, and image plane remain fixed.

| State | Object distance | Focus-unit travel | Computed magnification |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Infinity | ∞ | 0.00 mm | 0 |
| Close focus | ≈ 1.5 m | 8.05 mm toward object | −0.404×, about 1:2.47 |

In the patent's optical passage coordinates, this movement opens three optical spacings by the same amount: the L1-to-M1 spacing, the M1-to-M2 return spacing, and the M2-to-rear-corrector spacing. In the physical data file, the same movement is represented as a single variable spacing between the moving front unit and the fixed rear optical block: surface 6/8 changes from 41.50 mm at infinity to 49.55 mm at close focus.

A paraxial finite-object trace gives an image-plane-referenced object distance of about 1.507 m for the 8.05 mm close-focus shift. The small difference from 1.500 m is within the expected effect of rounded prescription values and the patent's own rounded close-focus statement.

## Aberration Correction Strategy

The two Mangin mirrors do most of the axial work. The primary mirror carries strong positive power and a large residual third-order contribution; the secondary carries strong negative power and supplies the compensating residual of opposite sign. This is the axial cancellation mechanism described in the patent's discussion of the third-order aberration coefficients.

The rear corrector group handles most of the field-dependent cleanup. Table 3 of the patent places especially large distortion coefficients at surfaces 10, 13, and 14, which are all in the rear corrector. The group also contributes field flattening, though field curvature is not confined to L2; the mirrors remain major contributors to the Petzval balance.

Using the surface-by-surface Petzval formula φ/(n·n′), including signed refractive index through reflection, the full system gives a Petzval sum of approximately +7.62 × 10⁻⁴ mm⁻¹. That corresponds to a Petzval radius of about +1313 mm, or roughly 2.63 times the 499.482 mm focal length. The positive M2 Petzval term and the negative M1 and rear-corrector terms nearly cancel, leaving a relatively flat field for a very short folded telephoto.

The aperture distinction also matters. The patent's F-number is geometric: F/6.7 from the clear annular entrance geometry. The production lens is designated f/8 because the secondary obstruction removes roughly 30% of the collecting area. The data file therefore records both: apertureDesign = 6.7 and apertureMarketing / nominalFno = 8.

## Verification Summary

The prescription was independently re-traced in paraxial y–n·u form before producing the final files. With the corrected 41.50 mm spacing after surface 8, the system matrix to the patent image plane gives B = 499.482 mm and an image-height residual of −2.3 × 10⁻⁴ per millimeter of input ray height. The residual is consistent with the rounded surface data in the patent table.

The rounded table's exact Gaussian back focus after surface 14 is 63.103 mm if the image plane is solved from the matrix, while the patent tabulates 63.216 mm. The latter value is retained in the data and analysis because it is the published prescription spacing and is also the value that gives the stated total track and focal length under the patent's rounded convention.

The close-focus trace with the 8.05 mm front-unit displacement gives an image-plane-referenced object distance of about 1.507 m and a lateral magnification of −0.404×, equivalent to approximately 1:2.47. These values confirm the patent and production statements of 1.5 m close focus and about 1:2.5 reproduction.

## Design Heritage and Context

The Reflex-Nikkor 500mm f/8 (New) was Nikon's compact successor to the earlier Reflex-Nikkor 500mm f/8. Nikon's design history states that the earlier lens was 91 mm in diameter, 126 mm long, 1000 g, and focused to 4 m, while the New version was 89 mm in diameter, 109 mm long, 840 g, and focused to 1.5 m. The patent's first embodiment explains the optical basis for that reduction: a telephoto ratio of 28.6%, a separate movable L1/M2 focus unit, and a rear corrector placed through the bored primary mirror.

The design's practical limitations are the usual ones for a compact catadioptric: fixed aperture, central obstruction, and annular out-of-focus highlights. Those are not secondary defects of this implementation; they follow from the two-mirror aperture geometry.
