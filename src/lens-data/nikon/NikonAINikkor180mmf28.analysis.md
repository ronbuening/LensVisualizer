# Nikon AI Nikkor 180mm f/2.8 ED — Optical Analysis

## Patent Reference and Design Identification

**Patent:** US 4,338,001  
**Application Number:** US 153,787  
**Filed:** May 27, 1980  
**Granted:** July 6, 1982  
**Priority:** JP 54/69871, June 6, 1979  
**Inventor:** Sei Matsui  
**Assignee:** Nippon Kogaku K.K.  
**Title:** Telephoto Lens System  
**Embodiment analyzed:** Embodiment 2 / Claim 4 numerical example

US 4,338,001 describes a five-element, five-group telephoto lens with a convergent forward group, a divergent rearward group, an aperture ratio of 1:2.8, and a claimed telephoto ratio range of 0.90-0.96. The numerical examples are normalized to a focal length of $f = 100$. The prescription transcribed in the accompanying data file is Embodiment 2, scaled by $1.8\times$ to the production 180 mm class.

The identification of Embodiment 2 with the AI Nikkor 180mm f/2.8 ED is probable rather than explicitly stated in the patent. The patent itself does not name the commercial lens. The match rests on convergent evidence: the production lens is a five-group, five-element telephoto with ED glass in the front element; Nikon's own retrospective identifies Sei Matsui as the designer and says the ED successor adopted a five-group, five-element structure; the patent's angle of view is $2\omega = 13.6^\circ$, matching a 180 mm lens on 135 format; and Embodiment 2 gives a telephoto ratio of 0.957, inside the patent's stated 0.90-0.96 target range.

The patent's Figure 3 aberration plots correspond to Embodiment 2. They show the second example's spherical aberration, astigmatism, and distortion; the data file therefore uses the Embodiment 2 numerical table, not the first or third embodiment.

## Optical Architecture

The design is a classical telephoto system: a positive forward group followed by a negative rearward group. The forward group contains L1-L3 and has paraxial focal length $f_1 = 80.383$ at patent scale, or $144.690$ mm after the $1.8\times$ production scaling. The rearward group contains L4-L5 and has paraxial focal length $f_2 = -118.959$ at patent scale, or $-214.127$ mm at production scale.

The forward group is a positive-negative-positive triplet. L1 is the ED positive collector, L2 is the high-index flint achromatizing element, and L3 is a positive meniscus that completes the forward convergence while helping control spherical aberration and field curvature. The patent specifically requires L1 to be biconvex with its stronger surface facing the object, L2 to be biconcave with its stronger surface facing the object, and L3 to be a positive meniscus convex to the object.

The rearward group is a negative-positive, air-spaced pair. L4 supplies the principal negative telephoto power, which pulls the rear principal point forward and makes the optical total track shorter than the focal length. L5 is not merely a relay element; the patent and Nikon retrospective both point to the final positive element as part of the pincushion-distortion correction mechanism.

All ten refracting surfaces are spherical. The patent publishes no aspherical coefficients and does not describe an aspherical manufacturing method. The correction strategy is therefore glass selection, strongly shaped spherical surfaces, and group-power distribution rather than aspherical departure.

## Element-by-Element Analysis

### L1 — Biconvex Positive, ED fluorophosphate crown

$n_d = 1.50032$, $\nu_d = 81.9$. Glass: unmatched Nikon ED fluorophosphate, near the S-FPL51/FCD1 class but not an exact catalog match. $f = +118.099$ mm at production scale.

L1 is the principal front collector and the low-dispersion member of the L1-L2 achromatizing pair. The first surface is stronger than the second, matching the patent's requirement that the biconvex first lens present its greater curvature to the object side. Its low index forces relatively strong curvatures for a positive lens of this aperture, but the high Abbe number sharply reduces the chromatic error generated by the front group.

The rear surface of L1 and the front surface of L2 are nearly matched in radius: $r_2/r_3 = (-78.244)/(-76.698) = 1.020$ at patent scale. This satisfies the patent's Condition (II), $1 < |r_2/r_3| < 1.1$, and is the most explicit mathematical clue that the pair is being used as the primary axial-chromatic correction unit.

The patent does not publish partial-dispersion constants for this ED glass. The data file therefore marks the anomalous partial dispersion as inferred from the ED fluorophosphate class rather than storing a numerical $\Delta P_{g,F}$ value.

### L2 — Biconcave Negative, lanthanum flint class

$n_d = 1.74950$, $\nu_d = 35.0$. Glass: LAFN7 / E-LAF7 class, 750/350 lanthanum flint. $f = -134.023$ mm at production scale.

L2 is the high-dispersion partner to the ED first element. The patent constrains this element to $n_2 > 1.7$ and $\nu_2 < 40$, which is the dense-flint territory needed to counter the chromatic power of L1 without requiring excessively steep curvatures.

The front surface of L2 is much stronger than the rear surface. That is not incidental: the patent states that the L1 rear surface and L2 front surface are the relevant achromatizing surfaces, and that the small spacing between them is needed because chromatic aberration generated at L1's rear surface must be negated by L2's front surface.

### L3 — Positive Meniscus, dense barium crown class

$n_d = 1.65844$, $\nu_d = 50.8$. Glass: N-SSK5 / S-BSM25 class, 658/509 dense barium crown. $f = +170.900$ mm at production scale.

L3 is the final forward-group element. It is a positive meniscus convex to the object side, as required by the patent claim. Its index and Abbe number fall in the range specified by Condition (III): $1.6 < n_3 < 1.7$ and $50 < \nu_3 < 60$.

Optically, L3 gives the forward group enough residual positive power to reach $f_1 \approx 0.804f$ while avoiding the stronger chromatic penalty that would result if the ED/flint pair had to carry all of the forward-group power. Its meniscus bending also helps temper the spherical aberration and field curvature generated by the first two elements.

### L4 — Negative Meniscus, crown-flint class

$n_d = 1.51454$, $\nu_d = 54.6$. Glass: KF3-class crown-flint, no current public catalog match asserted. $f = -73.841$ mm at production scale.

L4 is the main divergent component of the rear group. Its front radius, $r_7 = -19.722$ at patent scale, is the strongest curvature in the design. After scaling, that radius is $-35.500$ mm. This strong negative surface is the primary cause of the telephoto shortening effect and a principal source of the rear group's pincushion-distortion tendency.

The prior draft described this element as a generic light crown. That was too loose. The optical constants are closer to an older KF crown-flint region than to the S-TIL25 comparison previously suggested. Because no authoritative current vendor catalog entry was found that matches both $n_d = 1.51454$ and $\nu_d = 54.6$ closely, the data file uses a class-level label rather than a false exact glass name.

### L5 — Biconvex Positive, high-index flint / TAF-LaSF class

$n_d = 1.79668$, $\nu_d = 45.4$. Glass: unmatched TAF/LaSF-class high-index flint, near HOYA TAF2 / HIKARI J-LASF017 by optical code but not an exact catalog match. $f = +117.873$ mm at production scale.

L5 is the rear positive corrector. It partially cancels the pincushion distortion introduced by L4 while preserving the rear group's net negative power. The Nikon retrospective describes this same rear-group logic: the telephoto type shortens the lens through the rear concave element, and the last convex element cancels the pincushion tendency.

The prior draft called this glass a lanthanum crown. That classification was incorrect. With $\nu_d = 45.4$, the element lies on the flint side of the usual crown/flint boundary. It should be described as a high-index lanthanum/tantalum flint class glass, not as a crown. The exact melt remains unresolved.

## Glass Selection

| Element | $n_d$ | $\nu_d$ | Corrected glass classification | Catalog status | Optical role |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|---|
| L1 | 1.50032 | 81.9 | ED fluorophosphate crown | Unmatched; near S-FPL51/FCD1 code 497/816 but higher $n_d$ | Low-dispersion positive collector |
| L2 | 1.74950 | 35.0 | Lanthanum flint, LAFN7/E-LAF7 class | Close to Schott LAFN7 / HOYA E-LAF7 class | Achromatizing negative partner to L1 |
| L3 | 1.65844 | 50.8 | Dense barium crown, SSK5/S-BSM25 class | Good catalog-class match | Forward-group power and aberration balance |
| L4 | 1.51454 | 54.6 | KF3-class crown-flint | Class-level only; no exact current catalog match asserted | Main rear telephoto diverger |
| L5 | 1.79668 | 45.4 | TAF/LaSF-class high-index flint | Soft class match only; not a crown | Rear positive distortion corrector |

The essential chromatic strategy is the L1-L2 pairing. L1 supplies positive power with very low dispersion; L2 supplies high-index, higher-dispersion negative power. The Abbe-number ratio $81.9/35.0 = 2.34$ gives the pair much greater chromatic leverage than a conventional crown/flint achromat. The patent's Condition (II) then makes that pairing geometrically effective by forcing the achromatizing surfaces to have nearly equal curvature and a very small air gap.

The data file deliberately avoids overclaiming catalog identities for L1, L4, and L5. The L1 value is close to modern ED fluorophosphate glasses but does not reproduce the exact S-FPL51/FCD1 $n_d$. L4 and L5 fall in older Japanese catalog regions where current public tables do not provide a clean one-to-one match. The corrected treatment is to identify class and uncertainty explicitly rather than seed downstream dispersion logic with a wrong exact name.

## Focus Mechanism

The patent publishes only the infinity-focus prescription and does not give a floating-group or internal-focus variable-spacing table. The production manual-focus lens is therefore modeled as unit focus: the optical assembly translates as a unit and the only variable spacing in the data file is the final back-focus gap.

At production scale, the infinity back focal distance from surface 10 to the image plane is $41.261$ mm. Solving the finite-conjugate paraxial problem for a 1.8 m object distance measured from the image plane gives a close-focus back-focus value of $65.378$ mm. The corresponding modeled unit-focus extension is $24.117$ mm.

| Focus state | Final gap from surface 10 to image plane | Relative extension |
|---|---:|---:|
| Infinity | 41.260824 mm | 0.000 mm |
| 1.8 m close focus | 65.378011 mm | 24.117187 mm |

This corrects the prior draft's thinner approximation of roughly 20 mm. That earlier expression used $f^2/(s-f)$ with $s = 1800$ mm, but it treated the subject distance as if it were measured from a principal plane. Minimum focus distance for camera lenses is conventionally measured from the film or sensor plane, so the finite-conjugate trace must include the lens's own total track and principal-plane positions.

## Aspherical Surfaces

The design is all-spherical. US 4,338,001 lists only radii, thicknesses, refractive indices, and Abbe numbers for the examples. It provides no aspherical equation and no coefficient table. The corrected data file therefore uses `asph: {}`.

## Aberration Correction Strategy

Condition (I) controls the two-group telephoto power split:

- $0.75f < f_1 < 0.85f$
- $0.8f < |f_2| < 1.2f$
- $0.55f < l < 0.6f$

For Embodiment 2, the independent paraxial trace gives $f_1 = 80.383$, $f_2 = -118.959$, and $l = 57.047$ at patent scale. These values satisfy the condition and place the rear group close to the allowed high-magnitude end of the range.

Condition (II) controls the L1-L2 achromatizing interface. The trace and transcription give $|r_2/r_3| = 1.020$ and $d_2 = 1.167$, both satisfying the patent's requirements. This condition is more than a packaging rule: it keeps the marginal-ray heights at the two high-leverage chromatic surfaces close enough that the spherical and chromatic-spherical residuals do not diverge.

Condition (III) controls the first three glass choices. Embodiment 2 satisfies the patent's three glass constraints: $n_1 = 1.50032 < 1.54$, $\nu_1 = 81.9 > 65$; $n_2 = 1.74950 > 1.7$, $\nu_2 = 35.0 < 40$; and $n_3 = 1.65844$, $\nu_3 = 50.8$.

## Implementation Notes for the Data File

The data file is a production-scaled rendering prescription, not a verbatim patent table. All radii and axial distances from Embodiment 2 were multiplied by $1.8$. The final back focal distance was then computed paraxially rather than taken from the patent, because the patent table stops at $r_{10}$ and gives only the telephoto ratio.

The aperture stop is shown in the patent drawing inside the long $d_6$ air space, but its exact split is not tabulated. For the data file, $d_6 = 90.9$ mm at production scale was split as 54.0 mm from surface 6 to the stop and 36.9 mm from the stop to surface 7. With that split, the paraxial stop semi-diameter required for f/2.8 is $16.146$ mm.

The patent does not publish semi-diameters. The listed clear apertures are therefore conservative rendering apertures estimated from the paraxial marginal-ray envelope, the full-frame half field, and the 72 mm front filter constraint. They are not claimed as Nikon manufacturing drawings.

## Design Heritage and Context

Nikon's official retrospective places the AI Nikkor 180mm f/2.8 ED in direct succession to the earlier NIKKOR Auto / AI 180mm f/2.8 line. It attributes both the earlier design lineage and the ED successor to Sei Matsui. It also states that the ED version retained the same broad size class while changing to a five-group, five-element telephoto structure with ED glass.

The same retrospective describes the ED lens architecture in terms consistent with the patent: a convex-concave-convex forward group, a concave-convex rear group, ED glass in the top lens, a second concave glass chosen for chromatic pairing with ED, and a last convex element used to counter the pincushion distortion generated by the telephoto rear group.
