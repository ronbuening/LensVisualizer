# Nikon Reflex-Nikkor 1000mm f/11 — Optical Analysis

## Patent Reference and Design Identification

**Patent:** US 3,507,556  
**Filed:** October 20, 1966  
**Priority:** Japan, October 22, 1965  
**Granted:** April 21, 1970  
**Inventor:** Yoshiyuki Shimizu  
**Assignee:** Nippon Kogaku K.K., Tokyo  
**Title:** Telescope Lens of Reflecting-Refractive Type  
**Embodiment analyzed:** Embodiment 1 / Claim 1, f = 1000.0

US 3,507,556 discloses a compact reflecting-refractive telescope lens using a positive front lens, a rear-surface concave primary mirror, a rear-surface convex secondary mirror, and a rear diverging lens group. Embodiment 1 is the prescription used here because it is the patent's f = 1000.0 example and is reproduced again as Claim 1. The rendered patent table and Claim 1 table were checked directly; where OCR is ambiguous, the image tables are treated as controlling.

The identification with the Reflex-Nikkor 1000mm f/11 rests on converging evidence rather than on a single patent statement. Embodiment 1 has five glass bodies in five air-separated groups: front corrector L1, primary Mangin substrate M1, secondary Mangin substrate M2, and rear lenses L2 and L3. Its nominal focal length is 1000.0 mm. Fig. 3 plots aberrations to 1.23° half-field, which corresponds closely to the 35 mm diagonal field of a 1000 mm lens. The patent also states the design goal of shortening total length to about one-fourth of focal length while restricting the auxiliary mirror to about 40% of the effective aperture. Nikon's historical Reflex-Nikkor sequence places the 1000mm f/11 in 1966, consistent with the patent priority date.

The manufacturer's published hard specifications are outside the patent prescription but align with it: fixed f/11, five elements in five groups, 2°30′ picture angle, 8 m minimum focus, Nikon F mount, and 39 mm rear filters. The source patent itself publishes only the infinity optical prescription and therefore does not determine the focusing movement, filter system, or production weight.

## Optical Architecture

The design is a Cassegrain-type catadioptric telephoto using two Mangin mirrors. From object to image in optical sequence, light passes through L1, travels to the annular primary Mangin M1, reflects forward to the smaller secondary Mangin M2, reflects rearward through the primary perforation, then passes through the L2-L3 diverging corrector group to the final image plane behind the primary.

The power distribution is positive L1, converging primary mirror M1, diverging secondary mirror M2, and net-negative rear corrector group L2-L3. The primary mirror forms the first real image with strong convergence. The convex secondary enlarges that image and folds the light path back through the central opening. The rear group has negative composite focal length and is used mainly to counter the field curvature generated by the front lens and mirror system.

The prescription is all-spherical. The architectural distinction is not an aspherical correction method but the use of reverse-surface mirrors. A front-surface mirror would be intrinsically achromatic but would not provide the same spherical-aberration correction. A Mangin mirror uses a refracting front surface and a reflecting rear surface, so each mirror substrate contributes both refraction and reflection.

An independent folded-path paraxial trace of Embodiment 1 gives EFL = 1001.399 mm and BFD = 89.624 mm measured from r10, the last refracting surface. In the physical layout used by the viewer, that places the image plane at z = 273.824 mm. The EFL differs from the patent's nominal f = 1000.0 by 0.14%, consistent with rounded patent values and d-line calculation. Normalized marginal-ray heights are approximately 1.00 at L1, 0.88 at M1, 0.34 at M2, and 0.09 through the rear corrector group, supporting the patent's statement that the secondary can be much smaller than the primary.

## Element-by-Element Analysis

### L1 — Positive Plano-Convex Corrector

nd = 1.51823, νd = 59.0. Glass: K-crown 518/590 class, equivalent to Hoya E-C3 / Ohara S-NSL3 / Hikari J-K3. f = +1316.0 mm.

L1 is a weak positive plano-convex corrector with R1 = +682.000 and R2 = ∞. Its standalone power is low relative to the 1000 mm system EFL, so it functions more as a corrector than as the principal image-forming element. The patent treats the R1/R2 condition as a spherical-aberration and sine-condition constraint for the complete system, and the plane rear surface satisfies that condition trivially.

### M1 — Primary Mangin Mirror

nd = 1.54072, νd = 47.2. Glass: light-flint 541/472 class, equivalent to Hoya E-FEL2 / Ohara S-TIL2 / legacy LLF2-class glass. Refractive substrate f = -1759.6 mm; Mangin unit focal-length magnitude ≈ 337.8 mm.

M1 is a negative meniscus glass body with R3 = -319.000 and R4 = -485.185. As a refractor alone, it is weakly negative, matching the patent's description of the first mirror as a negative meniscus lens. As a Mangin mirror, its rear silvered surface supplies the main convergence.

The patent emphasizes that the L1-M1 image must be well corrected before secondary magnification. That point is important: residual aberration before M2 is enlarged by the secondary, so the primary cannot be treated merely as a compact folding mirror.

### M2 — Secondary Mangin Mirror

nd = 1.54814, νd = 45.9. Glass: light-flint 548/459 class, equivalent to LLF1 / Hoya E-FEL1 / Ohara S-TIL1 class. Refractive substrate f = -909.5 mm when traversed R5 -> R6; Mangin unit focal-length magnitude ≈ 181.5 mm.

M2 is the convex secondary Mangin mirror. In optical sequence after primary reflection, the ray enters through R5 = -169.430, reflects from R6 = -258.420, and exits again through R5. The convex reflection enlarges and relays the primary image back through the primary perforation.

Claim 1 describes the second mirror as being constituted by a positive meniscus lens. The numerical substrate traced through R5 then R6 computes weakly negative as a refractive element. The delivered files therefore describe it neutrally as a meniscus secondary and assign the functional sign to the catadioptric unit rather than to the bare substrate alone.

### L2 — Rear Biconcave Negative Corrector

nd = 1.51680, νd = 64.2. Glass: BK7/BSC7/H-K9L-family borosilicate crown, 517/642 class. f = -99.3 mm.

L2 is the strong negative member of the rear correcting group. The rendered patent table and Claim 1 table were checked for the rear radius; r8 is +69.200, not the +60.200 that appears in some OCR text. With r7 = -200.000 and r8 = +69.200, L2 is biconcave and strongly negative.

The high Abbe number is appropriate because L2 carries most of the rear group's negative power. A high-dispersion flint in this slot would impose a larger chromatic penalty.

### L3 — Rear Biconvex Dense-Flint Corrector

nd = 1.74077, νd = 27.7. Glass: SF13-family dense flint, equivalent to Hoya E-FD13 / Ohara S-TIH13 / Hikari J-SF13. f = +283.1 mm.

L3 is the positive dense-flint partner to L2, with r9 = +600.000 and r10 = -321.860. The independent matrix calculation gives f(L2-L3) = -155.6 mm, confirming that the two-lens rear group is net negative as the patent states.

The crown-negative / flint-positive pairing partly balances the color introduced by the rear group's strong negative power, but the patent's chromatic strategy is broader than a conventional two-element achromat. Reflection itself is achromatic; the refracting skins of the Mangin mirrors and the three lenses are balanced at the system level.

## Glass Identification and Selection

The patent gives nd and νd but does not name glass vendors. The table therefore uses catalog classes and close equivalents. The glass labels should be read as catalog-resolution aids, not as claims that Nikon used a particular supplier melt.

| Element | nd | νd | Code class | Catalog-equivalent wording used | Role |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|---|
| L1 | 1.51823 | 59.0 | 518/590 | Hoya E-C3 / Ohara S-NSL3 / Hikari J-K3 class | Weak front corrector crown |
| M1 | 1.54072 | 47.2 | 541/472 | Hoya E-FEL2 / Ohara S-TIL2 / LLF2 class | Primary Mangin substrate |
| M2 | 1.54814 | 45.9 | 548/459 | LLF1 / Hoya E-FEL1 / Ohara S-TIL1 class | Secondary Mangin substrate |
| L2 | 1.51680 | 64.2 | 517/642 | BK7/BSC7/H-K9L-family borosilicate crown | Strong negative rear corrector |
| L3 | 1.74077 | 27.7 | 741/277 | Hoya E-FD13 / Ohara S-TIH13 / Hikari J-SF13 class | Dense-flint rear positive |

No ED, fluorite, or anomalous-partial-dispersion glass is required by the patent. The lens should be described as a well-corrected achromatic catadioptric, not as an apochromat in the modern ED-glass sense. The patent's own explanation is architectural: reverse-surface mirrors provide useful spherical-aberration correction, while the remaining refractive elements control the chromatic cost of the Mangin construction.

## Focus Mechanism

The patent gives only the infinity prescription. It does not publish a close-focus prescription, a variable-spacing table, or a mechanical section sufficient to reconstruct which bodies move during focusing.

The `.data.ts` file therefore models only the verified infinity prescription and leaves `var` empty. The production minimum focus distance is recorded as `closeFocusM: 8` for catalog metadata, but the optical model does not alter group spacing at close focus. Assigning the motion to whole-cell focusing, internal focusing, or rear-group focusing would exceed the patent evidence.

The lens has no image stabilization and no diaphragm. The fixed-aperture value is represented by a synthetic entrance stop sized for f/11; it is a modeling aperture, not a patent diaphragm.

## Conditional Expressions

The patent gives four principal design conditions. Embodiment 1 satisfies all four.

| Patent condition | Computed value | Result |
|---|---:|---|
| 5R1 < \|R2\| | 5 × 682.000 = 3410 < ∞ | Satisfied |
| 1.35 < \|R4/R3\| < 1.75 | 485.185 / 319.000 = 1.521 | Satisfied |
| 1.3 < \|R6/R5\| < 2.0 | 258.420 / 169.430 = 1.525 | Satisfied |
| 0.2f < \|R6\| < 0.7f | \|R6\|/f = 0.258 | Satisfied |

The two mirror radius ratios both sit close to 1.5. This matches the patent's statement that a Mangin mirror using glass near n = 1.5 has favorable spherical-aberration correction when the back/front radius ratio is about 1.5. The patent does not reduce the design to that single value, however; it gives a finite range because the sine condition and off-axis behavior also have to remain balanced.

## Aberration Correction and Design Philosophy

The patent's central trade is between front-surface and reverse-surface reflection. A front-surface mirror does not add chromatic aberration, but it also lacks the refractive correction available in a Mangin mirror. A reverse-surface mirror can correct spherical aberration more effectively, but the glass path introduces chromatic terms that must be balanced elsewhere.

M1 and M2 are therefore not simple folding mirrors. Their radius ratios are chosen to hold spherical aberration and the sine condition within usable limits, while the rear group supplies the net negative power needed to control field curvature. A surface-by-surface Petzval calculation using signed refractive indices for the folded mirror path gives a Petzval sum of 2.074121e-04 mm^-1 under the sign convention used here, corresponding to a Petzval radius magnitude of about 4821 mm, or |Rp|/f ≈ 4.8.

The secondary-size condition is equally important. If |R6| is too small, M2's magnification becomes excessive and aberrations grow. If |R6| is too large, the secondary mirror diameter and the downstream correction burden increase. Embodiment 1 sits near the compact end of the allowed range while preserving the prescribed correction margins.

## Data-File Modeling Notes

The patent omits clear apertures. The data file uses estimated semi-diameters derived from an f/11 entrance pupil and a traced marginal/chief-ray envelope to 1.25° half-field. The stored values are 45.52 mm for the synthetic entrance aperture, 18.2 mm for the central obstruction, 52 mm for L1, 48 mm for the annular primary, 22 mm for the secondary, and 16.5 mm for the rear corrector pair.

The central obstruction is modeled as a synthetic `OBS` blocking surface placed immediately ahead of the secondary assembly. This approximates the patent's stated auxiliary-mirror obstruction without treating the secondary holder as an additional optical element.

The primary and secondary are modeled as second-surface mirrors. `opticalPath.surfaceOrder` repeats M1F and M2F because the ray passes through each Mangin front surface, reflects from the rear coated surface, and exits through the same front surface. The physical surface table remains in left-to-right axial order so the renderer can show L1, the secondary, the 10 mm thick primary, and the rear corrector group in their correct axial relationship.

No aspherical surfaces, cover glass, or filters are included.
