# Nikon Fisheye-Nikkor 6mm f/2.8 — Optical Analysis

## Patent Reference and Design Identification

**Patent:** US 3,737,214  
**Title:** Wide Angle Fisheye Lens  
**Application Number:** US 184,782  
**Filed:** September 29, 1971  
**Granted:** June 5, 1973  
**Priority:** September 30, 1970, Japan 45/85420  
**Inventor:** Yoshiyuki Shimizu  
**Assignee:** Nippon Kogaku K.K.  
**Embodiment analyzed:** Example I, corresponding to Claim 1

Example I is the patent embodiment corresponding to the production Fisheye-Nikkor 6mm f/2.8. The identification is based on the convergence of the patent's 12-element optical construction, the 220° stated angle of field, the f/2.8 aperture, the special retrofocus layout that clears the SLR mirror, and the production lens specification of a 6mm f/2.8 circular fisheye with 12 elements in 9 groups.

The patent's central contribution is the forward negative fisheye group, not the rear image-forming group. Shimizu states that the front group consists of three negative meniscus lenses convex to the object, followed by a biconcave negative lens and a positive lens. The worked examples then supply complete rear groups, but the patent text explicitly notes that the shape of the positive rear group has no essential significance to the invention. Example I is the only worked example in the patent that aligns with the production 6mm f/2.8 lens; Example II is a 2.0mm f/2 design, and Example III is a 10.2mm f/4 design.

A caution is required about the patent's stated focal length. The patent labels Example I as `f = 6.3 mm`, `F/2.8`, `2ω = 220°`, and `B.f. = 37.657 mm`. In a conventional centered paraxial trace, the full system has a Gaussian effective focal length of approximately 37.4 mm. The 6.3mm value is therefore not the Gaussian EFL of the centered optical system. It is best treated as the fisheye projection or nominal imaging constant associated with the circular 220° field, while the rear positive relay is designed to form the final image at the specified back focal position.

## Optical Architecture

The design is a large, all-spherical, retrofocus circular fisheye. It contains 12 glass elements in 9 air-spaced groups, plus a flat internal filter plate between the forward and rearward assemblies. The data file omits the flat filter plate as a filter, following the project data specification, and folds its optical path into the adjacent air spacing.

The optical system divides into two principal assemblies. The forward group, L1 through L5, has strong net negative power and a computed equivalent focal length of approximately -9.51 mm. The rearward group, L6 through L12, has net positive power, a computed equivalent focal length of approximately +59.99 mm, and a computed rear-group back focal length of approximately +37.25 mm from the final surface. The patent's tabulated `B.f. = 37.657 mm` is retained in the data file as the nominal image-space gap.

The forward group follows a deliberate graduated-bending strategy. L1, L2, and L3 are three negative meniscus lenses, each convex toward the object. Their surfaces progressively reduce the incidence angles of rays arriving at half-field angles near and beyond 90°. L4 supplies additional negative power after this progressive refraction, while L5 partly compensates L4 and sends the beam into the positive rear relay at a more manageable angular spread.

The rearward group is a compact positive relay made from one weak positive singlet followed by three cemented doublets. The first rear doublet is positive, the second is negative, and the third is positive. This positive-negative-positive sequence contributes image formation, chromatic correction, and Petzval balancing after the severe negative distortion generated by the forward group.

The aperture stop is not numerically specified in the patent table. The patent drawing places the diaphragm in the long air space following L5 and before the flat filter plate and rear relay. The data file therefore inserts an inferred stop in the folded R10-to-R13 air space. This is a modeling choice rather than a patent-stated axial dimension.

## Element-by-Element Analysis

### L1 — Negative Meniscus, Convex to Object

`nd = 1.51680`, `νd = 64.2`. Glass: J-BK7A / N-BK7 / S-BSL7 equivalent. `f ≈ -164.5 mm`.

L1 is the large front dome element. Its front surface has the patent's largest effective diameter, `φ1 = 213 mm`, and a radius of `R1 = +143.47 mm`, so the clear diameter exceeds the radius of curvature. This is the defining geometry of the deep-dome fisheye front end: the first surface is not a weak, nearly flat window, but a deeply curved refracting surface that intercepts rays from slightly behind the lens plane.

As an isolated element, L1 is only weakly negative. Its role is primarily geometric: it begins the inward bending of very oblique rays without forcing a single abrupt refraction. The use of a BK7-class crown is also practical. It is low-dispersion, readily available in large blanks, and does not impose unnecessary chromatic burden at the first refracting surface.

### L2 — Negative Meniscus, Convex to Object

`nd = 1.51680`, `νd = 64.2`. Glass: J-BK7A / N-BK7 / S-BSL7 equivalent. `f ≈ -106.9 mm`.

L2 continues the stepwise angular compression begun by L1. Its front radius, `R3 = +76.4 mm`, is smaller than L1's front radius, while its rear radius, `R4 = +31.521 mm`, is much stronger. Together with the air space from L1, it forms a strong negative air-lens region that continues bending the high-angle rays inward.

The element remains a standard crown meniscus. This keeps dispersion low in the physically large front group and avoids using high-index dense flints where large glass volume and steep surface exposure would be mechanically and coating-wise unattractive.

### L3 — Negative Meniscus, Convex to Object

`nd = 1.51680`, `νd = 64.2`. Glass: J-BK7A / N-BK7 / S-BSL7 equivalent. `f ≈ -37.6 mm`.

L3 is the strongest of the three leading BK7 menisci. Its rear surface, `R6 = +17.1 mm`, carries much of the remaining negative bending before the beam reaches the smaller L4/L5 pair. The patent text specifically explains why the surface of L3 adjacent to L2 may have a radius larger than its effective diameter: the preceding two menisci have already refracted the oblique rays sufficiently.

The first three elements therefore should not be interpreted as redundant large crowns. They split a single severe fisheye refraction into three lower-slope stages. That is the key reason this 220° design can maintain peripheral illumination while still providing mirror clearance.

### L4 — Biconcave Negative

`nd = 1.62041`, `νd = 60.3`. Glass: J-SK16 / N-SK16 / S-BSM16 equivalent. `f ≈ -25.6 mm`.

L4 is the strongest negative isolated element in the prescription. It is biconcave, with `R7 = -60.0 mm` and `R8 = +22.625 mm`, and it acts after the three BK7 menisci have already compressed the beam. The patent states that the object-side surface of L4 is concave relative to L3 for the same reason that L3's geometry changes: the rays arriving from L1 through L3 have already been substantially redirected.

The glass is a dense crown rather than a flint. This is significant because L4 carries strong negative power in a region where lateral color would otherwise accumulate rapidly. Its high Abbe number relative to L5 satisfies the patent condition `V4 > V5` and makes it the lower-dispersion member of the L4/L5 chromatic pair.

### L5 — Positive Meniscus Adjacent to L4

`nd = 1.72825`, `νd = 28.3`. Glass: J-SF10 / N-SF10 / S-TIH10 equivalent. `f ≈ +42.9 mm`.

L5 is the positive dense-flint partner following L4. Its front surface, `R9 = +23.9 mm`, is convex toward L4, and the Example I table gives a thin `0.6 mm` air space between L4 and L5. The patent prose allows both cemented and air-spaced forms; Example I is modeled as the air-spaced form because the numerical table gives distinct R8 and R9 surfaces separated by a finite gap.

The L4/L5 pair has a combined equivalent focal length of approximately -59.8 mm. L5 therefore does not neutralize the forward group; it moderates the very strong negative power of L4, contributes lateral-color correction, and prepares the beam for the rearward positive relay.

### Internal Filter Plate — Flat Optical Plate, Omitted from Data File

`nd = 1.51823`. Glass: J-K3 / S-NSL3 equivalent.

The patent includes two flat filter surfaces, R11 and R12, with a glass thickness of `1.8 mm` and a following air gap of `2.8 mm`. The production lens also uses a built-in filter system, consistent with the patent table's explicit filter notation.

The data file omits this plate as a filter, as required by the project data specification, and folds it into the surrounding air spacing. The folded spacing used between R10 and R13 is:

`25.0 mm + 1.8 mm / 1.51823 + 2.8 mm = 28.985591 mm`.

This preserves first-order axial optical path while avoiding a filter element in the renderer's element count.

### L6 — Weak Biconvex Positive Singlet

`nd = 1.64831`, `νd = 33.8`. Glass: J-SF2 class, with the patent index slightly higher than current J-SF2. `f ≈ +172.1 mm`.

L6 is the first glass element of the rearward group. It is weakly biconvex, not a meniscus: `R13 = +278.333 mm` and `R14 = -185.420 mm`. Its low power makes it a relay conditioning element rather than a primary imaging element.

The glass is close to the HIKARI J-SF2 / Schott SF2 family but not an exact current catalog match. The patent value `nd = 1.64831` is about `+0.00062` above current J-SF2 while retaining the same dense-flint character. It is therefore best labeled as an SF2-class or historical proprietary melt rather than asserted as an exact catalog glass.

### L7 + L8 — First Rear Cemented Doublet, Net Positive

L7: `nd = 1.53375`, `νd = 55.4`. Glass: unresolved crown, six-digit code 534554. Isolated `f ≈ +35.6 mm`.  
L8: `nd = 1.77250`, `νd = 49.5`. Glass: J-LASF016 / S-LAH66 / TAF1 / N-LAF34 equivalent. Isolated `f ≈ -59.6 mm`.  
Combined doublet: `f ≈ +84.7 mm`.

This is the first cemented doublet in the rear relay. The strong positive L7 is followed by a high-index lanthanum glass element, L8, at the cemented interface `R16 = -28.5 mm`. The doublet as a whole is positive, even though L8 is negative as a standalone component.

The prior identification of L8 as S-LAM52 is not supported by the checked catalog data. The current HIKARI match is J-LASF016, with equivalent or similar catalog references including S-LAH66, TAF1, H-LaF50B, and N-LAF34. L7 remains unmatched in current catalog data and should be treated as a historical crown-type melt rather than forced into a modern glass name.

### L9 + L10 — Second Rear Cemented Doublet, Net Negative

L9: `nd = 1.51823`, `νd = 59.0`. Glass: J-K3 / S-NSL3 equivalent. Isolated `f ≈ +37.5 mm`.  
L10: `nd = 1.79631`, `νd = 40.8`. Glass: unmatched lanthanum dense flint, six-digit code approximately 796/408. Isolated `f ≈ -31.5 mm`.  
Combined doublet: `f ≈ -137.5 mm`.

The second rear cemented group is the only net negative doublet in the rear relay. Its placement between positive groups is consistent with a field-flattening and Petzval-balancing role. It prevents the rear relay from becoming a simple high-Petzval positive block after the highly negative front group.

L10 does not have a close current HIKARI match. J-LASF02 and J-LASF03 are nearby in broad lanthanum-dense-flint class, but neither reproduces both `nd` and `νd` within a confident historical-transcription tolerance. The data file therefore uses an explicit unmatched label.

### L11 + L12 — Final Rear Cemented Doublet, Net Positive

L11: `nd = 1.71736`, `νd = 29.5`. Glass: J-SF1 / N-SF1 / S-TIH1 equivalent. Isolated `f ≈ -36.9 mm`.  
L12: `nd = 1.51680`, `νd = 64.2`. Glass: J-BK7A / N-BK7 / S-BSL7 equivalent. Isolated `f ≈ +29.3 mm`.  
Combined doublet: `f ≈ +118.6 mm`.

The final cemented doublet is a flint-leading achromat. L11 is a dense flint with negative isolated power, and L12 is a BK7-class crown with positive isolated power. The cemented group is net positive and completes the rear relay.

Using a low-index crown as the last glass element is a conservative final-interface choice. It reduces the index step at the last air-glass boundary compared with ending the system on dense flint and helps keep the final image-forming group chromatically balanced.

## Glass Identification and Selection

The patent gives refractive indices and Abbe numbers but does not name the glass types. Current HIKARI/Nikon optical-glass catalog data were used as the primary matching reference because the assignee is Nippon Kogaku/Nikon and the J-series catalog preserves many traditional Schott-family names with Japanese production equivalents. Where no current catalog entry matches the patent values closely enough, the glass is explicitly labeled as unmatched.

| Element(s) | Patent `nd` | Patent `νd` | Catalog identification used | Comment |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|
| L1, L2, L3, L12 | 1.51680 | 64.2 | J-BK7A; N-BK7 / S-BSL7 equivalent | Exact standard crown-class match within patent rounding. |
| L4 | 1.62041 | 60.3 | J-SK16; N-SK16 / S-BSM16 equivalent | Dense crown; the OHARA equivalent is S-BSM16 rather than S-SK16. |
| L5 | 1.72825 | 28.3 | J-SF10; N-SF10 / S-TIH10 equivalent | Dense flint partner for L4. |
| Filter, L9 | 1.51823 | 59.0 | J-K3; S-NSL3 equivalent | Filter glass and crown component of D2. |
| L6 | 1.64831 | 33.8 | J-SF2 class | Close to J-SF2 but not exact in `nd`; treated as historical/proprietary. |
| L7 | 1.53375 | 55.4 | 534554 crown, unresolved | No reliable current HIKARI match. |

The forward group uses a deliberately simple palette: BK7-class glass for the three large menisci, then a dense-crown/dense-flint pair for L4/L5. The rear group uses more specialized high-index and lanthanum glasses to supply positive relay power and chromatic correction in a compact space.

The patent condition `V4 > V5` is satisfied by L4 and L5: `60.3 > 28.3`. Because the patent's table uses `Vd` for Abbe number, this means L4 has lower dispersion than L5. That is the expected relationship for this negative-positive chromatic pair.

## Focus Mechanism

The patent provides only an infinity-focus prescription and gives no variable air-spacing table for close focus. The production lens focuses to 0.25 m, and the manual describes its focusing range as extending to 0.9 ft. There is no evidence in this patent of an internal or floating focus group.

The data file therefore models the lens as unit focus, represented by a single back-focus variable at the final surface. The infinity value is the patent image-space gap of `37.657 mm`. The close-focus value is a small extension, `37.819 mm`, estimated from the 6.3mm nominal fisheye constant at a 0.25 m subject distance. This is a first-order renderer model, not a patent-published close-focus prescription.

Minimum focus distance for F-mount lenses is conventionally specified from the film plane, not from the front vertex of the lens. The analysis therefore avoids treating the 0.25 m figure as a front-element working distance.

## Aspherical Surfaces

The design is entirely spherical. The patent gives no aspherical coefficients for Example I, and the prescription contains only spherical or plane surfaces. This is consistent with the design era and the very large front-element diameters involved.

The absence of aspheres is compensated by element count and by the progressive refraction of the forward group. Rather than using one strongly aspheric front member, the design splits the fisheye conversion across three large BK7 menisci and a smaller L4/L5 correction pair.

## Aberration Correction Strategy

The patent's Example I aberration plots show spherical aberration and sine-condition curves, sagittal and meridional image-surface curves, and a fisheye distortion curve. These plots should be read as design targets for the patent example, not as measured production MTF or field data.

The most important correction strategy is distribution. The front group generates the necessary negative distortion and angular compression in multiple stages. L4/L5 then adds a chromatic balancing pair within the front group. The rear relay uses a positive singlet and three cemented doublets to recover image formation, control longitudinal color, and reduce the Petzval burden introduced by the strong negative front group.

The distortion is intentional rather than a defect in the ordinary photographic-lens sense. The lens is designed around a circular fisheye projection, so large negative distortion is the mechanism by which a 220° field is compressed into a finite circular image.

## Data-File Modeling Notes

The `.data.ts` file follows the project data specification rather than transcribing every physical plate as a renderable glass element. The patent's filter plate is therefore omitted from the surfaces array, and its axial optical path is folded into the preceding air space. The element count remains 12 glass lens elements in 9 groups, matching the production lens and excluding the filter.

Several patent-listed effective diameters are full clear diameters rather than renderer-safe semi-diameters. The front surface of L1 uses the patent half-diameter `106.5 mm`, but the rear surfaces of the first three menisci are reduced in the data file to satisfy spherical rim-slope and cross-gap clearance constraints while keeping the diagram physically legible. The rear-group semi-diameters are conservative estimates from the patent drawing, checked against edge thickness and gap-sag intrusion.

The stop position is inferred from Fig. 1 rather than numerically specified. Its semi-diameter is a renderer parameter chosen to represent an f/2.8 aperture in the layout, not an independently published patent dimension.

## Design Heritage and Context

This lens is part of Nikon's early F-mount fisheye sequence, but it differs from mirror-lock-up fisheyes by using a retrofocus layout that permits reflex viewing. The production manual describes the lens as a 6mm f/2.8 circular fisheye with a 220° picture angle, a 23mm diameter image circle on film, and a 9-group/12-element construction. Those specifications agree with the patent's Example I at the level expected for a production implementation derived from a patent example.

The optical design is therefore best understood as a specialty SLR fisheye that accepts very large front-element dimensions in exchange for a 220° field, f/2.8 aperture, and mirror clearance. The patent's technical novelty lies in the deep-dome, three-meniscus front group and its conditional curvature/effective-diameter relationships.
