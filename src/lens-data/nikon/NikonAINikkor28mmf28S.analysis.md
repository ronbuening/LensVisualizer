## Patent Reference and Design Identification

**Patent:** US 5,917,663
**Application Number:** 09/009,116
**Filed:** January 20, 1998 (continuation-in-part of US application 08/590,152, filed January 23, 1996)
**Priority:** February 10, 1995 (JP 7-046337)
**Granted:** June 29, 1999
**Inventor:** Kenzaburo Suzuki
**Assignee:** Nikon Corporation, Tokyo, Japan
**Title:** Wide-Angle Lens with an Image Stabilizing Function
**Embodiment analyzed:** Example 2 (Table 2, Fig. 3)

The patent describes retrofocus wide-angle lenses made from a front negative group GF and a rear positive group GL. A positive partial group GLP within GL is rotated about a point on the optical axis for vibration reduction. Three worked examples are disclosed: two approximately 28.6 mm examples and one approximately 20.4 mm example. The present transcription uses Example 2.

Example 2 is identified as the closest patent analogue of the Nikon AI Nikkor 28mm f/2.8S on convergent optical and historical evidence:

1. The patent gives f = 28.610 mm and FNO = 2.89, matching a nominal 28mm f/2.8 production lens within ordinary patent-to-production rounding.
2. The prescription is 8 elements in 8 air-spaced groups, matching Nikon's description of the AI Nikkor 28mm f/2.8S construction.
3. The layout is an all-spherical retrofocus formula with a convex front element, matching Nikon's design-history article for this lens.
4. Nikon states that the production lens uses close-range correction by varying the spacing between the fourth and fifth elements while the whole lens extends; that is consistent with the Table 2 L4-L5 interval, although the patent itself publishes only the infinity-focus numerical prescription.
5. The patent is much later than the 1981 production release of the AI Nikkor 28mm f/2.8S. The patent is therefore not treated as the original production-design patent. It is treated as a Nikon VR demonstration patent that reuses or closely models the established 28mm f/2.8S optical form.

The production AI Nikkor 28mm f/2.8S does not incorporate the patent's vibration-reduction mechanism.

## Optical Architecture

Example 2 is a retrofocus wide-angle design. The front group GF has negative net power, fF = -34.854 mm. The rear group GL has positive net power, fL = +28.134 mm. The back focal distance is 38.979 mm, which is longer than the 28.610 mm effective focal length; this is the defining retrofocus condition relevant to SLR mirror clearance.

The 38.979 mm BFD is measured from the final optical surface to the image plane. It is not the Nikon F register distance. If the image plane is placed at the F-mount film plane, the rear optical vertex would lie about 7.52 mm in front of that plane, since the Nikon F register is 46.5 mm.

GF consists of three air-spaced menisci. L1 is a weak positive meniscus, while L2 and L3 are negative menisci that supply most of the retrofocus divergence. GL consists of L4 through L8, with the aperture stop between L5 and L6. L6 is a strong biconcave dense-flint element behind the stop. L7 and L8 form GLP, the two-element positive subgroup used by the patent for rotational image stabilization.

The total track from the first vertex to the image plane is 89.279 mm. The total-track-to-focal-length ratio is 3.12. The full diagonal field corresponding to a 21.60 mm semi-field is 74.1 degrees.

The surface-by-surface Petzval sum is PA = +0.0053078 mm^-1, giving a Petzval radius of about 188.4 mm. GF contributes a negative Petzval component, while GL contributes a larger positive component; the result is a modest positive net field curvature for a 28 mm retrofocus lens.

## Element-by-Element Analysis

### L1 - Positive Meniscus, convex to object

nd = 1.67003, νd = 47.07. Glass: N-BAF10 catalog surrogate (Schott; J-BAF10 class). f = +180.4 mm.

L1 is a weak positive front meniscus. Both radii are positive (R1 = +60.500 mm, R2 = +118.300 mm), so the meniscus presents a convex first surface to the object side. It acts chiefly as a front corrector rather than as the main source of positive power.

Nikon's design-history article states that the convex front element suppresses barrel distortion to about one percent. That statement is consistent with the patent prose, which also treats the positive front meniscus as a useful distortion-correction feature in a retrofocus wide-angle design.

### L2 - Negative Meniscus, convex to object

nd = 1.51680, νd = 64.10. Glass: N-BK7 catalog surrogate (Schott; J-BK7A class). f = -52.4 mm.

L2 is the first strong negative lens in GF. Its rear radius, R4 = +15.400 mm, is much stronger than its front radius, so the element diverges the beam while retaining a convex object-side surface.

The patent's condition (3) uses this lens as f1, the negative meniscus nearest the object side in GF. Independent paraxial tracing gives f1 = -52.388 mm, matching the patent result.

### L3 - Negative Meniscus, convex to object

nd = 1.51680, νd = 64.10. Glass: N-BK7 catalog surrogate (Schott; J-BK7A class). f = -65.7 mm.

L3 continues the negative power of GF. It uses the same borosilicate crown as L2 and has a similar meniscus form. Together, L2 and L3 dominate the negative front-group power; L1 only partially offsets them.

The 14.400 mm air space after L3 is the main separation between GF and GL. That long air space is part of the retrofocus power distribution rather than a focus variable in the patent table.

### L4 - Plano-Convex Positive, convex to object

nd = 1.66755, νd = 41.96. Glass: J-BASF6 (HIKARI). f = +42.8 mm.

L4 is the first element of GL. It has a convex object-side surface and a plano rear surface, concentrating its positive power on the front surface.

In the production AI Nikkor 28mm f/2.8S, Nikon describes the close-range correction mechanism as changing the interval between the fourth and fifth elements while the whole lens extends. The patent does not supply the finite-conjugate L4-L5 spacing table, so the data file cannot reproduce the actual CRC cam law.

### L5 - Plano-Convex Positive, convex to image

nd = 1.62041, νd = 60.14. Glass: S-BSM16 catalog surrogate (Ohara; J-SK16 class). f = +54.8 mm.

L5 is plano-convex with its convex surface facing the image side. It sits immediately before the aperture-stop space. The L4-L5 pair forms the front positive part of GL and frames the production lens's CRC interval.

The higher Abbe number of L5 contrasts with L4's lower-dispersion-coordinate barium flint, giving the rear group an achromatizing balance before the strong post-stop negative L6.

### Aperture Stop

The aperture stop is in the air space between L5 and L6. With the Table 2 prescription, a paraxial entrance-pupil calculation gives a stop semi-diameter of about 6.7245 mm for FNO = 2.89. The data file uses this value directly.

### L6 - Biconcave Negative

nd = 1.75520, νd = 27.61. Glass: SF4 catalog surrogate (Schott; J-SF4 class). f = -19.5 mm.

L6 is the strongest individual element in the design. It is a dense-flint biconcave lens behind the stop, with R12 = -20.800 mm and R13 = +53.180 mm.

The patent states that the surface adjacent to the image side of the aperture stop should preferably have strong divergent characteristics. L6 satisfies that requirement: its front surface has negative refractive power because light enters a high-index glass across a negative radius.

### L7 - Positive Meniscus, convex to image (GLP front element)

nd = 1.67025, νd = 57.53. Glass: unmatched J-LAK02 class (HIKARI nearest). f = +41.9 mm.

L7 is the front element of GLP. Its two radii are negative (R14 = -46.001 mm, R15 = -17.900 mm), giving a positive meniscus with its convex side facing the image.

The shape factor required by condition (7), using the Table 2 radii, is (R2 + R1)/(R2 - R1) = -2.274. The patent's resulting-value table reports -2.433, which does not follow from the listed radii. The condition is satisfied under both values, but the computed value from the prescription is -2.274.

### L8 - Biconvex Positive (GLP rear element)

nd = 1.67025, νd = 57.53. Glass: unmatched J-LAK02 class (HIKARI nearest). f = +39.8 mm.

L8 is the rear element of GLP and the final glass element before the image plane. It is biconvex, with the stronger curvature on the image-side surface.

The patent's condition (6) requires the Abbe number of the biconvex lens nearest the image side in GL to exceed 40. L8 has νd = 57.53, so it satisfies the condition with margin.

## Focus Mechanism

Nikon describes the production AI Nikkor 28mm f/2.8S as using close-range correction: the spacing between the fourth and fifth elements changes while the whole lens extends. This is the correct production focusing description.

US 5,917,663 Table 2 provides only the infinity-focus prescription. It does not publish finite-conjugate CRC spacings. The data file therefore uses an explicit approximation: the final BFD changes from 38.9793 mm to 46.3152 mm, corresponding to an added extension of 7.3359 mm from the published 1:3.9 maximum reproduction ratio. The L4-L5 CRC interval is left fixed rather than guessed.

## Image Stabilization Context

The patent's purpose is a rotational image-stabilization method for retrofocus wide-angle lenses. In Example 2, GLP consists of L7 and L8. The patent gives AWL = 0.00611 radian, D = 12.7 mm from the image-side surface of GLP to the center of rotation, and an image movement of +0.128 mm.

This mechanism is patent context only for the AI Nikkor 28mm f/2.8S. The production manual-focus lens has no VR unit, and the data file does not model any image-stabilization displacement.

## Design Heritage and Context

Nikon states that the AI Nikkor 28mm f/2.8S was released in 1981 after two trial-production rounds and that Daijiro Fujie led the redesign. Nikon also states that it was developed partly to differentiate the Nikkor version from the Series E 28mm f/2.8, with the short 0.2 m minimum focus distance and 1:3.9 maximum reproduction ratio being central distinguishing specifications.

The patent is therefore best read as a later Nikon stabilization patent using a formula closely matching the production 28mm f/2.8S, not as the chronological origin of the 1981 manual-focus lens.
