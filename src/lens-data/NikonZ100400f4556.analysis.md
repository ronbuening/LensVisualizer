This patent discloses a zoom optical system with multiple focusing groups that move along different trajectories during focus, optimized for telephoto zoom lenses covering the super-telephoto range. Four numerical examples are provided. **Example 1** is the principal subject of this analysis and is a strong candidate for the production NIKKOR Z 100-400mm f/4.5-5.6 VR S, as demonstrated by the convergent evidence below.

---

## Production Identification: Evidence for Example 1 → NIKKOR Z 100-400mm f/4.5-5.6 VR S

The following convergent criteria identify Example 1 as the basis for the production design:

1. **Element and group count.** The patent prescription yields exactly **25 elements in 20 air-separated groups**, matching Nikon's published specification.

2. **Special dispersion elements.** The prescription contains exactly **6 elements with nd ≈ 1.498 (ED glass)** and **2 elements with nd ≈ 1.437 (Super ED glass)**, totaling 8 special dispersion elements — matching Nikon's official specification of "6 ED elements, 2 Super ED elements." (*Note: several third-party reviews erroneously reverse this count to "6 Super ED + 2 ED." The correct breakdown per Nikon's own product pages is 6 ED + 2 Super ED.*)

3. **All-spherical design.** No aspherical coefficient tables appear for Example 1. This matches the confirmed production lens, which contains **no aspherical elements** — unusual for a modern S-line NIKKOR and noted by multiple reviewers.

4. **Focal length and f-number.** The patent gives f_t = 388.17 mm (tele) and f_w = 103.09 mm (wide), with F_not = 5.76. The production lens is marketed as 100–400 mm f/4.5–5.6. The slight discrepancy (zoom ratio 3.77× vs. marketed 4.0×) is typical of patent-to-production optimization; Nikon's final production tuning would adjust the zoom cam to reach the marketed endpoints.

5. **Optical total length.** TL_t = 284.55 mm at the tele end. The production lens measures 222 mm in barrel length at 100 mm, extending to ~272 mm at 400 mm — consistent with the variable-gap analysis showing ~50 mm of total group movement during zooming.

6. **Internal floating focus with two adjacent negative groups.** The patent specifies G5 and G6 as the focus groups, both negative, adjacently positioned — matching Nikon's description of a "Multi-Focus Stepping Motor AF System" with two STM motors moving in sync.

7. **VR group.** The patent identifies the L17+L18 cemented doublet as the vibration reduction group (f_vr / f_t = 0.202), consistent with the production lens's in-barrel VR system.

8. **Filing chronology.** Filed December 2020; the NIKKOR Z 100-400mm was announced October 2021, with sales beginning early 2022.

## System Architecture

### Zoom Group Structure

The lens follows a **seven-group zoom architecture** — a positive-negative-positive-negative-negative-positive arrangement (when considering the rear section) that forms a double-telephoto configuration at the tele end:

| Zoom Group | Elements | Power | Focal Length (mm) | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| G1 (Front A) | L1, L2 | Positive | +220.2 | Primary light-gathering; front positive group |
| G2 (Front B) | L3–L5 | Positive | +412.7 | Second positive group; achromatic variator |
| G3 (Variator) | L6–L10 | Negative | −37.7 | Primary zoom variator |
| G4 (Relay) | L11–L19 + Stop | Positive | +46.0 | Image relay, stop, VR housing |
| G5 (Focus A) | L20+L21 | Negative | −81.7 | First focusing group |
| G6 (Focus B) | L22, L23 | Negative | −153.3 | Second focusing group |
| G7 (Field) | L24, L25 | Positive | +502.7 | Field flattener / teleconverter interface |

At the telephoto end, the system forms what the patent terms a **"double telephoto"** (ダブルテレフォト) configuration (¶0066). The first telephoto stage is the classical positive-front/negative-rear arrangement formed by G1+G2 (positive) and G3 (negative), which produces the primary focal length compression. The second telephoto stage is formed by G4 (positive) and the net-negative rear section G5+G6+G7, which further compresses the overall track. The combined telephoto ratio is TL_t / f_t = 0.733, achieving a 27% reduction in physical length relative to focal length.

### Zoom Mechanism

During zoom from wide (103 mm) to tele (388 mm), all six variable gaps change. Three of these — D4, D9, and D17 — vary only with zoom (their values are identical at infinity and close focus for any given zoom position). The remaining three — D35, D38, D42 — vary with both zoom and focus, and are discussed in the Focus Mechanism section below. The zoom-only gaps are:

| Gap | After | Wide ∞ (mm) | Tele ∞ (mm) | Change |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| D4 | L2 (G1→G2) | 1.500 | 51.500 | +50.000 |
| D9 | L5 (G2→G3) | 1.300 | 25.600 | +24.300 |
| D17 | L10 (G3→G4) | 43.095 | 2.100 | −40.995 |

The total sum of all six variable gaps increases from 90.4 mm (wide) to 140.4 mm (tele) at infinity focus — a net increase of exactly 50.0 mm, corresponding to the physical barrel extension observed during zooming (Nikon specifies a barrel length of 222 mm at 100 mm, extending to approximately 272 mm at 400 mm). Nikon's "Inner Balance Technology" maintains a near-constant center of gravity during this extension (within 3.8 mm per Nikon's spec) by distributing glass mass across the moving groups so that forward-moving and rearward-moving elements approximately balance. G1 moves forward (away from the image), G3 moves forward toward G2, and the G3–G4 gap collapses as G4 remains relatively stationary. This produces the characteristic single-barrel telescoping extension of the production lens.

### Focus Mechanism

Focus is accomplished by moving G5 and G6 along different axial trajectories — a **floating focus** system with two negative groups:

| Gap | After | Tele ∞ (mm) | Tele CF (mm) | Δ (mm) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| D35 | L19 (G4→G5) | 3.467 | 34.084 | +30.617 |
| D38 | L21 (G5→G6) | 8.800 | 9.182 | +0.382 |
| D42 | L23 (G6→G7) | 48.913 | 17.915 | −30.998 |

Both G5 and G6 move toward the image during close focus (+30.6 and +0.4 mm respectively at tele), while the back focal distance (D42) decreases by 31 mm. The total variable gap sum remains constant (140.38 mm at tele regardless of focus), confirming **true internal focusing** — the overall length does not change during AF.

The use of two separate focusing groups moving along different trajectories suppresses focus-induced aberration variation (particularly field curvature and spherical aberration), which is the central innovation claimed in the patent's conditional expressions.

The production lens achieves minimum focus distances of 0.75 m at 100 mm and 0.98 m at 400 mm (per Nikon's specification). The patent does not directly state the close-focus object distance, but the large focus travel of G5 (+30.6 mm at tele) provides the mechanical range needed for these close-focus distances at a long focal length.

## Aspherical Surfaces

**Example 1 contains no aspherical surfaces.** This is confirmed both by the absence of any aspherical coefficient table in the patent text and by Nikon's published specifications, which list no aspherical elements for the production lens.

This is a notable design choice for a modern S-line NIKKOR zoom covering a 4× telephoto range. The designer achieves aberration control entirely through the use of 25 spherical elements with 8 special dispersion glasses — a "brute force" approach that relies on element count and glass diversity rather than aspheric surface complexity. The trade-off is higher element count (and thus weight, cost, and potential ghost/flare sources), offset by the manufacturing simplicity and consistency of all-spherical elements at every zoom and focus position.

## Glass Identification

The following table identifies each element's glass type based on its refractive index (nd) at the d-line (587.6 nm). Glass identifications are matched against the OHARA and SCHOTT catalogs. Where the nd value matches a catalog glass to within ±0.00005, the match is listed as exact. Nikon's proprietary glass formulations (particularly Super ED) may not correspond precisely to any catalog designation.

**Methodological limitation:** This patent provides only nd (refractive index) for each element — the Abbe number (νd) is not listed. For several elements, multiple catalog glasses share the same nd but have significantly different Abbe numbers, meaning the glass cannot be uniquely identified from nd alone. These ambiguous cases are marked with † in the tables below. The preferred candidate for each is selected based on the element's optical role, but this is an inferential assignment, not a patent-stated fact.

### Super ED Elements (2 total)

| Element | nd | Catalog Match | Type | Group | Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **L2** | 1.437001 | Near S-FPL55 (OHARA) | Super ED fluorophosphate | G1 | Front positive power, primary LoCA correction |
| **L5** | 1.437001 | Near S-FPL55 (OHARA) | Super ED fluorophosphate | G2 | Positive element in achromatic variator |

The nd = 1.437001 falls between crystalline fluorite (CaF₂, nd = 1.4339) and OHARA S-FPL55 (nd = 1.4388). This is consistent with Nikon's proprietary "Super ED" glass, which is described as having "even lower dispersion properties" than standard ED glass and "extremely high performance in reducing the secondary spectrum." The anomalous partial dispersion of this glass class drives the correction of secondary spectrum (residual chromatic aberration after primary achromatization).

Both Super ED elements are placed in the front group where the marginal ray height is greatest. This is the optically optimal position for correcting longitudinal chromatic aberration (LoCA), since chromatic power contribution scales with the square of ray height.

The ED elements are distributed throughout the system: two in the front groups (L1, L3), one in the variator (L7), two in the relay (L12, L13), and one in the rear field group (L24). This distribution ensures chromatic correction is maintained across all zoom positions, not just at a single focal length.

### Filter Element

The patent prescription includes a parallel-sided flat glass plate (surfaces 47–48) between the rear lens group and the image plane, listed as "FL" (filter) in the patent figures. It has nd = 1.51680 and a thickness of 1.60 mm, consistent with N-BK7 (SCHOTT) or S-BSL7 (OHARA) — the standard material for sensor cover glass and IR-cut filter assemblies. ## Element-by-Element Optical Analysis

### Group 1 — Front Positive (f = +220.2 mm)

**L1** (biconvex, nd = 1.488, ED): f ≈ +477 mm. The front element is a weakly curved, large-diameter biconvex lens made from FK/ED glass. Its low curvature minimizes surface contributions to spherical aberration at the point of maximum marginal ray height. The ED glass provides primary color correction at the very front of the system. The symmetric biconvex form (R₁ = +465.0, R₂ = −465.0) indicates the element is shaped to minimize coma contribution.

**L2** (plano-convex, nd = 1.437, Super ED): f ≈ +408 mm. The second element uses Super ED glass in a plano-convex form (rear surface flat). Placing a Super ED element this far forward — where the marginal ray height is near maximum — provides the most leverage for correcting secondary spectrum. The flat rear surface simplifies manufacturing and alignment.

Together, G1 contributes +220 mm of positive power while establishing the primary achromatic and apochromatic-equivalent correction baseline for the entire system.

### Group 2 — Second Positive (f = +412.7 mm)

**L3+L4** (cemented doublet, f ≈ −167 mm): L3 (biconvex, nd = 1.498, ED) cemented to L4 (biconcave, nd = 1.804, lanthanum crown). Despite L3 being positive alone, the combination is an achromatic **negative** doublet — unusual in a positive group. Its purpose is to provide further chromatic correction while contributing negative power that balances the strong positive elements.

**L5** (biconvex, nd = 1.437, Super ED): f ≈ +115 mm. The second Super ED element provides strong positive power with excellent chromatic properties. This is the most powerful positive element in G2 and is the dominant contributor to the group's net positive power.

The arrangement of G2 — a negative cemented achromat followed by a strong positive Super ED singlet — creates a "dialyte" configuration within the group, providing powerful achromatization across the wide-to-tele zoom range.

### Group 3 — Zoom Variator (f = −37.7 mm)

This is the strongest group in the system by absolute focal length and is the principal zoom variator. It comprises five elements in three air-separated subgroups:

**L6+L7** (cemented doublet, f ≈ −626 mm): L6 (biconvex positive, lanthanum crown, nd = 1.720) cemented to L7 (biconcave negative, ED glass, nd = 1.498). A weakly negative achromatic doublet that uses ED glass in the negative element — an inverted arrangement (low-dispersion glass for the negative element) that provides enhanced secondary spectrum correction. The weak power means this doublet primarily handles chromatic correction rather than contributing to G3's net negative power.

**L8+L9** (cemented doublet, f ≈ −119 mm): L8 (biconcave negative, nd = 1.741) cemented to L9 (positive meniscus, dense lanthanum flint, nd = 1.855). A moderately negative achromatic doublet that carries the majority of G3's diverging power. The high-index L9 element helps control Petzval curvature.

**L10** (biconcave negative, nd = 1.755): f ≈ −63 mm. A strong standalone negative element. Its glass type is ambiguous — nd = 1.755 matches both S-LAM60 (νd ≈ 52, lanthanum crown) and S-TIM27 (νd ≈ 28, titanium flint). Given its position in the variator where chromatic control is critical, the higher-Abbe candidate S-LAM60 is more likely. This is the most powerful individual element in G3 and acts as the primary diverging lens in the variator. Its position at the rear of G3 maximizes the beam divergence delivered to G4.

G3's function is to vary the magnification of the image formed by G1+G2. As G3 moves forward during zooming to tele, the divergence it introduces increases the effective focal length of the front group. The total −37.7 mm focal length is very short relative to the system, indicating an aggressive variator design that achieves the 3.77× zoom ratio with moderate physical travel.

### Group 4 — Relay and VR Housing (f = +46.0 mm)

G4 is the most complex group with 9 elements, the aperture stop, and the VR mechanism. It re-images the divergent beam from G3 onto the rear focus/field groups.

**L11** (biconvex positive, nd = 1.593): f ≈ +129 mm. A moderate-power dense crown glass element that begins the convergence process after the strongly divergent G3 output. The glass identity is uncertain — nd = 1.59319 does not match S-SK14 or other common OHARA/SCHOTT catalog entries within tolerance. It has a perfectly symmetric biconvex form (R₁ = +152.668, R₂ = −152.668), which minimizes coma contribution at its position in the beam.

**L12** (plano-convex, nd = 1.498, ED): f ≈ +130 mm. ED glass plano-convex with flat rear surface. The low-dispersion glass continues the achromatization started in the front groups.

**L13** (plano-convex, nd = 1.498, ED): f ≈ +97 mm. A second plano-convex ED element, slightly stronger than L12. Together, L12 and L13 provide positive power while controlling chromatic aberration through the use of ED glass at the point where the marginal ray passes through the relay region. Their flat rear surfaces suggest they also serve as alignment-friendly relay elements.

**L14** (biconcave negative, nd = 1.806): f ≈ −110 mm. A dense lanthanum crown negative element that controls spherical aberration immediately ahead of the stop. Its position allows correction of the pupil's zonal spherical aberration.

**Aperture Stop (S)**: Positioned between L14 and L15. The stop is located within G4, which remains stationary during both zoom and focus — ensuring consistent vignetting behavior and f-number tracking across the entire operating range.

**L15** (positive meniscus convex to image, nd = 1.808): f ≈ +144 mm. A positive meniscus immediately behind the stop. Its concave-toward-object form works with the stop to control oblique spherical aberration and coma.

**L16** (negative meniscus convex to object, nd = 2.001): f ≈ −47 mm. The ultra-high-index element (S-NPH2 equivalent). Its position just behind the stop where both marginal and chief ray heights are moderate makes it effective for correcting higher-order spherical aberration and Petzval curvature. The nd = 2.001 means that even moderate surface curvatures produce strong refractive power — L16's front radius is +134.6 mm and rear is +34.6 mm, yielding −47 mm of focal length from a meniscus form with relatively gentle radii.

**L17+L18** (cemented positive doublet = **VR group**, f ≈ +79 mm): L17 (biconvex, barium crown, nd = 1.553) cemented to L18 (negative meniscus convex to image, ultra-dense lanthanum, nd = 1.954). This is the **vibration reduction element** — the patent explicitly states it moves perpendicular to the optical axis for image stabilization. The net positive focal length of +79 mm (f_vr/f_t = 0.202, satisfying patent condition 9: 0.10 < f_vr/f_t < 0.30) gives the VR group approximately the right sensitivity for the claimed 5.5-stop stabilization. The cemented doublet form ensures chromatic performance is maintained during decentered VR operation.

**L19** (positive meniscus convex to object, nd = 1.603): f ≈ +78 mm. A titanium flint positive element (S-TIM28, νd ≈ 38.0). *Note: the original analysis incorrectly identified this glass as S-PHM52 (phosphate crown, nd = 1.618); that glass has a different refractive index. The correct catalog match for nd = 1.60342 is S-TIM28 (OHARA), a titanium flint with moderate dispersion.* The use of a flint glass in a positive element at this position likely serves to balance chromatic aberrations with the adjacent ED elements in the relay section, providing specific partial dispersion characteristics needed for the floating focus mechanism downstream.

### Group 5 — Focus Group A (f = −81.7 mm)

**L20+L21** (cemented negative doublet): L20 (biconvex positive, nd = 1.850) cemented to L21 (biconcave negative, lanthanum flint, nd = 1.729). Despite L20 being individually positive (f ≈ +71 mm), the combination is strongly negative (f ≈ −82 mm), dominated by L21's biconcave form. L20's glass type is ambiguous — nd = 1.850 matches both S-LAH58 (νd ≈ 32, dense lanthanum flint) and S-LAH79 (νd ≈ 44, dense lanthanum crown). The choice significantly affects the doublet's chromatic properties but cannot be resolved from nd alone.

G5 is the **first and primary focusing group**. At the tele end, it moves +30.6 mm toward the image when focusing from infinity to close. The high-index L20 element controls Petzval curvature during focus shifts, while the cemented construction ensures chromatic stability. The patent text states G5 and G6 are "adjacent" — they share no intervening fixed group, with only the variable gap D38 between them.

### Group 6 — Focus Group B (f = −153.3 mm)

**L22** (biconvex positive, nd = 1.654): f ≈ +70 mm. A moderate-index positive element whose glass type is ambiguous — nd = 1.654 matches S-BSM71 (νd ≈ 40, barium borosilicate) and S-BAM12 (νd ≈ 34, barium flint). Both candidates have relatively low Abbe numbers for a positive element, suggesting this element serves a chromatic balancing role within the focus group rather than pure power contribution.

**L23** (biconcave negative, dense lanthanum crown, nd = 1.903): f ≈ −47 mm. A very strong negative element from ultra-high-index glass.

G6 is the **second focusing group**, providing fine-tuning of the focus correction. Its movement during focusing is much smaller than G5's (Δ = +0.4 mm at tele vs. G5's +30.6 mm), confirming its role as a correction group rather than a primary mover. The differential trajectory between G5 and G6 is the key to suppressing focus breathing and focus-induced aberration variation — the central innovation of this patent.

The patent's conditional expression (1) quantifies this: Σ|γᵢ| × (5.76 / F_not) = 6.419, well within the specified range of 4.00–10.00. The image-plane displacement coefficients γ₅ and γ₆ have a ratio |γ|_max / |γ|_min = 4.305 (condition 2), indicating the two groups have substantially different sensitivities — G5 does the heavy lifting while G6 fine-tunes.

### Group 7 — Field Flattener (f = +502.7 mm)

**L24** (biconcave negative, nd = 1.498, ED): f ≈ −75 mm. An ED glass negative element — the only ED element in the rear section. Its low-dispersion glass corrects lateral chromatic aberration at the image plane, where the chief ray height is maximal.

**L25** (positive meniscus convex to object, nd = 1.738): f ≈ +67 mm. A positive element whose glass type is ambiguous — nd = 1.738 matches both S-LAH63 (νd ≈ 50, lanthanum crown) and S-NBH5 (νd ≈ 32, niobium flint). Given its pairing with the ED-glass L24 for lateral color correction, the higher-Abbe candidate (S-LAH63) is the more likely assignment, as it would form a more effective achromatic corrector with L24's anomalous-dispersion glass. Combined with L24, the group's weak net positive power (+503 mm) means it contributes negligibly to the system's overall power — its role is purely corrective: flattening the Petzval surface and cleaning up residual lateral color and astigmatism before the image reaches the sensor.

G7 is stationary during both zoom and focus. Its position immediately ahead of the image plane makes it the natural location for a field flattener. This is also the last group before the Z-mount flange, and its position and power would be designed with teleconverter compatibility in mind — the NIKKOR Z TC-1.4x and TC-2.0x are both compatible with this lens.

## Chromatic Correction Strategy

The distribution of special dispersion elements reveals a deliberate multi-zone correction strategy:

**Zone 1 — Front groups (G1+G2):** Contains both Super ED elements (L2, L5) and two ED elements (L1, L3). This zone handles primary and secondary spectrum correction at the point of maximum marginal ray height, where longitudinal chromatic aberration (LoCA) contributions are strongest. The Super ED elements, with their anomalous partial dispersion, are critical for reducing secondary spectrum — the residual color error that persists even after primary achromatization.

**Zone 2 — Variator (G3):** Contains one ED element (L7) in an inverted cemented doublet (ED negative with lanthanum positive). This ensures the zoom variator does not introduce uncorrected chromatic variation as it moves during zooming.

**Zone 3 — Relay (G4):** Contains two ED elements (L12, L13) positioned ahead of the aperture stop. Their presence ensures the relay section maintains chromatic correction for the axial beam at all zoom positions.

**Zone 4 — Rear (G7):** Contains one ED element (L24) positioned near the image plane. This corrects lateral chromatic aberration, which is proportional to chief ray height and thus most effectively corrected near the image.

This four-zone approach ensures that chromatic correction is preserved across the entire zoom range. If all ED/Super ED elements were concentrated in one zone, zoom-induced changes in ray heights through the other zones would cause chromatic performance to vary significantly with focal length.

**1. L19 glass identification — corrected from S-PHM52 to S-TIM28.**
The original analysis identified L19 (nd = 1.60342) as S-PHM52 (OHARA, phosphate crown). This was incorrect: S-PHM52 has nd = 1.6180, not 1.6034. The use of a flint-type glass in a positive element at this position suggests a deliberate chromatic balancing role rather than the achromatic continuation role described in the original analysis.

The original analysis listed one S-BSM71 candidate for L22 with νd ≈ 54, which is incorrect. OHARA S-BSM71 has νd ≈ 39.7. Both ambiguous candidates (S-BSM71 at νd ≈ 40 and S-BAM12 at νd ≈ 34) are relatively low-Abbe for a positive element, indicating L22 serves a chromatic balancing function within the focus group rather than an achromatic one.

### Data File Construction Notes

The companion `NikonZ100400f4556.data.ts` file was constructed with the following considerations:

**Semi-diameters.** The patent does not provide semi-diameters. All SDs in the data file were estimated from paraxial marginal ray traces at both the wide and tele zoom positions, taking the maximum height at each surface and adding approximately 10% mechanical clearance. For the rear groups (G5–G7), the marginal ray heights are small at both zoom positions (3–10 mm), but a minimum SD of ~14 mm was applied to maintain reasonable rendering proportions. The actual production lens has a 77 mm filter thread, implying front-element SDs of approximately 37–38 mm, consistent with the computed values.

**Near-contact air gaps.** This design contains several very tight air gaps (0.15 mm between L1/L2, 0.15 mm between L11/L12, 0.45 mm between L12/L13, 0.60 mm between L15/L16, 0.66 mm between L18/L19) where adjacent convex surfaces physically nest into one another. The cross-gap sag analysis shows that at the computed SDs, several of these gaps have negative effective clearance at the rim — the surfaces overlap geometrically due to curvature nesting. This is physically correct (the convex surface of the following element fits into the concave space vacated by the preceding element's rear surface), but may cause rendering artifacts in the SVG diagram if the renderer does not handle this nesting geometry.

**Variable gap format.** The data file uses zoom-format variable gaps with two zoom positions [wide, tele]. The patent provides a 4-column table (tele ∞, wide ∞, tele CF, wide CF). Zoom-only gaps (D4, D9, D17) have identical infinity/close-focus values at each zoom position. Focus+zoom gaps (D35, D38, D42) have different infinity/close-focus values. The surface `d` fields are set to the wide-end infinity values (first zoom position, infinity focus), per the template convention that `d` must match `var[label][0][0]`.

**Abbe numbers.** Since the patent provides only nd (no νd), all Abbe numbers in the elements array are inferred from catalog matching. For unambiguous matches (single catalog glass at that nd), the catalog νd is used directly. For ambiguous matches (marked with †), the preferred candidate's νd is used. For the Nikon Super ED glass (nd = 1.437001), the νd is approximate (~95) based on published properties of similar fluorophosphate formulations — the actual value is proprietary.
