# smc Pentax 67 Macro 100mm f/4 — Optical Analysis

## Patent Reference and Design Identification

**Patent:** US 6,002,533  
**Filed:** Jul. 24, 1998 (filing date corrected by Certificate of Correction)  
**Granted:** Dec. 14, 1999  
**Priority:** JP 9-219893. The cover page gives Mar. 17, 1997; the specification text identifies Japanese Application No. 9-219893 as filed Jul. 31, 1997. Both dates are recorded because the patent text is internally inconsistent.  
**Inventor:** Shuji Yoneyama  
**Assignee:** Asahi Kogaku Kogyo Kabushiki Kaisha, Tokyo, Japan  
**Title:** Close-Up Attachment Lens  
**Embodiment analyzed:** Embodiment 1, Table 1 and Figs. 1-4; main lens LM, surfaces 7-16

The patent is formally directed to a three-element close-up attachment lens, LA, mounted on the object side of a photographic main lens. Table 1 nevertheless gives a full prescription for the main lens LM as well as for the attachment. The data file accompanying this analysis transcribes the main lens only, retaining the patent's surface labels 7-16 for traceability.

The main lens prescription is identifiable as the smc Pentax 67 Macro 100mm f/4 by convergent evidence. The computed Gaussian focal length is 99.9687 mm, agreeing with the 100 mm marked focal length. The transcribed main lens has 6 elements in 4 air-spaced groups, matching published catalog specifications for the production lens. The patent's image-height basis is Y = 44.1 mm, matching the half diagonal of the nominal 70 x 56 mm Pentax 6x7 frame. Published product listings give a 48° angle of view, 0.443 m minimum shooting distance, 0.52x maximum magnification for the master lens, 77 mm filter thread, 8-blade diaphragm, and 600 g weight; these figures are consistent with the patent-derived field and construction. The lens was supplied with a matched life-size adapter/converter, consistent with the patent's close-up-attachment subject matter.

All three patent embodiments share the same main-lens prescription. Embodiments 2 and 3 vary only the attachment lens; Embodiment 3 additionally states that the short-distance value of d11 is 15.60 mm rather than 15.58 mm. The main-lens prescription itself is therefore not ambiguous.

## Optical Architecture

The smc Pentax 67 Macro 100mm f/4 is an all-spherical, modified Gauss-type macro lens. In air-spaced construction it is a 6-element / 4-group design: a positive meniscus singleton, a cemented doublet of net negative power, a second positive meniscus singleton behind the stop, and a rear cemented doublet of weak net positive power. The patent also describes the main lens functionally as two lens groups: a front group containing LM1-LM3 and a rear group containing LM4-LM6.

The air-spaced group power sequence is positive - negative / positive - positive. The front half consists of LM1 followed by the LM2+LM3 cemented doublet, separated by only 0.20 mm of air. The rear half consists of LM4 followed by the LM5+LM6 cemented doublet, separated by 3.45 mm of air. The diaphragm lies in the large inter-half space between surfaces 11 and 12 and is specified as 4.00 mm in front of surface 12.

Independent paraxial tracing gives a main-lens EFL of 99.9687 mm and a BFD of 77.4258 mm from surface 16 at the infinity-focus Table 1 spacing. With Y = 44.1 mm, the rectilinear full field is 47.61°, close to the 48° published angle of view. The surface-by-surface Petzval sum is +0.0012606 mm^-1, corresponding to a Petzval radius of about 793.3 mm. That low positive Petzval sum is a central design result for a medium-format macro lens, where flat-field performance is more important than compactness.

The design is quasi-symmetric in silhouette rather than strictly symmetric in power. The front doublet is negative, with f = -212.3 mm, while the rear doublet is weakly positive, with f = +467.3 mm. The rear half has the stronger net positive power, f = +117.5 mm, compared with f = +195.5 mm for the front half. This distribution is consistent with a macro design that must preserve correction over a close-focus range without resorting to aspherical surfaces.

## Element-by-Element Analysis

### LM1 — Positive Meniscus, convex to object

nd = 1.72000, νd = 43.7. Glass: S-LAM52 (OHARA, legacy/NR), code 720437. f = +120.9 mm.

LM1 is the front positive collector. Both surfaces have positive radii, r7 = +46.500 mm and r8 = +95.900 mm, so the element is a positive meniscus with the steeper convex surface facing the object. This shape supplies positive power without the higher spherical-aberration burden of a biconvex element of comparable power.

With νd = 43.7, S-LAM52 sits in the flint-region lanthanum family by normal crown/flint convention, even though it is used here as a positive element. The high refractive index reduces the Petzval burden of the positive front element.

### LM2+LM3 — Front Cemented Doublet, net negative

The front doublet lies immediately before the diaphragm and has net focal length f = -212.3 mm. It reduces the front half to weak net positive power while providing the primary front-side chromatic and Petzval balancing. The front doublet uses a high-νd positive member followed by a lower-νd negative member, a conventional achromatizing arrangement.

#### LM2 — Positive Meniscus, convex to object

nd = 1.67790, νd = 55.3. Glass: S-LAL12 (OHARA). f = +70.3 mm.

LM2 is the positive member of the front cemented doublet. It has relatively strong curvature at r9 = +25.064 mm and is the most crown-like glass in the main lens. Its higher Abbe number makes it the natural positive achromat partner for the lower-νd LM3.

The element contributes substantial positive power near the stop. Its Petzval contribution must therefore be counterbalanced immediately by LM3, especially at the steep r11 exit surface.

#### LM3 — Negative Meniscus, convex to object

nd = 1.62004, νd = 36.3. Glass: S-TIM2 (OHARA). f = -46.6 mm.

LM3 is the negative flint member of the front cemented doublet. Its strongest optical surface is r11 = +17.750 mm, which contributes a large negative Petzval term. This surface is one of the principal field-flattening features of the design.

The LM2/LM3 dispersion difference, Δνd about 19, supplies ordinary C-F achromatization without any ED or anomalous-partial-dispersion glass. The patent does not publish partial-dispersion data, so secondary-spectrum claims should remain limited to conventional inference from the glass pairing.

### Diaphragm

The diaphragm lies between surfaces 11 and 12. Patent Table 1 gives d11 as a variable gap, 13.30-15.58 mm, and the text states that the distance from the diaphragm to surface 12 is 4.00 mm. The data file therefore splits d11 into r11-to-stop = 9.30-11.58 mm and stop-to-r12 = 4.00 mm.

For the infinity-focus Table 1 prescription, an f/4 entrance-pupil semi-diameter is 12.4961 mm. Paraxial transfer from the entrance pupil to the stop gives a physical stop semi-diameter of 9.4467 mm, which is the value used in the data file.

### LM4 — Positive Meniscus, concave to object

nd = 1.74400, νd = 44.8. Glass: S-LAM2 (OHARA), code 744448. f = +118.7 mm.

LM4 is the first rear-group element, placed immediately after the stop. Both radii are negative, r12 = -83.484 mm and r13 = -43.469 mm, giving a positive meniscus that is concave toward the object and convex toward the image.

This element mirrors LM1 in broad form but uses a slightly higher-index OHARA S-LAM2 glass. Its two Petzval terms partially cancel, leaving a moderate net positive contribution. Because LM4 is close to the stop, it participates in balancing field and off-axis aberrations rather than merely adding axial power.

### LM5+LM6 — Rear Cemented Doublet, weak positive

The rear cemented doublet has net focal length f = +467.3 mm. It is not a strong power group; its principal role is chromatic and field balancing near the image side. Its glass pairing is lower-νd negative flint followed by higher-νd positive lanthanum glass.

#### LM5 — Negative Meniscus, concave to object

nd = 1.74077, νd = 27.8. Glass: S-TIH13 (OHARA), code 741278. f = -47.8 mm.

LM5 is the most dispersive glass element in the main lens. The catalog identification is OHARA S-TIH13. HOYA's close cross-reference for this 741/278 glass code is E-FD13.

The steep front surface r14 = -28.598 mm contributes a large negative Petzval term, offsetting the positive field curvature introduced by the outer positive elements. Its low Abbe number also makes it the rear group's main chromatic corrector against LM6.

#### LM6 — Positive Meniscus, concave to object

nd = 1.72000, νd = 43.7. Glass: S-LAM52 (OHARA, legacy/NR), code 720437. f = +55.5 mm.

LM6 is the final positive power element and uses the same S-LAM52 glass as LM1. Its steep rear surface r16 = -33.124 mm contributes a substantial positive Petzval term that is balanced by LM5 and by the negative contribution of the front doublet.

The cemented interface r15 = -168.970 mm is weakly curved. Its refractive-index step is small, but the dispersion contrast between S-TIH13 and S-LAM52 is large enough to make it a useful chromatic interface with little additional field curvature.

## Glass Identification and Selection

The patent gives only nd and νd values, not vendor glass names. The table below uses OHARA catalog matches because every main-lens glass has an exact or near-exact OHARA designation, including S-LAM52, which is now non-recommended/legacy. HOYA equivalents are shown only where the present HOYA cross-reference is reasonably direct.

| Element | nd | νd | Catalog identification | Cross-reference note | Optical role |
|---|---:|---:|---|---|---|
| LM1 | 1.72000 | 43.7 | S-LAM52 (OHARA, legacy/NR) | 720437 glass code | Front positive collector |
| LM2 | 1.67790 | 55.3 | S-LAL12 (OHARA) | HOYA LAC12 class | Positive member of front achromat |
| LM3 | 1.62004 | 36.3 | S-TIM2 (OHARA) | HOYA E-F2 class | Negative member of front achromat |
| LM4 | 1.74400 | 44.8 | S-LAM2 (OHARA) | HOYA LAF2 class | Rear positive meniscus |
| LM5 | 1.74077 | 27.8 | S-TIH13 (OHARA) | HOYA E-FD13 class | Dense flint rear corrector |
| LM6 | 1.72000 | 43.7 | S-LAM52 (OHARA, legacy/NR) | 720437 glass code | Final positive power element |

This palette is not simply “lanthanum crown plus flint.” By Abbe number, only LM2 is crown-like in the ordinary νd > 50 sense. LM1, LM4, and LM6 are high-index lanthanum glasses in the flint-region range, and LM3 and LM5 are explicit flint/titanium-flint classes. The system uses high index to keep positive-element Petzval terms manageable while using the two cemented doublets for chromatic correction.

No ED, fluorite, fluorophosphate, or anomalous partial-dispersion glass is indicated by the patent prescription. The chromatic correction should therefore be described as conventional achromatization by dispersion pairing, not apochromatic correction.

## Focus Mechanism

Patent Table 1 gives only one variable main-lens spacing: d11, the separation between surface 11 and surface 12. The patent text also states that the diaphragm is between those surfaces and is 4.00 mm in front of surface 12. The transcribed data file therefore keeps STO-to-r12 fixed at 4.00 mm and places all d11 variation in the r11-to-STO portion of the gap.

| State | Patent d11 (mm) | r11-to-STO (mm) | STO-to-r12 (mm) | EFL (mm) | BFD from r16 (mm) |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Infinity-focus patent state | 13.30 | 9.30 | 4.00 | 99.9687 | 77.4258 |
| Short-distance patent state with attachment | 15.58 | 11.58 | 4.00 | 100.9706 | 76.8238 |
| Change | +2.28 | +2.28 | 0.00 | +1.0019 | -0.6020 |

The patent's variable data are for the main lens as used with the close-up attachment; they are not a complete standalone focusing cam law for the production master lens from infinity to 0.443 m. The production lens is published as focusing to 0.443 m and 0.52x without the life-size adapter, but the patent does not provide enough standalone spacing data to reconstruct that full movement. The data file therefore models only the documented patent movement and records the published close-focus distance as catalog metadata.

The focus type should not be overstated. The patent's simplified numerical table shows a single changing internal separation. Published descriptions may call the production lens a floating-focus macro design, but the patent table alone supports only a two-group separation change for the main lens in the attachment configuration.

## Aspherical Surfaces

The main lens contains no aspherical surfaces. Patent Table 1 lists only spherical radii for surfaces 7-16 and gives no conic constants or polynomial aspheric coefficients. The attachment lens in Embodiment 1 is also all-spherical.

## Conditional Expressions

The patent's five conditional expressions apply to the close-up attachment lens LA, not to the main lens LM. They are included here because they explain why the patent's attachment is compact while remaining corrected over the -0.5 to -1.0 magnification range.

| Condition | Expression | Patent range | Embodiment 1 value | Result |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| (1) | d2 / fA | 0.005-0.040 | 0.016 | Satisfied |
| (2) | (d2 + d4) / fA | 0.010-0.060 | 0.033 | Satisfied |
| (3) | (r1 + r2) / (r2 - r1) | 0.400-1.000 | 0.732 | Satisfied |
| (4) | (r3 + r4) / (r4 - r3) | 0.300-0.700 | 0.357 | Satisfied |
| (5) | n1 | > 1.70 | 1.7725 | Satisfied |

Condition (5) is relevant by analogy to the main lens because the patent text explicitly connects high refractive index to Petzval-sum suppression in the attachment. The main lens applies the same broad design principle by using high-index positive elements, but the formal inequalities do not constrain the main lens surfaces.

## Aberration Correction Strategy

The aberration diagrams in Figs. 2A-2E and 4A-4E show the combined attachment-plus-main-lens system, not the bare main lens alone. They therefore should not be used as standalone performance curves for the master lens. They do, however, show the behavior of the matched system at FNo. 1:4.0 and at the 44.1 mm image height.

The diagram scale indicates controlled spherical aberration and sine-condition error at full aperture. The d-, g-, and C-line curves remain conventionally grouped rather than apochromatically superposed, which is consistent with the ordinary glass palette. The lateral chromatic-aberration plots are small on the ±0.05 mm scale, and the distortion plots remain within the ±1% frame shown in the patent. The astigmatism plots show nonzero sagittal/meridional separation toward the field edge, but the low computed Petzval sum keeps the mean field relatively flat for a large 6x7 macro field.

## Design Heritage and Context

The production smc Pentax 67 Macro 100mm f/4 belongs to the late Pentax 6x7 / 67 system period and is mechanically associated with the matched smc Life-Size Converter 67. The patent should therefore be read as a matched-system disclosure: the attachment is the claimed invention, but the main lens prescription is sufficiently complete to identify and model the 100 mm f/4 master lens.

The design is conservative by late-1990s 35 mm macro standards: it has no aspherical surfaces, no ED elements, and only six glass elements. That conservatism is less limiting in a 6x7 f/4 macro lens than it would be in a faster 35 mm-format macro lens. The patent-derived prescription shows the priorities clearly: moderate individual curvatures, high-index positive elements, two cemented chromatic correctors, and very low Petzval sum across a large field.
